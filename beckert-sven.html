Sven Beckert

1965 – ? · German-American
#history#political-economy#capitalism#slavery#globalization

German-American historian, Laird Bell Professor of American History at Harvard, and one of the central figures in the so-called New History of Capitalism movement that reshaped American historiography in the 2010s and 2020s. Born in West Germany in 1965, trained at Hamburg and Columbia, Beckert has spent his career re-narrating capitalism as a globally constituted, violent, and historically contingent system rather than as the natural unfolding of free exchange.

His first major book, The Monied Metropolis (2001), is a social history of how the New York City elite consolidated itself after the Civil War — already an argument about how class power is built rather than given. The breakthrough was Empire of Cotton: A Global History (2014), which traced the cotton economy from roughly 1500 to the present and argued that modern capitalism emerged not from peaceful market exchange but from violence — what Beckert calls war capitalism: imperial conquest, plantation slavery, expropriation of indigenous land, and state-backed coercion in global commodity markets. The Manchester factory of the nineteenth century is not the origin of capitalism but its successor regime, built on the capital, raw materials, labor systems, and world markets that war capitalism had already established. Empire of Cotton won the Bancroft Prize, was a Pulitzer finalist, and is now standard reading.

His most recent and most ambitious book, Capitalism: A Global History (2025), extends the methodology from a single commodity to the whole tapestry. Over a thousand pages, it argues that capitalism was born global — emerging from trading networks across Asia, Africa, and Europe long before the European industrial takeoff — and that its radical recasting of economic life took root only gradually and unevenly across regions. The book is a deliberate refutation of the "European miracle" story and an extension of the war-capitalism / industrial-capitalism framework worldwide. It was named a New York Times Notable Book and a Financial Times Best Book of the Year on publication.

Methodologically, Beckert is the historians' counterpart to Piketty in inequality studies and Acemoglu in institutions: continental and centuries-long scales, archival rigor, and a willingness to ask questions narrower empirical disciplines had set aside. The deep intellectual debt is to W.E.B. Du Bois, whose Black Reconstruction (1935) made the core argument — that Black labor, enslaved and free, built American capitalism — eighty years before the New History of Capitalism rediscovered it archive by archive. Beckert's work is, among other things, the global-scale archival vindication of Du Bois.

Key ideas

  • War capitalism — the long phase (c. 1500–1850) of imperial conquest, slavery, and state-backed coercion that established the conditions for industrial capitalism
  • Capitalism as globally constituted from its origins, not a European export to the world
  • Cotton as the analytic thread tying plantation, factory, and peasantry across continents
  • Capitalism as a violent, contingent, politically constructed system rather than as the natural state of market exchange
  • Slavery as constitutive of, not incidental to, the wealth of the nineteenth-century United States
