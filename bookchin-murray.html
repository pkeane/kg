Murray Bookchin

1921 – 2006 · American
#anarchism#ecology#political-theory#communalism#environmentalism#libertarian-socialism

American political philosopher and one of the principal 20th-century theorists of the relationship between ecological crisis and social hierarchy. Born in the Bronx to Russian Jewish immigrants, Bookchin began his political life in the Communist youth movement of the 1930s, broke with Stalinism in 1939, passed through Trotskyism in the 1940s, and by the early 1950s had moved toward anarchism. He spent most of the rest of his life developing what he came to call social ecology, and in 1974 co-founded the Institute for Social Ecology in Plainfield, Vermont, which became the institutional home of the tradition.

The central claim of social ecology is that the domination of nature is rooted in the domination of human beings by other human beings. Hierarchies of class, gender, age, and ethnicity, Bookchin argued, are historically prior to and the condition of the instrumental attitude toward the non-human world; the ecological crisis is therefore a political and social crisis, not a problem of "humanity" as an undifferentiated species or of population, technology, or consumer choice as such. The argument is set out at length in The Ecology of Freedom (1982), his major work, which traces the emergence of hierarchy from preliterate societies through to capitalism. Our Synthetic Environment, written under the pseudonym Lewis Herber and published in 1962 a few months before Carson's Silent Spring, had already laid out the chemical and ecological evidence that the postwar industrial order was poisoning its own substrate.

From the late 1960s onward Bookchin developed a positive program he called first libertarian municipalism and later communalism: face-to-face direct democracy in neighborhood and town assemblies, federated through recallable delegates into a "confederal" counter-power to the nation-state and capitalism. The program is intended as a political form for a post-scarcity society in which modern technology has, in principle, made toil unnecessary — the argument of Post-Scarcity Anarchism (1971). In Social Anarchism or Lifestyle Anarchism (1995) Bookchin attacked individualist, primitivist, and countercultural strands of contemporary anarchism — Hakim Bey, John Zerzan, deep ecology — as bourgeois withdrawal from organized politics; the polemic split the anarchist milieu and Bookchin himself eventually dropped the label "anarchist" in favor of "communalist." His four-volume The Third Revolution (1996–2003) is a history of popular revolutionary movements from the English Civil War to the Spanish Revolution of 1936.

Bookchin's largest practical legacy is the adoption of communalism by Abdullah Öcalan and the Kurdish movement as the basis for "democratic confederalism," the political form of the Rojava administration in northern Syria from 2012 onward.

Key ideas

  • Social ecology: ecological crisis as rooted in social hierarchy
  • The historical priority of hierarchy over class
  • Post-scarcity as material condition for non-hierarchical society
  • Libertarian municipalism / communalism: confederated direct democracy
  • Critique of "lifestyle anarchism" and deep ecology
  • Dialectical naturalism
