Goods That Don't Scale

20th–21st century
#political-economy#economics#collective-action#externalities#labor#finance#platforms#environment#regulation

A recurring structural pattern: a practice that is rational, beneficial, or harmless when a few people do it becomes destructive when it becomes the dominant mode rather than a marginal one. A worker taking gig jobs for flexible side income is in a different situation from a labor market in which gig arrangements have replaced salaried employment with benefits; an individual buying a house as a hedge is different from an asset class organized around housing as a financial instrument. The logical name for the underlying inference error is the fallacy of composition — assuming that what is true of a part must be true of the whole. The economic cognates are the negative externality (a private actor's gain imposes uncosted harm on others), the collective-action problem (individually rational free-riding under-provides shared goods), and the paradox of thrift (individually prudent saving, in aggregate, can lower demand and incomes).

The cases below are organized by domain. Each names the mechanism by which aggregation flips the sign — congestion, risk-shifting, free-riding, network capture, resistance, positional competition — rather than gesturing at "balance." That discipline matters because "good in small doses" can itself be a rhetorical move: a way to defend a practice from regulation by attributing its harms to mere overuse rather than to the practice becoming structural. The general lesson, drawn from Elinor Ostrom's work, is that whether a good "scales" is usually a question of what institutions govern it, not a fixed property of the good. See Economics as Open System and Systems Theory and Politics for the analytic frame.

The logic of the part and the whole

The conceptual toolkit the cases draw on:

  • John Maynard Keynes, The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936) — the paradox of thrift. In a downturn, every household's individually sensible decision to save more reduces aggregate demand, lowers incomes, and can leave everyone saving less. Paul Samuelson's Economics (1948) later fixed "fallacy of composition" as a standard teaching term.
  • A.C. Pigou, The Economics of Welfare (1920) — the gap between private and social marginal cost, and the corrective ("Pigouvian") tax on activities whose costs fall on third parties.
  • Garrett Hardin, "The Tragedy of the Commons," Science (1968) — each herdsman rationally adds cattle to a shared pasture; the sum is overgrazing. Influential and much criticized: Hardin conflated open access with common property and later drew anti-immigration conclusions that Ostrom rejected.
  • Mancur Olson, The Logic of Collective Action (1965) — large groups under-provide collective goods because each member can free-ride; organization requires selective incentives.
  • Elinor Ostrom, Governing the Commons (1990) — the empirical rebuttal: local institutions repeatedly solve common-pool problems through monitoring, graduated sanctions, and nested governance. "Tragedy" is a design problem, not a destiny.
  • Thomas Schelling, Micromotives and Macrobehavior (1978) — how individually mild preferences aggregate into collective outcomes nobody intended (the tipping models of residential segregation).
  • Fred Hirsch, Social Limits to Growth (1976) — positional goods, valued precisely because others lack them; universal pursuit is self-cancelling.
  • Hyman Minsky, Stabilizing an Unstable Economy (1986) — the financial-instability hypothesis: in good times, individually rational increases in leverage make the system as a whole progressively more fragile.

Economy and labor

The gig economy

Flexible side income for an individual; at scale, gig arrangements replace salaried employment with benefits, shifting the cost of downtime, insurance, and retirement onto workers reclassified as independent contractors. The mechanism is risk-shifting plus erosion of the regulatory floor (minimum wage, overtime, unemployment insurance) that attaches to employment but not to contracting.

  • Guy Standing, The Precariat: The New Dangerous Class (2011) — names the class-in-formation produced by labor flexibilization: workers without occupational identity, security, or benefits.
  • Alex Rosenblat, Uberland: How Algorithms Are Rewriting the Rules of Work (2018) — ethnography of platform-managed driving and the gap between entrepreneurial rhetoric and algorithmic control without employer obligations.

401(k) plans

A tax-advantaged supplement to a pension works for a high earner with surplus to invest. As a societal replacement for defined-benefit pensions, the same instrument transfers market, timing, and longevity risk onto individual retirees and leaves the median worker under-saved. The mechanism is risk-shift from a pooled obligation to an individual one.

  • Jacob Hacker, The Great Risk Shift (2006; rev. 2019) — the systematic transfer of economic risk from government and employers onto households, with the move from defined-benefit to defined-contribution plans as the central case.
  • Teresa Ghilarducci, When I'm Sixty-Four: The Plot Against Pensions and the Plan to Save Them (2008) — the empirical indictment of the 401(k) as a retirement system.

Algorithmic pricing

A single merchant using software to optimize inventory and match local demand is unremarkable. Across an industry, when many firms feed and follow the same pricing model, individually legal optimization can function as tacit coordination — price-fixing without an explicit agreement. The mechanism is convergence on a shared optimizer.

  • Cathy O'Neil, Weapons of Math Destruction (2016) — algorithmic systems that optimize locally while producing opaque, self-reinforcing harm at scale. The U.S. Department of Justice's 2024 antitrust suit against RealPage alleges that shared rent-pricing software functioned as de facto coordination across landlords.

Stock buybacks

For one firm with no productive use for surplus cash, returning it to shareholders is defensible. Economy-wide, the shift from "retain and reinvest" to "downsize and distribute" diverts corporate earnings from R&D, capacity, and wages toward share price. The mechanism is composition: capital allocation sensible per firm starves aggregate investment.

  • William Lazonick, "Profits Without Prosperity," Harvard Business Review (2014) — documents the shift and quantifies the share of earnings spent on buybacks rather than reinvestment.

Technology and media

Substack and the unbundling of journalism

Direct-subscription newsletters let an individual writer monetize a niche audience. At scale, the unbundling strips out the cross-subsidy by which advertising and popular sections once funded investigative and local reporting, and sorts audiences into paid, often ideologically homogeneous silos. The mechanism is loss of the bundle's cross-subsidy plus audience fragmentation.

  • Margaret Sullivan, Ghosting the News: Local Journalism and the Crisis of American Democracy (2020) — the collapse of local investigative reporting as advertising revenue migrated to platforms and audiences fragmented.

Ad-supported platforms

Free, useful software for a user in exchange for data is a real bargain at the individual level. As the dominant model it rewards engagement maximization, behavioral surveillance, and outrage, because attention and data are what get sold. The mechanism is that the incentive to monetize attention dominates once advertising is the whole business.

  • Shoshana Zuboff, The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (2019) — the extraction and sale of behavioral data as an economy-wide apparatus of prediction and behavioral modification.
  • Tim Wu, The Attention Merchants (2016) — the long history of monetizing attention by resale to advertisers, and the resulting incentive toward whatever maximizes engagement.

Algorithmic content curation

A personalized feed is pleasant and efficient for one user. Across a society, per-user relevance optimization erodes the shared exposure to unchosen information and common reference points that deliberative democracy depends on. The mechanism is that optimizing each feed narrows the informational commons.

  • Cass Sunstein, #Republic: Divided Democracy in the Age of Social Media (2017) — personalization versus the shared public sphere.
  • Eli Pariser, The Filter Bubble (2011) — the early statement of the problem.

Society and lifestyle

Tourism

In moderation, tourism brings local income and cross-cultural exchange. At scale ("overtourism") it prices out residents, strains infrastructure, and converts living cities into curated stage sets. The mechanism is congestion combined with a positional/commons dynamic — the destination's character is a shared resource that mass visitation depletes.

  • Elizabeth Becker, Overbooked: The Exploding Business of Travel and Tourism (2013) — tourism as a development engine and a degrading force, depending on volume and governance.

Hyper-specialization

Specialized expertise is efficient within a domain. At scale it leaves organizations and societies short of the integrative, cross-domain judgment that complex, interconnected problems require. The mechanism here is a systemic blind spot rather than an externality, which makes it the loosest fit for the frame.

  • David Epstein, Range: Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World (2019) — the case for breadth and analogical transfer against premature specialization.

Antibiotics

Antibiotics save individual lives from acute bacterial infection. Overused in general medicine and especially in industrial farming, they accelerate antimicrobial resistance, a cost borne by everyone. The mechanism is a textbook negative externality over a depletable common resource: the effectiveness of antibiotics is a shared inheritance that each unnecessary use erodes.

  • Maryn McKenna, Big Chicken (2017) — the routine, growth-promoting use of antibiotics in agriculture and its link to resistant pathogens.
  • The UK government's Review on Antimicrobial Resistance (Jim O'Neill, 2016) put the global cost of unchecked resistance in the same externality frame.
