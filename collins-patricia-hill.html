Patricia Hill Collins

1948 – ? · American
#feminism#african-american-thought#sociology#intersectionality#standpoint-theory

American sociologist, Distinguished University Professor emerita at the University of Maryland and former chair of African American studies at the University of Cincinnati. Black Feminist Thought established Black feminist standpoint as a distinctive epistemic tradition in American sociology. She served as the 100th president of the American Sociological Association in 2009, the first Black woman to hold the office.

Black Feminist Thought: Knowledge, Consciousness, and the Politics of Empowerment (Unwin Hyman, 1990; revised editions 2000, 2008) argues that Black women in the United States have developed, across roughly two centuries of struggle, a coherent body of knowledge rooted in their position at the intersection of racism, sexism, and class oppression. Collins draws on Anna Julia Cooper, Ida B. Wells, Sojourner Truth, Audre Lorde, Toni Morrison, June Jordan, and others to reconstruct a Black feminist epistemological tradition with characteristic emphases: lived experience as a criterion of meaning; dialogue rather than adversarial debate as a method of arriving at knowledge claims; an ethic of care; an ethic of personal accountability. The book is also one of the more sustained treatments of Black women's intellectual production as theory rather than as illustration or testimony.

Her account of standpoint is explicit in refusing the essentialism charge that has been pressed against feminist standpoint theory. Standpoint is not deduced from being-a-Black-woman; it is constructed through collective intellectual and political work on the experience that social position makes available. The position is congruent with Harding's and Haraway's — see Situated Knowledge and the Embodied Knower for the broader topic — but rooted in a different intellectual genealogy.

Collins also develops the concept of the "matrix of domination" — the interlocking structure of oppressions whose specific configurations cannot be deduced from any single axis. Her later work — Black Sexual Politics (2004), Another Kind of Public Education (2009), Intersectionality (with Sirma Bilge, 2016), Intersectionality as Critical Social Theory (2019) — develops intersectionality (the framework named by Crenshaw) into a systematic social theory.

Key ideas

  • Black feminist standpoint as a distinctive epistemic tradition
  • The "matrix of domination" as the interlocking structure of oppression
  • Intersectionality as social theory, not only legal framework
  • Lived experience, dialogue, ethic of care, ethic of personal accountability as Black feminist criteria of knowledge
  • Self-definition as resistance to controlling images
