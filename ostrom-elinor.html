Elinor Ostrom

1933 – 2012 · American
#economics#political-science#commons#governance#institutions

American political economist (7 August 1933, Los Angeles – 12 June 2012, Bloomington, Indiana). She took her undergraduate degree at UCLA in 1954 and her Ph.D. in political science at UCLA in 1965, having been told that women were not welcome in the economics department. She spent her career at Indiana University Bloomington, where in 1973 she and her husband Vincent Ostrom founded the Workshop in Political Theory and Policy Analysis. In 2009 she became the first woman to receive the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, sharing it with Oliver Williamson.

Her work overturned a result that until the late 1980s had the status of established theory: Garrett Hardin's "tragedy of the commons" (1968), which argued that any resource held in common — pasture, fishery, forest, irrigation system, groundwater basin — would be overused and destroyed unless privatized or placed under central state authority. Ostrom and her collaborators built a vast empirical archive of cases — Alpine grazing meadows, Maine lobster fisheries, Philippine irrigation systems, Nepali forests, Spanish huertas, Turkish coastal fisheries — in which user communities had in fact governed common-pool resources sustainably across centuries, neither by privatizing them nor by handing them to the state. The institutions through which they did so could be catalogued, and Ostrom catalogued them. Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action (Cambridge, 1990) is the canonical statement, organized around eight "design principles" — clearly defined boundaries, rules matched to local conditions, collective-choice arrangements that include resource users, monitoring, graduated sanctions, low-cost conflict resolution, recognized rights to self-organize, and nested governance for larger systems.

Her Bloomington school developed the Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework as a method for the comparative study of any rule-governed situation, and applied it across policing, urban services, and increasingly to global commons including the climate. Her Nobel lecture, "Beyond Markets and States: Polycentric Governance of Complex Economic Systems" (American Economic Review, 2010), generalized the argument: there is no privileged level — local, regional, national, global — at which collective-action problems must be solved, and durable governance is typically polycentric, with overlapping arrangements at multiple scales.

Her position cuts orthogonally to the standard left-right framing: it refuses both the neoliberal program of privatizing the commons and the centralist statist program of nationalizing them, in favor of attention to actually-existing local institutions and their capacities for self-governance. She was widely read by left commons-and-cooperatives writers and by libertarian-leaning institutionalists alike.

Key ideas

  • Common-pool resources as a distinct economic category, distinct from public goods, private goods, and club goods
  • Eight design principles for durable community management of the commons
  • Polycentric governance — multiple, overlapping centers of decision-making across scales
  • The Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework
  • The empirical falsification of Hardin's "tragedy of the commons" generalization

Key works

  • Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action (1990)
  • with Roy Gardner and James Walker, Rules, Games, and Common-Pool Resources (1994)
  • Understanding Institutional Diversity (2005)
  • "Beyond Markets and States: Polycentric Governance of Complex Economic Systems," American Economic Review 100 (2010)
