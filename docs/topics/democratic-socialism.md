# Democratic Socialism

Democratic socialism names the family of political traditions that pursue socialist ends — collective or public control over significant parts of economic life, and the reduction of class inequality — through democratic and electoral means rather than through revolution or single-party rule. The term marks a double boundary: against capitalism on one side, and against the authoritarian state socialism of the Soviet model on the other. What counts as a "socialist end," and how far the program departs from a regulated market economy, varies widely across the figures grouped under the label, and the internal disagreements are as substantial as the external ones.

A recurring source of confusion is the relation between *democratic socialism* and *social democracy*. Historically the two terms were near-synonyms; both descend from the Second International (1889–1916) and the parties that called themselves Social Democratic. They diverged over the twentieth century. "Social democracy" came to denote the position — associated with the postwar European welfare states, the German SPD's 1959 Godesberg Program, and Anthony Crosland's revisionism — that accepts a private-property market economy and seeks to civilize it through full employment, progressive taxation, universal public services, and strong labor protections. "Democratic socialism" is often reserved for positions that retain the longer-run aim of transcending capitalism, even where the near-term program is indistinguishable from robust social democracy. In contemporary American usage the line is blurred: figures such as [[sanders-bernie|Bernie Sanders]] and [[mamdani-zohran|Zohran Mamdani]] use "democratic socialist" to describe programs — Medicare for All, public housing, sectoral bargaining, higher taxation of wealth — that sit within the social-democratic range against the postwar Western European baseline, while the rhetorical commitment to "socialism" signals a more systemic critique.

The American tradition is distinct from the European one in lacking a mass labor or social-democratic party. From [[debs-eugene|Eugene V. Debs]] through Norman Thomas, [[harrington-michael|Michael Harrington]], and the founders of the [[democratic-socialists-of-america|Democratic Socialists of America]], the American current has operated as a minority tendency — sometimes a third party, more often a pressure inside the Democratic coalition or the labor movement. Much of its moral vocabulary was supplied not by Marxism but by the [[social-gospel]] and the Black church tradition. This page collects the texts that define the tradition and its internal divisions. The forward-looking strategic question — whether the post-2016 American current can scale beyond the city level — is treated separately in [[democratic-socialism-and-scale]]; the broader center-left settlement is treated in [[post-neoliberal-politics-in-america]].

## Annotated bibliography

### The revisionist origin: reform versus revolution

- **Eduard Bernstein**, *Evolutionary Socialism* (*Die Voraussetzungen des Sozialismus*, 1899). The founding text of the reformist position. Bernstein, a leading German Social Democrat and literary executor of Engels, argued that Marx's predictions of capitalist collapse and class polarization were not being borne out, and that socialism would be reached through the gradual extension of democracy, trade unionism, and cooperatives rather than through a revolutionary rupture. His formula — "the movement is everything, the final goal nothing" — drew the line that the tradition has argued over ever since.
- **Karl Kautsky**, *The Road to Power* (1909) and the broader Bernstein-Kautsky "revisionist debate." Kautsky defended Marxist orthodoxy against Bernstein while still rejecting Blanquist insurrection; his "orthodox" position held that the party should win democratic majorities but not abandon the revolutionary horizon. The debate established the two poles — evolutionary reform and revolutionary patience — between which democratic socialism has oscillated.
- **Rosa Luxemburg**, *Reform or Revolution* (1900). The reply from the left: Luxemburg argued that reforms within capitalism, however valuable, could not by accumulation become socialism, and that Bernstein's revisionism amounted to abandoning the goal. Read as the permanent left-flank critique of social-democratic gradualism. See [[western-marxism]].

### The European social-democratic statement

- **Anthony Crosland**, *The Future of Socialism* (1956). The most influential postwar revisionist text in the English-speaking world. Crosland, a British Labour politician and theorist, argued that ownership of the means of production had become secondary: managerial capitalism, Keynesian demand management, and the welfare state had already tamed the system, so socialism should be redefined as the pursuit of equality and social welfare rather than nationalization. The intellectual charter of the Labour right and of "social democracy" in the narrower sense.
- **Sheri Berman**, *The Primacy of Politics: Social Democracy and the Making of Europe's Twentieth Century* (2006). Comparative-historical account arguing that social democracy, not communism or liberalism, was the most successful political response to the crises of the interwar period, because it asserted the primacy of democratic politics over both markets and class. Useful as the scholarly defense of the social-democratic settlement.
- **Tony Judt**, *Ill Fares the Land* (2010). A late polemic by the historian of postwar Europe, arguing for the recovery of the social-democratic achievement — the mid-century mixed economy and welfare state — against both market triumphalism and a left that had lost the language to defend public goods. Elegiac rather than programmatic.

### The American tradition

- **Eugene V. Debs**, speeches and writings, esp. the 1918 Canton, Ohio anti-war speech and the 1920 presidential campaign conducted from a federal prison cell. The founding figure of American electoral socialism; the Socialist Party of America under Debs won nearly a million votes in 1912 and 1920. See [[debs-eugene]].
- **Norman Thomas**, *Socialism Re-examined* (1963). Thomas led the Socialist Party from the late 1920s through the 1950s, running for president six times. His later writing records the tradition's pivot away from public ownership toward civil liberties, anti-militarism, and the welfare state as the party's electoral base eroded into the New Deal coalition.
- **[[harrington-michael|Michael Harrington]]**, *The Other America: Poverty in the United States* (1962) and *Socialism* (1972). *The Other America* influenced the War on Poverty and made Harrington the most visible American socialist of his generation; *Socialism* is his theoretical statement of a democratic, non-Communist socialism. Harrington founded the Democratic Socialist Organizing Committee (1973), a predecessor of the [[democratic-socialists-of-america|DSA]], and articulated the "realignment" strategy of working inside the Democratic Party. See [[poverty-in-america]].
- **Irving Howe**, *Socialism and America* (1985) and the journal *Dissent* (founded 1954). Howe gave the tradition its leading intellectual magazine and its characteristic anti-totalitarian, social-democratic, literary-humanist register. *Socialism and America* is his account of why socialism failed to take root in the United States.
- **Bayard Rustin**, "From Protest to Politics: The Future of the Civil Rights Movement" (*Commentary*, 1965). The strategic argument for fusing the civil-rights and labor movements into a democratic-socialist electoral coalition inside the Democratic Party; one of the clearest statements of the link between the Black freedom struggle and American social democracy. See [[social-gospel]].

### Contemporary restatement

- **[[sunkara-bhaskar|Bhaskar Sunkara]]**, *The Socialist Manifesto: The Case for Radical Politics in an Era of Extreme Inequality* (2019). The founder of *Jacobin* magazine restates the democratic-socialist case for a post-2016 audience: social-democratic reforms as a transitional program, a "class-struggle" electoral strategy, and an explicit retention of the longer-run aim of moving beyond capitalism. The most widely-read recent introduction.
- **Vivek Chibber**, *The ABCs of Socialism* (ed., 2016) and *The Class Matrix* (2022). The *Jacobin* primer answers the standard objections in short form; *The Class Matrix* is the theoretical argument that a materialist, class-based politics remains the necessary foundation, against the culturalist turn of the academic left.
- **Nathan Robinson**, *Why You Should Be a Socialist* (2019). A popular, accessible polemic from the editor of *Current Affairs*; useful as a sample of the millennial-left register that accompanied the Sanders campaigns.
- **[[milanovic-branko|Branko Milanović]]**, *Capitalism, Alone* (2019). Included as the skeptical counterpoint: Milanović argues that no systemic alternative to capitalism is currently on offer, so the real choice is between varieties of capitalism rather than between capitalism and socialism. A check on the "post-capitalism" framing some contemporary socialists adopt. See [[what-is-capitalism]].

### Reference and overview

- **Michael Newman**, *Socialism: A Very Short Introduction* (2005, rev. 2020). Compact overview of the socialist tradition with the democratic/authoritarian and reform/revolution distinctions clearly drawn.
- **Donald Sassoon**, *One Hundred Years of Socialism: The West European Left in the Twentieth Century* (1996). The standard one-volume history of European social democracy and democratic socialism from the Second International to the end of the century.
- **Geoff Eley**, *Forging Democracy: The History of the Left in Europe, 1850–2000* (2002). A longer institutional and movement history; emphasizes the left's role in the expansion of democracy itself.
