# Systems Theory and Politics

Systems analysis entered political science in the 1950s as part of a broader postwar enthusiasm for cybernetics, general systems theory, and structural-functionalism. The dominant figures were [[easton-david|David Easton]], who modeled the polity as a system converting **inputs** (demands and supports) into **outputs** (decisions and policies) regulated by feedback; [[deutsch-karl|Karl Deutsch]], who recast government as a communication and steering system in the cybernetic tradition of [[wiener-norbert|Norbert Wiener]]; and [[almond-gabriel|Gabriel Almond]], who built a structural-functional framework for comparing political systems by the functions they perform rather than by their formal institutions. Their sociological counterpart was [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]], whose AGIL scheme treated society as an integrated system of functional subsystems. The intellectual root was outside political science altogether — [[bertalanffy-ludwig|Ludwig von Bertalanffy]]'s general system theory in biology, generalized.

For roughly two decades the Easton–Almond model was the closest thing American political science had to a unifying framework. It was displaced. The behavioral revolution it helped launch generated its own counter-movement (Easton himself acknowledged the "post-behavioral revolution" in his 1969 APSA presidential address); rational choice took over in the 1980s; historical institutionalism, interpretive theory, and a renewed political economy filled the rest of the space. The substantive complaints had been there from the start — that functionalism assumed equilibrium and could not theorize revolutionary change ([[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]]'s attack on Parsons in *The Sociological Imagination*, 1959), that systems vocabulary dissolved class and conflict into neutral-sounding flows of inputs and outputs, that the model described an abstraction no actual polity matched.

The framework did not so much die as migrate. Its strongest direct continuation is German-language sociology and legal theory in the Luhmannian tradition, where the political system is one of several functionally differentiated subsystems of modern society, each operationally closed and reproducing itself through its own distinctions. Adjacent strands — Earth system governance, resilience theory, complex-adaptive-systems policy studies, and world-systems analysis — use systems vocabulary for problems (planetary boundaries, transnational governance, the capitalist world-economy) that the older national-polity input/output model was not built to handle. None has the disciplinary centrality that Easton briefly enjoyed.

## Annotated bibliography

### The classical framework (1950s–1970s)

- **[[bertalanffy-ludwig|Ludwig von Bertalanffy]]**, *General System Theory: Foundations, Development, Applications* (1968) — the source text outside the social sciences. A system is a set of interrelated elements whose behavior cannot be reduced to the sum of its parts; the same formal principles should apply across biology, engineering, economics, and society. Read for the ambition, not as political theory.
- **[[wiener-norbert|Norbert Wiener]]**, *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine* (1948) — the other founding text. Introduces feedback and control as the basic mechanism of self-regulating systems. The vocabulary Easton and Deutsch absorb.
- **[[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]]**, *The Social System* (1951) — sociological functionalism in its most systematic form. The AGIL scheme (adaptation, goal-attainment, integration, latency) treats society as a system of subsystems, each performing one function. Parsons is the intellectual context for Easton's translation into political science.
- **[[easton-david|David Easton]]**, *The Political System* (1953), *A Framework for Political Analysis* (1965), *A Systems Analysis of Political Life* (1965) — the central political-science application. Defines politics as "the authoritative allocation of values for a society" and models the polity as a system receiving demands and supports, processing them through institutions, producing outputs, and adjusting via feedback. The textbook version of "political system" descends directly from this.
- **[[deutsch-karl|Karl Deutsch]]**, *The Nerves of Government: Models of Political Communication and Control* (1963) — the cybernetic alternative to Easton. Government as a steering system; emphasis on information flow, learning, and self-correction rather than law or coercion. The most explicitly Wiener-derived political work.
- **[[almond-gabriel|Gabriel Almond]] and James Coleman (eds.)**, *The Politics of the Developing Areas* (1960); **Almond and G. Bingham Powell**, *Comparative Politics: A Developmental Approach* (1966) — structural-functionalism in comparative politics. Political systems compared by the functions they perform (interest articulation, aggregation, rule-making, rule-application, rule-adjudication) rather than by formal constitutional form.
- **[[simon-herbert|Herbert Simon]]**, *Administrative Behavior* (1947) — adjacent rather than central. Bounded rationality and the limits of formal decision models inform the policy-analysis side of the systems tradition, including the RAND Corporation's institutional uptake at McNamara's Defense Department (PPBS, the Planning-Programming-Budgeting System).

### Management cybernetics and Cybersyn

- **[[beer-stafford|Stafford Beer]]**, *Brain of the Firm* (1972) and *The Heart of Enterprise* (1979) — the two-volume statement of the Viable System Model: any organization at any scale is modeled as five recursively nested subsystems (operations, coordination, control, intelligence, policy) on analogy with the human nervous system. The most developed cybernetic theory of organization in English.
- **Stafford Beer**, *Designing Freedom* (1974, CBC Massey Lectures) — the compressed political statement. Argues that existing institutions fail because they cannot absorb the variety of the environments they face; redesign the information and control structure, not the people.
- **Eden Medina**, *Cybernetic Revolutionaries: Technology and Politics in Allende's Chile* (MIT, 2011) — the standard scholarly history of Beer's Project Cybersyn (1971–73), the only state-level instance of cybernetic economic management actually attempted. Central reference point for the contemporary [[socialist-calculation-debate]] revival.
- **Andrew Pickering**, *The Cybernetic Brain: Sketches of Another Future* (Chicago, 2010) — places Beer alongside Ashby, Walter, and Pask as the British cybernetics tradition; the most ambitious recent attempt to recover the road not taken in postwar systems thought.

### The internal critique and the discipline's exit

- **C. Wright Mills**, *The Sociological Imagination* (1959) — Chapter 2, "Grand Theory," is the canonical attack on Parsons; by extension on any systems-theoretic style that mistakes abstract typology for explanation. The charge that systems language obscures power and conflict starts here.
- **David Easton**, "The New Revolution in Political Science," APSA presidential address (1969) — Easton himself naming the post-behavioral turn. A document of the framework's loss of confidence in itself.
- **Brian Barry**, *Sociologists, Economists and Democracy* (1970) — an early statement of the rational-choice alternative that would displace systems analysis in the discipline; reads Easton and Almond as descriptively rich but explanatorily empty.

### Luhmann and the continuing German tradition

- **[[luhmann-niklas|Niklas Luhmann]]**, *Soziale Systeme* (1984; English *Social Systems*, 1995) and *Die Gesellschaft der Gesellschaft* (1997; English *Theory of Society*, 2012) — the most ambitious later systems theory. Society is composed of functionally differentiated subsystems (politics, law, economy, science, religion, art) that are operationally closed and reproduce themselves through their own distinctions ("autopoiesis," borrowed from Maturana and Varela). The political system is coded around power / no-power and cannot directly steer the others.
- **Niklas Luhmann**, *Political Theory in the Welfare State* (1981; English 1990) and *Die Politik der Gesellschaft* (2000) — the explicitly political applications. Argues that the welfare state's expansion has reached the limits of what the political subsystem can do without overreaching into law, economy, and science.
- **[[teubner-gunther|Gunther Teubner]]**, *Constitutional Fragments: Societal Constitutionalism and Globalization* (2012) — applies Luhmann's functional differentiation to transnational law. Constitutions are not only state-based; functional subsystems generate their own constitutional norms across borders.
- **[[thornhill-chris|Chris Thornhill]]**, *A Sociology of Constitutions* (2011) and *A Sociology of Transnational Constitutions* (2016) — systems-theoretic historical sociology of constitutional development from the early modern state through contemporary transnational law.
- **Hans-Georg Moeller**, *The Radical Luhmann* (2012) — a compact English-language exposition of Luhmann for readers coming from outside the German sociological tradition.

### Earth system governance and the Anthropocene

- **[[biermann-frank|Frank Biermann]]**, *Earth System Governance: World Politics in the Anthropocene* (2014) — the central statement of the field. Argues that the conceptual unit of governance has to shift from the international system of states to the Earth system as a whole, on the grounds that ecological processes ignore the boundaries the older systems theories presupposed.
- **Johan Rockström et al.**, "A Safe Operating Space for Humanity," *Nature* (2009) — the planetary-boundaries framework that political theorists working on climate governance work downstream of. Earth-system science rather than political theory, but unavoidable as background.
- **Oran Young**, *Governing Complex Systems: Social Capital for the Anthropocene* (2017) — decades of work on international environmental regimes recast in systems-theoretic vocabulary.

### Resilience theory in governance

- **C.S. Holling**, "Resilience and Stability of Ecological Systems," *Annual Review of Ecology and Systematics* (1973) — the ecological source for what becomes a governance vocabulary.
- **Brian Walker and David Salt**, *Resilience Thinking* (2006) — the application to social-ecological systems and policy.
- **Jeremy Walker and Melinda Cooper**, "Genealogies of Resilience: From Systems Ecology to the Political Economy of Crisis Adaptation," *Security Dialogue* (2011) — the critical reply. Argues that resilience theory smuggles neoliberal assumptions (adaptation, self-organization, the dissolution of state responsibility) into governance under the cover of systems-scientific neutrality. Essential counterweight reading.

### Complexity and agent-based modeling

- **[[axelrod-robert|Robert Axelrod]]**, *The Complexity of Cooperation: Agent-Based Models of Competition and Collaboration* (1997) — the canonical application of complex-adaptive-systems methods to political and social questions, building on his earlier *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984).
- **Joshua Epstein**, *Generative Social Science: Studies in Agent-Based Computational Modeling* (2006) — the methodological manifesto for "generative" explanation: if you cannot grow a phenomenon in an agent-based model, you have not explained it.
- **Scott Page**, *The Difference* (2007) and *Diversity and Complexity* (2010) — complexity theory applied to collective decision-making, with normative implications for democratic design.

### System dynamics and limits-to-growth

- **Donella Meadows, Dennis Meadows, Jørgen Randers, and William Behrens**, *The Limits to Growth* (1972) — the Club of Rome report. Applied Jay Forrester's system-dynamics modeling (World3) to global population, resource use, and pollution. Politically polarizing on publication, partially vindicated by subsequent ecological economics, and the proximate source of the systems vocabulary now common in degrowth and ecological-economics literatures.
- **[[meadows-donella|Donella Meadows]]**, *Thinking in Systems: A Primer* (2008, posthumous) — the most accessible introduction to system-dynamics thinking, written for policy audiences. Frequently cited across environmental governance, public-health, and sustainability literatures.

### World-systems analysis

- **[[wallerstein-immanuel|Immanuel Wallerstein]]**, *The Modern World-System*, four volumes (1974–2011) — different sense of "system" (the capitalist world-economy as the unit of analysis, with core, semi-periphery, and periphery), but explicitly systems-framed and continuous with the broader tradition through its Parsons-trained author. Wallerstein's introductory *World-Systems Analysis: An Introduction* (2004) is the compressed version.
- **[[arrighi-giovanni|Giovanni Arrighi]]**, *The Long Twentieth Century* (1994) and *Adam Smith in Beijing* (2007) — the most influential post-Wallerstein world-systems work. Traces successive cycles of capital accumulation (Genoese, Dutch, British, American) and argues for an East Asian re-centering.
- **Christopher Chase-Dunn and Thomas Hall**, *Rise and Demise: Comparing World-Systems* (1997) — comparative world-systems analysis extended back into pre-capitalist history; the discipline's effort to generalize Wallerstein's framework beyond the modern period.

### What faded, and what replaced it

- **Theda Skocpol**, *States and Social Revolutions* (1979) — not a systems critique per se, but the canonical demonstration of what historical-comparative analysis could do that the Easton/Almond framework could not: explain actual revolutions in France, Russia, and China through state-structural and international-conjunctural analysis. Marks the historical-institutionalist turn that absorbed the discipline's energy away from systems theory.
- **Peter Hall and Rosemary Taylor**, "Political Science and the Three New Institutionalisms," *Political Studies* (1996) — useful as a map of what political science was doing once systems theory had receded: rational-choice, historical, and sociological institutionalisms competing for the ground systems analysis had vacated.
