# Energy Democracy and the Bottom-Up Grid

Energy democracy is a term used in the environmental-justice and public-power literatures for pairing the renewable-energy transition with decentralization of ownership and control of energy infrastructure — community and cooperative ownership, public participation in energy policymaking, and local generation. A related but distinct engineering literature proposes reorganizing the physical and institutional architecture of the electric grid from a top-down system (large remote plants, one-way flow to passive consumers) to a layered, bottom-up system in which local areas coordinate their own distributed resources and the bulk transmission system serves as backup. The two literatures overlap in their conclusions but arrive from different premises: one from questions of ownership and justice, the other from questions of system architecture and cost.

Lorenzo Kristov, formerly a principal at the California Independent System Operator (CAISO) for over eighteen years, is one exponent of the architectural argument. In a 2024 interview with David Roberts, Kristov proposed a nested-layers design: microgrids, campuses, and neighborhoods coordinate internally under a Distribution System Operator (DSO), which presents a single net demand and supply point to the transmission system. He cited an NREL study finding rooftop solar could supply 38.6 percent of national electricity sales (74 percent in California), and identified investor-owned utility regulation — guaranteed rates of return on asset construction — as the principal obstacle, since coordination models reduce infrastructure spending and therefore utility revenue. Pilot efforts he named include Holy Cross Energy (Colorado), a legislated DSO feasibility study in Maine (RFP issued January 2024), an Ontario IESO working group, and Kit Carson Electric Cooperative (New Mexico). Two further points from the interview: front-of-meter solar sited on the built environment (warehouse roofs, parking lots, schools) as a distinct planning category with non-energy benefits, and "grid defection" — the risk that wealthy customers build microgrids and leave fixed system costs to poorer ratepayers.

The ownership argument runs on a separate track. Sandeep Vaheesan's history of U.S. electrification treats the New Deal restructuring — the Tennessee Valley Authority and the rural electric cooperatives — as precedent for a publicly led decarbonization; the Fairchild–Weinrub collection argues for community ownership led by marginalized communities. Meredith Angwin's critique of Regional Transmission Organization governance is skeptical of the restructured-market status quo from a reliability standpoint rather than a decentralization one, and is included here as a counterweight.

## Annotated bibliography

### The architectural argument

- **David Roberts (host), interview with Lorenzo Kristov**, "Envisioning a more democratic, bottom-up energy system," *Volts* podcast, May 15, 2024. https://www.volts.wtf/p/envisioning-a-more-democratic-bottom — Kristov lays out the layered DSO architecture, front-of-meter solar on the built environment, direct developer-to-CCA power purchases, and the regulatory obstacle of utility returns on asset ownership. He argues that transmission planning should model high-DER scenarios rather than assuming all new electrification load arrives at the bulk system, and cites the UC Irvine and UC San Diego campuses as existing cases of many buildings operating as a single controllable entity.
- **Lorenzo Kristov, Paul De Martini, and Jeffrey D. Taft**, "A Tale of Two Visions: Designing a Decentralized Transactive Electric System," *IEEE Power and Energy Magazine* 14:3 (May/June 2016), 63–69. Distinguishes two paradigms for a system with high penetration of distributed energy resources — a "total DSO" model and a "grand central" model — and is Kristov's technical statement of the layered architecture.
- **Lorenzo Kristov**, "The Bottom-Up (R)Evolution of the Electric Power System: The Pathway to the Integrated-Decentralized System," *IEEE Power and Energy Magazine* 17:2 (March/April 2019). DOI 10.1109/MPE.2018.2885204. Argues that decarbonization, resilience, and end-user control together point to a transition driven as much from the bottom up as from the top down, including a convergence of urban planning with power-system planning.

### The grid itself

- **Gretchen Bakke**, *The Grid: The Fraying Wires Between Americans and Our Energy Future* (2016). History of the U.S. grid — financial, legal, regulatory, environmental — with a sketch of a more distributed future system.
- **Meredith Angwin**, *Shorting the Grid: The Hidden Fragility of Our Electric Grid* (2020). Argues that Regional Transmission Organization governance is insider-dominated and has made the grid more fragile. Written from a reliability and pro-nuclear standpoint, and a counterpoint to the decentralization proposals rather than a version of them: Angwin's objection to the restructured market is that it has already dispersed responsibility too far to keep the lights on.

### Local energy and ownership

- **Sandeep Vaheesan**, *Democracy in Power: A History of Electrification in the United States* (University of Chicago Press, 2024). History of the public-versus-private contest over U.S. electricity — the New Deal, the TVA, the rural electric cooperatives — ending in an argument for a publicly led decarbonization. Vaheesan is legal director of the Open Markets Institute, which locates the book in the antimonopoly revival discussed in [[monopoly-and-democracy]]. Reviewed in *Jacobin*, January 2025: https://jacobin.com/2025/01/energy-democracy-vaheesan-review
- **Denise Fairchild and Al Weinrub (eds.)**, *Energy Democracy: Advancing Equity in Clean Energy Solutions* (Island Press, 2017). Essay collection arguing for shifting control and ownership of energy systems from corporations to communities, with an environmental-justice frame and an emphasis on leadership by low-income communities and communities of color. The source of the term as it is now used in the U.S. movement literature.
- **Bill Nussey**, *Freeing Energy: How Innovators Are Using Local-scale Solar and Batteries to Disrupt the Global Energy Industry from the Outside In* (2021). The business case for "local energy," by a technology CEO and venture investor, based on 320 interviews. Nussey's argument is that small solar-plus-battery systems behave like technologies rather than fuels, and so follow declining cost curves rather than commodity prices.

### Adjacent in the graph

- **[[ostrom-elinor|Elinor Ostrom]]**, *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action* (1990). Design principles for common-pool resource institutions, cited throughout the community-energy literature as the reference account of how local users can govern a shared resource without either privatization or central administration.
- **[[klein-naomi|Naomi Klein]]**, *This Changes Everything: Capitalism vs. the Climate* (2014), which makes the ownership question central to the climate question; and **[[malm-andreas|Andreas Malm]]**, *Fossil Capital* (2016), on the historical entanglement of fossil energy with the concentration of ownership — steam engines, unlike water wheels, could be sited where labor was.
- **[[mazzucato-mariana|Mariana Mazzucato]]**, *The Entrepreneurial State* (2013), on public investment in energy technology, and the mission-oriented framing that runs through [[state-capacity-and-technology]].
- **[[berry-wendell|Wendell Berry]]**, for the older localist case that the scale of an infrastructure determines the character of the community it serves.
