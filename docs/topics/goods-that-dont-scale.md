# Goods That Don't Scale

A recurring structural pattern: a practice that is rational, beneficial, or harmless when a few people do it becomes destructive when it becomes the dominant mode rather than a marginal one. A worker taking gig jobs for flexible side income is in a different situation from a labor market in which gig arrangements have replaced salaried employment with benefits; an individual buying a house as a hedge is different from an asset class organized around housing as a financial instrument. The logical name for the underlying inference error is the **fallacy of composition** — assuming that what is true of a part must be true of the whole. The economic cognates are the **negative externality** (a private actor's gain imposes uncosted harm on others), the **collective-action problem** (individually rational free-riding under-provides shared goods), and the **paradox of thrift** (individually prudent saving, in aggregate, can lower demand and incomes).

The intellectual lineage is well established. **John Maynard Keynes**'s *The General Theory* (1936) gave the canonical macroeconomic case in the paradox of thrift; **Paul Samuelson**'s *Economics* (1948) fixed "fallacy of composition" as a standard teaching term. **A.C. Pigou**'s *The Economics of Welfare* (1920) is the foundational treatment of the divergence between private and social cost. **Garrett Hardin**'s "The Tragedy of the Commons" (*Science*, 1968) is the most cited statement of the open-access version, and **Mancur Olson**'s *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965) the most cited statement of the free-rider version. **Elinor Ostrom**'s *Governing the Commons* (1990) is the decisive empirical rebuttal: communities repeatedly devise institutions that govern common-pool resources without either privatization or top-down state control — which means "doesn't scale" is usually a claim about the absence of governance, not a law of nature.

This page collects contemporary cases — in labor, finance, platforms, and the environmental and health commons — where the small-dose / large-dose distinction is doing political work. One caution runs through it. "Good in small doses" can itself be a rhetorical move: a way to defend a practice from regulation by attributing its harms to mere overuse rather than to the practice becoming structural. The honest version of the claim always specifies the mechanism by which aggregation flips the sign — congestion, free-riding, risk-shifting, network capture, resistance, positional competition — rather than gesturing at "balance." See [[economics-as-open-system]] and [[systems-theory-and-politics]] for the analytic frame.

## Annotated bibliography

### The logic of the part and the whole

- **[[keynes-jm|John Maynard Keynes]]**, *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936) — the paradox of thrift. In a downturn, every household's individually sensible decision to save more reduces aggregate demand, lowers incomes, and can leave everyone saving less. The founding macroeconomic instance of the composition fallacy.
- **A.C. Pigou**, *The Economics of Welfare* (1920) — develops the gap between private and social marginal product, the conceptual basis for the corrective ("Pigouvian") tax on activities whose costs fall on third parties. The standard answer to externalities is to price them.
- **Garrett Hardin**, "The Tragedy of the Commons," *Science* (1968) — each herdsman rationally adds cattle to a shared pasture; the sum is overgrazing and collapse. Enormously influential and much criticized: Hardin conflated open access with common property, and later drew anti-immigration and coercive-population conclusions that Ostrom and others rejected. Read with its rebuttal.
- **Mancur Olson**, *The Logic of Collective Action: Public Goods and the Theory of Groups* (1965) — large groups under-provide collective goods because each member can free-ride on others' effort; organization requires selective incentives. The microfoundation for why diffuse interests lose to concentrated ones.
- **[[ostrom-elinor|Elinor Ostrom]]**, *Governing the Commons* (1990) — the empirical case that local institutions (irrigation districts, fisheries, forests) solve common-pool problems through monitoring, graduated sanctions, and nested governance. Reframes "tragedy" as a design problem with documented solutions.
- **Thomas Schelling**, *Micromotives and Macrobehavior* (1978) — how individually mild preferences aggregate into collective outcomes nobody intended (the tipping models of residential segregation are the famous example). The general mechanics of part-to-whole surprise.
- **Fred Hirsch**, *Social Limits to Growth* (1976) — **positional goods**: things valued precisely because others lack them (an exclusive address, a credential's scarcity, a clear road). Universal pursuit is self-cancelling; growth in their supply destroys the value sought. A distinct mechanism from congestion or pollution.

### Labor and retirement

- **Guy Standing**, *The Precariat: The New Dangerous Class* (2011) — names the class-in-formation produced by labor flexibilization: workers without occupational identity, security, or benefits. The argument that "flexibility" framed as individual freedom is, in aggregate, a transfer of risk.
- **Alex Rosenblat**, *Uberland: How Algorithms Are Rewriting the Rules of Work* (2018) — ethnography of platform-managed driving: the gap between the rhetoric of independent entrepreneurship and the reality of algorithmic control without employer obligations.
- **[[hacker-jacob|Jacob Hacker]]**, *The Great Risk Shift* (2006; rev. 2019) — the systematic transfer of economic risk from government and employers onto individuals and families, with the move from defined-benefit pensions to defined-contribution (401(k)) plans as the central case. A retirement-savings vehicle that supplements a pension is not a substitute for one.
- **Teresa Ghilarducci**, *When I'm Sixty-Four: The Plot Against Pensions and the Plan to Save Them* (2008) — the empirical indictment of the 401(k) as a societal retirement system: it works for high earners with surplus to invest and fails the median worker exposed to market timing and longevity risk.

### Finance and the firm

- **[[minsky-hyman|Hyman Minsky]]**, *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy* (1986) — the financial-instability hypothesis: in good times, individually rational increases in leverage make the system as a whole progressively more fragile, until a small shock triggers collapse. Stability breeds instability. The clearest fallacy-of-composition account of finance.
- **William Lazonick**, "Profits Without Prosperity," *Harvard Business Review* (2014) — stock buybacks, sensible for a single firm managing its share price, become at scale a diversion of corporate earnings away from R&D, wages, and productive investment. Documents the shift from "retain and reinvest" to "downsize and distribute."
- **Cathy O'Neil**, *Weapons of Math Destruction* (2016) — algorithmic decision systems that optimize locally while producing opaque, self-reinforcing harm at scale. The frame for understanding algorithmic pricing; the U.S. Department of Justice's 2024 antitrust suit against RealPage alleges that shared pricing software functioned as de facto rent coordination across landlords.

### Platforms, attention, and the public sphere

- **[[zuboff-shoshana|Shoshana Zuboff]]**, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019) — the "free" ad-supported platform as a bargain in which behavioral data is extracted and sold; convenient per user, it builds an economy-wide apparatus of prediction and behavioral modification.
- **[[wu-tim|Tim Wu]]**, *The Attention Merchants* (2016) — the long history of monetizing attention by resale to advertisers, and the resulting incentive toward whatever maximizes engagement regardless of social cost.
- **Cass Sunstein**, *#Republic: Divided Democracy in the Age of Social Media* (2017) — personalized curation, pleasant for the individual user, erodes the shared exposure to unchosen information and common reference points that a deliberative democracy depends on.
- **Eli Pariser**, *The Filter Bubble* (2011) — the early statement of the personalization-versus-shared-reality problem; algorithmic relevance optimized per user narrows the collective informational commons.
- **Margaret Sullivan**, *Ghosting the News: Local Journalism and the Crisis of American Democracy* (2020) — the collapse of local investigative reporting as advertising revenue migrated to platforms and audiences fragmented across direct-subscription and niche outlets.

### Commons, environment, and health

- **Elizabeth Becker**, *Overbooked: The Exploding Business of Travel and Tourism* (2013) — tourism as a development engine in moderation and a degrading force at scale: pricing out residents, straining infrastructure, and converting living cities into curated stage sets ("overtourism").
- **Maryn McKenna**, *Big Chicken* (2017) — the routine use of antibiotics in industrial agriculture: rational and cheap per operation, it accelerates antimicrobial resistance, a textbook negative externality borne by the whole population. The UK government's *Review on Antimicrobial Resistance* (Jim O'Neill, 2016) put the global cost in the same frame.
- **David Epstein**, *Range: Why Generalists Triumph in a Specialized World* (2019) — the case that hyper-specialization, efficient within a domain, leaves organizations and societies short of the integrative, cross-domain judgment that complex problems require. The weakest fit for the "externality" frame and the strongest for the "positional / systemic blind spot" one.
- **[[ostrom-elinor|Elinor Ostrom]]** (see above) — climate and the atmosphere are the largest common-pool case; Ostrom's later work argued for **polycentric** governance (action at many overlapping scales) rather than waiting for a single global treaty. The general lesson of this page: whether a good "scales" is largely a question of what institutions govern it.
