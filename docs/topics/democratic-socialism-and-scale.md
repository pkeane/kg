# Democratic Socialism and the Problem of Scale

The American democratic-socialist current that emerged from Bernie Sanders's 2016 and 2020 presidential primary campaigns, organized through a tenfold expansion of the Democratic Socialists of America, and now holds executive power in New York City through [[mamdani-zohran|Zohran Mamdani]]'s 2025 mayoral victory, has a city-level proof of concept and an unresolved national problem. The proof of concept is real: a socialist coalition can build the field operation, the candidate pipeline, the donor base, and the messaging capacity to win a major American city against the institutional Democratic Party and a heavily-funded opponent. The unresolved problem is whether anything in that achievement is portable to the state and federal levels where the policies the movement actually wants would have to be enacted.

This page is a structured pre-analysis of that question, in the [[systems-theory-and-politics|systems-analytic frame]] used on the symmetrical right-wing question in [[the-rise-of-maga-systems-view]]. The argument is not that democratic socialism *will* scale, nor that it *cannot*. It is that a serious answer requires identifying the subsystems whose alignment produced the city-level wins, asking which of those subsystems exist at the state and national level, and identifying what would have to be built or restructured for the alignment to be reproducible. The European comparative cases — the Corbyn-era British Labour Party (2015–2019), the German Linke (founded 2007), the French France Insoumise (2016–present), the Spanish Podemos (2014–present) — are not models to be copied (the institutional environments are different) but data points on what does and does not work when democratic-socialist projects try to operate at national scale.

The literature consulted here is partisan in the sense that most of the authors writing on the strategic question are themselves democratic socialists or fellow travelers — Bhaskar Sunkara, Eric Blanc, Vivek Chibber, Jane McAlevey, Kim Moody, Dustin Guastella, Matt Karp. That partisanship is not concealed in their books. Where the analysis is contested *inside* the left, it is usually contested between people who agree on the goal and disagree on the route.

## The subsystems Mamdani 2025 had

The five elements whose alignment a plausible reconstruction would identify:

1. **Mature local organizational infrastructure.** NYC-DSA, built up over a decade and tested through state legislative campaigns (Julia Salazar 2018; Mamdani, Jabari Brisport, Marcela Mitaynes, Phara Souffrant Forrest 2020; subsequent waves) and through the 2018 Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez congressional campaign that established the template.
2. **Permissive institutional features.** Ranked-choice voting in New York City primaries (adopted 2019); fusion voting and the Working Families Party ballot line, a New York-specific institutional survival from the 1890s that most states banned. Both lower the coordination penalty for outsider candidates.
3. **A compressed material agenda matched to acute local pain.** Rent, transit, childcare, groceries — costs the median voter pays monthly and can attribute to specific city-level policy. Following the McAlevey / Hahrie Han argument that durable wins require concrete material asks rather than identity-affinity politics.
4. **Native-media literacy in short-form video** as a partial counter to the right-wing media asymmetry documented by Benkler.
5. **A degraded incumbent coalition.** The opponents (a scandal-burdened former governor, an incumbent under federal indictment) were structurally weaker than usual. Insurgent wins almost always require this; it is the easiest condition for observers to under-weight in retrospect.

The crucial point for the scale question is that *all five* were necessary. Removing any one would have substantially reduced the probability of the outcome. A scaling project that reproduces three of five is not 60% there.

## What thins out beyond the city

- **Organizational infrastructure** drops sharply outside large metros. The DSA has perhaps 80,000 dues-paying members nationally; even if every one of them were in a battleground district they would not be a majority anywhere. Theda Skocpol's *Diminished Democracy* (2003) documented the long-run decline of cross-class civic associations; the post-Sanders left has not rebuilt them at scale.
- **Permissive institutional features** are largely absent. Fusion voting exists in only a handful of states. Ranked-choice voting has spread to Maine, Alaska, and a few cities but is contested in many state legislatures.
- **Material agenda matched to local capacity.** Cities can experiment with transit, rent, and grocery policy at the margins. The signature national democratic-socialist proposals — Medicare for All, federal jobs guarantee, Green New Deal, free public college — require federal action under a constitutional system whose Senate, Electoral College, judicial-review architecture, and filibuster were designed to block redistributive majorities. See **Levitsky and Ziblatt**, *Tyranny of the Minority* (2023).
- **Media literacy** is a candidate-specific capacity that has not been built into the infrastructure. There is no national left equivalent of the right-wing media ecosystem Benkler analyzed; *Jacobin* and a handful of podcasts are not it.
- **A degraded establishment** is not reliably present at the state and national level, especially after the Democratic Party's post-2024 reorganization.

## What would have to be built (the literature's answers)

### Party infrastructure

- **Bhaskar Sunkara**, *The Socialist Manifesto: The Case for Radical Politics in an Era of Extreme Inequality* (2019) — the Jacobin-house argument for a "dirty break": work inside the Democratic Party until the conditions exist to form an independent labor party, then leave. The Sanders 2016/2020 model is treated as a transitional vehicle, not the destination.
- **Seth Ackerman**, "A Blueprint for a New Party," *Jacobin* (2016) — the most-cited institutional half-measure: a party-within-a-party with its own membership, discipline, and endorsement process, operating through the Democratic ballot line where necessary but maintaining organizational autonomy.
- **Daniel Schlozman and Sam Rosenfeld**, *The Hollow Parties* (2024) — not a left strategy book but indispensable background; the U.S. two-party system's institutional hollowness creates both the opportunity (parties are capturable) and the constraint (captured parties cannot enforce programmatic discipline).
- **Eric Blanc**, *Revolutionary Social Democracy: Working-Class Politics Across the Russian Empire (1882–1917)* (2024) — historical-comparative. The pre-1917 European social-democratic parties built mass membership, dense local infrastructure, and educational apparatus *before* contesting power, and lost when they tried to skip that step. Read as an implicit critique of the post-2016 American left's electoral focus.

### Labor

- **Jane McAlevey**, *No Shortcuts: Organizing for Power in the New Gilded Age* (2016) — the canonical statement of "deep organizing" over "shallow mobilizing." Identifies why staff-driven advocacy and election cycles do not build durable power, and what does: workplace-by-workplace majority-supermajority organizing among the rank-and-file. McAlevey died in 2024; the book is the field's central reference.
- **McAlevey**, *A Collective Bargain: Unions, Organizing, and the Fight for Democracy* (2020) — the explicitly political extension: democratic-socialist politics without union density at European levels (35%+) is structurally pre-empted from European-style social democracy.
- **Kim Moody**, *On New Terrain: How Capital Is Reshaping the Battleground of Class War* (2017) and *Breaking the Impasse: Electoral Politics, Mass Action, and the New Socialist Movement in the United States* (2022) — the most sustained labor-strategy treatment from the U.S. socialist left. Argues the strategic center has to be class-struggle unionism, with electoral work subordinated to and accountable to it.
- **Hahrie Han**, *How Organizations Develop Activists* (2014) — distinguishes "organizing," "mobilizing," and "lone wolf" modes of civic engagement, with empirical evidence that organizing builds durable capacity and mobilizing does not. The methodological complement to McAlevey.

### Media and ideological infrastructure

- **Yochai Benkler et al.**, *Network Propaganda* (2018) — as on the MAGA page. The right's asymmetric media ecosystem is the model the left does not have. Building one is constrained by audience size, advertiser base, and the fact that the political-economic interests that fund right-wing media have no left equivalent.
- **Vivek Chibber**, *The Class Matrix: Social Theory After the Cultural Turn* (2022) — argues that the academic left's culturalist turn from the 1970s onward stripped it of the materialist vocabulary needed to make a class-based political appeal; the rebuilding of that vocabulary is a prerequisite for the rebuilding of the politics. Chibber is also the editor of *Catalyst*, the strategic-theory journal that has done the most sustained work on these questions.
- **Astra Taylor**, *Democracy May Not Exist, But We'll Miss It When It's Gone* (2019) and her work with the Debt Collective — argues that movements organized around concrete material grievances (debt, housing, healthcare) build constituencies the formal left often cannot reach.

### Electorate

This is the area of sharpest internal disagreement.

- **Matt Karp**, "The Politics of a Second Gilded Age" (*Jacobin*, 2017) and subsequent essays — class-first populist appeal aimed explicitly at non-college voters across racial lines. Treats the rural / exurban third of the electorate as recoverable through material economic appeals.
- **Dustin Guastella**, essays in *Catalyst* and *Damage* — extends the Karp position; argues the urban-coded cultural politics of the post-2020 left actively repels the working-class majority it needs.
- **Chris Maisano**, essays in *Catalyst* and *Jacobin* — similar position, with more attention to the institutional Catholic and evangelical communities the left has written off.
- **The opposing position**, articulated in different registers by Jamelle Bouie (*New York Times* columns), Adam Serwer (*The Atlantic*), and Keeanga-Yamahtta Taylor (*From #BlackLivesMatter to Black Liberation*, 2016), argues that the racial structure of the American electorate makes a class-only appeal politically inert and morally insufficient. The Mamdani 2025 coalition (multi-racial, urban) is one data point in the argument; whether a similar appeal lands in Wisconsin or Pennsylvania is what the disagreement is about.

### Institutional reform

- **Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt**, *Tyranny of the Minority* (2023) — the case for constitutional reform: Electoral College, Senate, judicial appointments, filibuster. Without these, the policy ceiling for a democratic-socialist program is low even after electoral wins.
- **Jacob Grumbach**, *Laboratories Against Democracy* (2022) — state-level evidence that GOP institutional capture has nationalized; symmetric strategy on the left would have to operate at state level.

## The European comparative cases

The systems-frame question for each: what subsystems aligned, what failed, what is the lesson for the U.S.?

- **Corbyn-era British Labour Party (2015–2019).** Two general elections (2017, 2019). 2017 produced the largest vote-share increase for Labour since 1945 and denied the Conservatives a majority; 2019 produced the worst Labour defeat since 1935. The asymmetry — the same leadership, the same broad program, two opposite outcomes — is the case study. Owen Jones, *This Land: The Story of a Movement* (2020), is the sympathetic-insider account; Tom Baldwin's *Keir Starmer: The Biography* (2024) the establishment counter. The Brexit referendum was the single subsystem the Corbyn project could not absorb; party-internal warfare, hostile media (the systemic British press hostility was an order of magnitude beyond what U.S. left candidates face), and intelligence-service leaks are the secondary factors. **Lesson:** national left projects can win seats and shift the discourse without winning power; cross-cutting issues outside the left's framing can collapse the coalition.
- **France Insoumise (2016–present) and Mélenchon.** Three presidential campaigns (2012, 2017, 2022), the last finishing third with 22% and within 1.2 points of the second round. The 2022 NUPES alliance produced a large parliamentary bloc but did not hold; the 2024 New Popular Front prevented a Rassemblement National majority but did not produce a Mélenchon-led government. **Lesson:** in proportional or semi-proportional systems, left blocs can sustain national-scale infrastructure without holding power; the U.S. winner-take-all system structurally precludes the equivalent.
- **Podemos (Spain, 2014–present).** Founded out of the 2011 indignados movement; entered government as junior partner in the 2020 Sánchez coalition. **Lesson:** a movement-party can enter executive power in a multi-party system through coalition; the entry forces concessions that hollow out the movement base, a tension Pablo Iglesias has written about in *Manifesto for the New Left* and elsewhere.
- **Die Linke (Germany, 2007–present).** Formed from the PDS (East German successor party) and the WASG (West German Social Democratic split). Lost about half its vote between 2017 and 2025; was substantially displaced by the Sahra Wagenknecht Bündnis split in 2024. **Lesson:** democratic-socialist parties can be cannibalized by right-populist or "left-conservative" projects that absorb their economic message while jettisoning the cultural one. The U.S. equivalent — a Trumpist party that absorbs left economic demands while keeping the cultural reaction — has been threatened (the Bannonite economic-nationalist faction inside MAGA) but not consummated.
- **Syriza (Greece, 2012–2019).** Won government in 2015 on an anti-austerity platform; capitulated to the Troika within months; lost the next election. **Lesson:** governmental power without monetary or fiscal sovereignty (Greece in the Eurozone, but also U.S. states relative to the federal government, or U.S. cities relative to states) does not produce the policy outcomes the program promised. [[varoufakis-yanis|Yanis Varoufakis]], *Adults in the Room* (2017), is the insider account.
- **Latin American "Pink Tide" (1998–c. 2016).** Chávez (Venezuela), Lula (Brazil), Kirchner (Argentina), Morales (Bolivia), Correa (Ecuador). The longest-running democratic-socialist (or socialist-adjacent) governmental experience in the modern era. Outcomes range from substantial poverty reduction (Brazil under Lula) to catastrophic collapse (Venezuela post-Chávez). The literature is enormous; Steve Ellner (ed.), *Latin America's Pink Tide* (2020), is a recent overview. **Lesson:** dependence on commodity-price cycles for the fiscal base of redistributive programs is a single-point-of-failure; institution-building survives the cycle, transfer programs do not.

## What the systems frame actually predicts

A compressed reading of the literature:

- **Necessary** for U.S. democratic socialism at scale: substantially higher union density (probably 20%+ private sector); a coherent national organizational vehicle with discipline (whether inside or outside the Democratic ballot line); a media ecosystem with sustained audience reach beyond the existing left publications; a policy program that includes federal institutional reform as a precondition, not an afterthought; and a serious answer to the non-urban third of the electorate.
- **Not yet present** in any of the five at adequate scale.
- **The Mamdani win** is consistent with the systems frame: a single subsystem-rich locality produced a result the national infrastructure has not. It is evidence that the model *can* work where the conditions are present, not evidence that the conditions will spontaneously appear elsewhere.
- **The structural argument against scaling** is that the U.S. constitutional order is uniquely hostile to redistributive majorities and that the institutional reform required is itself blocked by the same institutions. **Levitsky and Ziblatt** are explicit about this; **Hacker and Pierson** (*Off Center*, 2005) made the earlier version of the argument.
- **The structural argument for scaling** is that the material conditions — wage stagnation, housing crisis, healthcare costs, climate disruption, generational wealth divide — are intensifying faster than the institutional barriers are hardening, and that a sufficient crisis will create the political space the current infrastructure cannot. This is the position **Sunkara** and **Blanc** take. It is also the position the right-populist literature (Hacker and Pierson on plutocratic populism, Norris and Inglehart on cultural backlash) suggests is being filled, in the absence of an equivalent left, by the right.

The honest summary is that the literature does not converge. The strategic question is open and being decided in real time, with Mamdani's mayoralty as one of the live experiments and the 2026 and 2028 cycles as the next data points.
