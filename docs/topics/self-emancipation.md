# Self-Emancipation and the Black Struggle to End Slavery

The "self-emancipation" thesis holds that enslaved and free Black people were active agents in the destruction of slavery rather than its passive beneficiaries — that the flight of enslaved people to Union lines, their labor and military service, and decades of Black abolitionist organizing made emancipation a political and military necessity and pushed federal policy further than its leaders initially intended. In this account Abraham Lincoln's hand was forced, by events on the ground as much as by his own convictions. The thesis originates with [[du-bois-web|W.E.B. Du Bois]]'s *Black Reconstruction in America* (1935) and was revived by the historians of the Freedmen and Southern Society Project in the 1980s and 1990s.

The thesis is contested. Critics argue that it understates the role of the Union army, of antislavery constitutionalism, and of Lincoln himself, and that without federal arms and Republican policy the flight of enslaved people could not have abolished slavery nationally. The reading list below is organized to make that argument legible from both sides, and then to set it inside the longer history of Black abolitionism that preceded the war.

## The foundational statement

- **[[du-bois-web|W.E.B. Du Bois]]**, *Black Reconstruction in America* (1935) — Chapter 4, "The General Strike," argues that the mass withdrawal of enslaved labor to Union lines functioned as a general strike that crippled the Confederacy and forced emancipation. The headwater of the entire argument; ignored for decades, now treated as foundational.
- **[[du-bois-web|W.E.B. Du Bois]]**, *John Brown* (1909) — Du Bois's biography of Brown, read by many as an earlier statement of his conviction that slavery would be ended by force from below.

## The modern self-emancipation historians

- **Ira Berlin**, *Slaves No More: Three Essays on Emancipation and the Civil War* (1992) — the sharpest modern formulation, growing out of the Freedmen and Southern Society Project Berlin directed. The essay "The Slaves' War" makes the case that enslaved people initiated emancipation by their own actions.
- **Ira Berlin et al.**, *Freedom: A Documentary History of Emancipation, 1861–1867* (multiple volumes, 1982– ) — the documentary foundation, assembling enslaved people's own wartime actions from the federal archives.
- **David Williams**, *I Freed Myself: African American Self-Emancipation in the Civil War Era* (2014) — the most direct book-length defense of the thesis for a general reader.
- **Steven Hahn**, *A Nation under Our Feet: Black Political Struggles in the Rural South from Slavery to the Great Migration* (2003), Pulitzer winner, and the essays in *The Political Worlds of Slavery and Freedom* (2009) — frames wartime flight as the largest slave rebellion in modern history.
- **Glenn David Brasher**, *The Peninsula Campaign and the Necessity of Emancipation* (2012) — argues that the labor and intelligence enslaved people provided the Union army made emancipation a military necessity.
- **Chandra Manning**, *Troubled Refuge: Struggling for Freedom in the Civil War* (2016) — the "contrabands" who reached Union lines and the camps they built.

## The historiographical debate

- **James Oakes**, *Freedom National: The Destruction of Slavery in the United States, 1861–1865* (2012) — argues that antislavery politics and constitutionalism, not slave flight alone, destroyed slavery; a deliberate corrective to a pure self-emancipation reading.
- **[[foner-eric|Eric Foner]]**, *The Fiery Trial: Abraham Lincoln and American Slavery* (2010), Pulitzer winner — tracks Lincoln's evolution under wartime and political pressure; a middle position that credits both Lincoln and the forces acting on him.
- **James McPherson**, "Who Freed the Slaves?", in *Drawn with the Sword: Reflections on the American Civil War* (1996) — disputes Berlin directly, insisting that Lincoln's leadership and Union arms were decisive. Read alongside Berlin, this is the cleanest way to see the dispute; Barbara Fields states the self-emancipation side in Ken Burns's documentary *The Civil War* (1990).

## Black abolitionism before the war

- **Manisha Sinha**, *The Slave's Cause: A History of Abolition* (2016) — centers Black abolitionists, enslaved and free, as the engine of the movement rather than its junior partners.
- **Kellie Carter Jackson**, *Force and Freedom: Black Abolitionists and the Politics of Violence* (2019) — recovers the wing of Black abolitionism that defended the use of force.
- **Vincent Harding**, *There Is a River: The Black Struggle for Freedom in America* (1981) — the long arc of the freedom struggle written from within the Black radical tradition.
- **Benjamin Quarles**, *Black Abolitionists* (1969) and *The Negro in the Civil War* (1953) — the pioneering studies that recovered Black agency decades before it became standard.
- **[[douglass-frederick|Frederick Douglass]]**, *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass* (1845) and *My Bondage and My Freedom* (1855) — the central first-person antebellum texts; Douglass's own life as the argument that the enslaved would not wait to be freed.

## Where to start

Berlin's *Slaves No More* is the concise thesis statement; Williams's *I Freed Myself* is the accessible narrative; Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction* is the source the whole debate is arguing with. Reading Oakes or McPherson against them supplies the counter-case.
