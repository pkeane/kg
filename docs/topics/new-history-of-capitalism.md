# The New History of Capitalism

The historiographical movement that, beginning in the 2000s and accelerating through the 2010s, fundamentally reshaped how American historians understand the relationship between capitalism, slavery, and the wealth of the nineteenth-century United States. The basic claim is that the American industrial economy — the railroads, the textile mills, the New York banks, the Northern manufacturing capital — was not built *despite* slavery, with slavery a moral stain on an otherwise free-labor economy. It was built *through* slavery, financed by the profits of slave-produced cotton, organized in part by accounting and management techniques pioneered on the plantation, and entangled at every level with the Atlantic slave trade and its successor systems of coerced labor. The American historical profession can no longer separate "the peculiar institution" from "American economic development" the way it routinely did before about 2000.

The school is not an ideology but a set of overlapping research programs centered on archival empirical work. Its origins lie in the rediscovery of [[du-bois-web|W.E.B. Du Bois]]'s *Black Reconstruction* (1935) — which made the core argument generations earlier — combined with Eric Williams's *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944), [[robinson-cedric|Cedric Robinson]]'s *Black Marxism* (1983), and the broader global-history turn associated with Kenneth Pomeranz, Immanuel Wallerstein's world-systems analysis, and the British Marxist historians. Methodologically the school combines deep work in plantation records, merchant ledgers, customs houses, and bank archives with a comparative-historical sensibility that follows commodity chains across continents. The result is empirically dense, hard to dismiss, and, where it succeeds, definitive.

The school has critics. Economic historians have contested specific empirical claims (the Olmstead–Rhode critique of Edward Baptist's productivity figures is the best-known case), and earlier scholarly traditions have pushed back on what they see as overreach. The debate has been productive — it has refined rather than overturned the school's broader claims, and the books are now standard graduate-program reading in American history.

## Annotated bibliography

### Foundational ancestors

- **[[du-bois-web|W.E.B. Du Bois]]**, *Black Reconstruction in America* (1935) — the prophetic ancestor. Du Bois's claim that Black labor, enslaved and free, built American capitalism — and that Reconstruction was destroyed because the Northern bourgeoisie ultimately preferred sectional reconciliation with white supremacist capital to interracial democracy — anticipated almost everything the New History of Capitalism would rediscover archive by archive eighty years later. The single most important book to read before reading any of the figures below.
- **Eric Williams**, *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944) — Trinidadian historian (later Prime Minister of Trinidad and Tobago) whose Oxford doctoral thesis argued that the British Industrial Revolution was financed by the profits of Caribbean slavery, and that British abolitionism, when it came, was driven by economic interest as much as by moral conscience. Long marginalized in mainstream British historiography; now widely read again.
- **[[robinson-cedric|Cedric Robinson]]**, *Black Marxism: The Making of the Black Radical Tradition* (1983) — the theoretical articulation. Robinson coined the term [[racial-capitalism|*racial capitalism*]] and argued that capitalism was racial from its origins, and that the racialization of labor was constitutive rather than incidental. The conceptual frame within which much of the New History of Capitalism operates, even when it doesn't cite him directly.

### The core scholarship

- **[[beckert-sven|Sven Beckert]]**, *Empire of Cotton: A Global History* (2014) — the breakthrough work that brought the school to wide academic and public attention. Cotton as the thread tracing plantation, factory, and peasantry across centuries and continents; *war capitalism* as the analytic frame for the violent first phase of capitalism's history. Won the Bancroft Prize.
- **Edward Baptist**, *The Half Has Never Been Told: Slavery and the Making of American Capitalism* (2014) — the most polemical of the major books and the one that drew the sharpest counter-critique. Baptist argues that the rising productivity of American slavery in the antebellum decades was driven by systematized torture (the "pushing system") rather than by capital improvements or land quality, and that this slave-produced cotton drove American industrial growth. The argument is contested in its empirical specifics (see Olmstead and Rhode below) but its broader thesis has held.
- **Walter Johnson**, *River of Dark Dreams: Slavery and Empire in the Cotton Kingdom* (2013) — a sweeping account of the Mississippi Valley cotton economy as an imperial project that pulled global trade, ecology, settler conquest, and slavery into a single system. Johnson's earlier *Soul by Soul: Life Inside the Antebellum Slave Market* (1999) is also essential reading. Both are deeply researched and stylistically distinctive.
- **Seth Rockman**, *Scraping By: Wage Labor, Slavery, and Survival in Early Baltimore* (2009) — a precise local study of how the wage-labor and slave-labor economies were intertwined in a single American city. The book that demonstrated the school's methodological rigor at the urban scale.
- **Caitlin Rosenthal**, *Accounting for Slavery: Masters and Management* (2018) — the argument that modern management techniques — depreciation schedules, productivity measurement, scientific scheduling of human labor — were pioneered on slave plantations rather than in nineteenth-century factories. Reframes the "scientific management" tradition as having a slaveholding genealogy.
- **Christine Desan**, *Making Money: Coin, Currency, and the Coming of Capitalism* (2014) — the legal-historical companion. Desan, Beckert's frequent collaborator, argues that money itself is a constitutional and legal construction rather than a spontaneous market emergence — a foundational claim for the school's broader case that capitalism is politically built, not naturally occurring.

### The synthetic statements

- **[[beckert-sven|Sven Beckert]]** and **Christine Desan**, eds., *American Capitalism: New Histories* (2018) — the programmatic edited volume; the school's clearest single statement of its agenda.
- **[[beckert-sven|Sven Beckert]]**, *Capitalism: A Global History* (2025) — the most recent and most ambitious synthesis; over a thousand pages extending the war-capitalism / industrial-capitalism framework worldwide and arguing that capitalism was born global rather than European.

### Critics and the methodological debate

- **Alan Olmstead** and **Paul Rhode**, "Cotton, Slavery, and the New History of Capitalism" (*Explorations in Economic History*, 2018) — the most influential empirical pushback, contesting Baptist's productivity figures and arguing that improved cotton varieties, not torture, account for much of the antebellum productivity gain. The debate has been productive and has refined rather than overturned the school's broader claims.
- **Eric Hilt**, "Economic History, Historical Analysis, and the New History of Capitalism" — a critical assessment from inside economic history, arguing that the New History of Capitalism sometimes underestimates how much economic historians had already established and overstates the novelty of its claims.
