# What Should Jimmy Carter Have Done Instead?

A common periodization places the U.S. turn toward [[neoliberalism]] with Reagan in 1981. A revisionist scholarly literature that took shape in the 2000s and consolidated after 2010 places the turn one administration earlier, under Jimmy Carter (1977–1981). On this account the major deregulatory statutes — the Airline Deregulation Act (1978), the Natural Gas Policy Act (1978), the Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act (March 1980), the Motor Carrier Act (July 1980), the Staggers Rail Act (October 1980) — were Carter-era laws, signed by a Democratic president and in several cases championed by Democratic senators (notably Ted Kennedy on airlines and trucking). The decisive appointment of Paul Volcker to the Federal Reserve in August 1979, and the "Volcker shock" of October 6, 1979, were Carter's. The Labor Law Reform Act, which would have made union organizing meaningfully easier and which the AFL-CIO treated as its central legislative ask, was defeated by filibuster in June 1978 with the Carter White House offering only limited help. Judith Stein's *Pivotal Decade* (Yale, 2010) is the standard scholarly statement of this revisionist periodization; Gary Gerstle's *The Rise and Fall of the Neoliberal Order* (Oxford, 2022) treats Carter and Reagan as joint architects of what Gerstle calls the "neoliberal order."

The historiography divides on the *why*. One cluster of explanations stresses objective economic constraint: the simultaneous high unemployment and high inflation ("stagflation") of the 1970s that Keynesian demand management could not address, the productivity slowdown that began around 1973, two oil shocks (1973 and 1979), the collapse of the Bretton Woods fixed-exchange-rate system in 1971, and the falling rate of profit in U.S. manufacturing (Robert Brenner, *The Economics of Global Turbulence*, Verso, 2006). On this reading Carter inherited a crisis the postwar policy toolkit could not solve and improvised. A second cluster — Stein, Jefferson Cowie, Thomas Frank, Matt Stoller — stresses contingent political choices: an administration staffed with fiscally conservative Democrats (Treasury Secretary Michael Blumenthal, CEA chair Charles Schultze, domestic policy adviser Stuart Eizenstat, OMB director James McIntyre) that chose to fight inflation rather than unemployment, that broke with organized labor on the issues labor cared most about, and that accepted advisers (Alfred Kahn at CAB, then as "inflation czar") whose framework was already post-Keynesian. A third cluster — David Harvey, Greta Krippner — treats the Carter shift as one expression of a deeper class restructuring driven by the search for new fields of capital accumulation as the postwar industrial model exhausted itself.

The "what should he have done instead" framing is not strictly historians' work — counterfactuals are not how academic history is usually written — but it is the question that animates much of the contemporary post-neoliberal literature that looks back at the period. The standard candidates for a road not taken: a serious U.S. industrial policy along the lines later argued for by Robert Reich and Lester Thurow; a defense of the New Deal anti-monopoly tradition rather than its dismantling (the position Stoller defends in *Goliath*); national health insurance on the Kennedy plan; a labor-law reform that would have rebuilt union density; a more aggressive energy transition built on Carter's own (more ambitious than what passed) National Energy Plan; a different Federal Reserve chair, or different instructions to Volcker; capital controls and a coordinated international response rather than unilateral interest-rate shock. None of these counterfactuals has consensus support; each is defended in identifiable books.

The annotated bibliography below is organized around the question rather than by author. It is heavily American and heavily oriented toward the post-2008 wave of writing that reread the 1970s once the financial crisis discredited the neoliberal synthesis. Carter himself, his domestic-policy adviser Eizenstat, and his standard biographers are listed under sympathetic accounts; the bulk of the scholarship is broadly critical.

## Annotated bibliography

### The revisionist periodization: "it started with Carter"

- **Judith Stein**, *Pivotal Decade: How the United States Traded Factories for Finance in the Seventies* (Yale, 2010) — the central book. Argues that the Carter administration made a series of fateful choices — fighting inflation over unemployment, abandoning manufacturing to imports, deregulating finance and transport, declining to push hard for Labor Law Reform — that together broke the postwar social contract before Reagan arrived. The Volcker appointment is treated as the decisive moment. Stein is a labor historian and the book is written from that vantage.
- **Jefferson Cowie**, *Stayin' Alive: The 1970s and the Last Days of the Working Class* (New Press, 2010) — the cultural-political companion to Stein. Documents the Democratic Party's break with its labor base across the 1970s, with the defeat of the Labor Law Reform Act (1978) and the Humphrey-Hawkins compromise as inflection points. Argues the white working class did not so much leave the Democrats as get left by them.
- **Bruce J. Schulman**, *The Seventies: The Great Shift in American Culture, Society, and Politics* (Free Press, 2001) — the earlier general history that frames the decade as the underrated hinge of postwar America. Less focused on Carter specifically but the indispensable wide-angle context.
- **Gary Gerstle**, *The Rise and Fall of the Neoliberal Order: America and the World in the Free Market Era* (Oxford, 2022) — periodizes the neoliberal order from roughly 1980 (giving Carter the late-1970s setup role) to its 2016–2020 collapse. Gerstle treats orders as bipartisan formations, so Carter–Reagan and Clinton–Bush are read symmetrically.
- **Daniel T. Rodgers**, *Age of Fracture* (Belknap/Harvard, 2011) — intellectual history of the dissolution of postwar social vocabularies (the "social," the "structure," the "collective") into market and choice metaphors from roughly 1975 onward. The background ideational shift the Carter administration was operating within.
- **Lily Geismer**, *Left Behind: The Democrats' Failed Attempt to Solve Inequality* (PublicAffairs, 2022) — focuses on the Clinton-era "New Democrat" continuation, but reads the lineage back through Carter. Useful for seeing the Carter pivot as the opening, not the climax, of a long Democratic accommodation to market governance.

### The economic constraints Carter faced

- **Meg Jacobs**, *Panic at the Pump: The Energy Crisis and the Transformation of American Politics in the 1970s* (Hill & Wang, 2016) — the standard history of the 1973 and 1979 oil shocks as drivers of policy change, including Carter's energy plan, deregulation of natural gas, and the political collapse around gasoline lines in summer 1979. Argues that the energy crisis structurally weakened the postwar regulatory order in ways no administration could have ignored.
- **Robert Brenner**, *The Economics of Global Turbulence* (Verso, 2006) — long Marxist account of the post-1973 fall in manufacturing profitability across the OECD, treated as the deep cause of the policy turn rather than a contingent ideological choice. The strongest statement of the "Carter had no alternative" thesis from the left.
- **Greta R. Krippner**, *Capitalizing on Crisis: The Political Origins of the Rise of Finance* (Harvard, 2011) — argues that the financialization of the U.S. economy was not the product of a coherent neoliberal plan but a series of ad hoc policy improvisations — including the Carter-era removal of interest-rate ceilings (DIDMCA 1980) and the abandonment of credit controls — that allowed the state to displace distributional conflicts onto the financial system.
- **Marc Levinson**, *An Extraordinary Time: The End of the Postwar Boom and the Return of the Ordinary Economy* (Basic, 2016) — argues that the productivity boom of 1948–1973 was the historical anomaly and that the slowdown beginning in 1973 was a return to normal, against which all 1970s administrations were going to look like failures.

### The deregulation acts and who pushed them

- **Matt Stoller**, *Goliath: The 100-Year War Between Monopoly Power and Democracy* (Simon & Schuster, 2019) — chapters on the late 1970s argue that airline and trucking deregulation broke the New Deal anti-monopoly tradition that had been the institutional core of Democratic political economy since the 1930s. Hardest on Ted Kennedy, Stephen Breyer (then Kennedy's Judiciary staffer), and Alfred Kahn for converting a left antimonopoly critique of the captured agencies into a deregulation that empowered concentrated capital. The clearest statement of the counterfactual that the Democrats had an alternative tradition available and chose not to use it.
- **Thomas K. McCraw**, *Prophets of Regulation* (Belknap/Harvard, 1984) — chapter on Alfred Kahn (CAB chair under Carter) is the standard account of how a Cornell economist of broadly Keynesian formation came to dismantle airline economic regulation in the name of consumer welfare. Important because it shows Kahn was not a Chicago-school neoliberal but a New Deal liberal who concluded the existing regulation no longer served its original purposes.
- **Paul Sabin**, "Environmental Law and the End of the New Deal Order," *Law and History Review* 33 (2015) — argues that public-interest litigation in the 1970s, much of it from the Nader-adjacent left, contributed to the delegitimation of the captured regulatory agencies that deregulation then dismantled.
- **Reuel Schiller**, *Forging Rivals: Race, Class, Law, and the Collapse of Postwar Liberalism* (Cambridge, 2015) — institutional account of how the labor-civil-rights coalition that anchored postwar Democratic political economy came apart in the 1970s, with regulatory politics as one of the breaking points.

### The Volcker shock and the abandonment of full employment

- **William Greider**, *Secrets of the Temple: How the Federal Reserve Runs the Country* (Simon & Schuster, 1987) — the canonical journalistic history of Volcker's tenure. Detailed on the October 6, 1979 "Saturday Night Special," the resulting 1981–82 recession, and the political class's tacit acceptance that 7–8% unemployment was an acceptable price for breaking inflation expectations.
- **William L. Silber**, *Volcker: The Triumph of Persistence* (Bloomsbury, 2012) — sympathetic biography. Useful for understanding what Volcker himself believed he was doing and how Carter's Treasury (G. William Miller) and the administration as a whole understood the appointment.
- **Robert Pollin**, *Contours of Descent: U.S. Economic Fractures and the Landscape of Global Austerity* (Verso, 2003) — post-Keynesian critique of the Volcker disinflation as the foundational event of the neoliberal U.S. macroeconomy. Argues full-employment commitment was the actual casualty of 1979–82.
- **L. Randall Wray**, "The Rise and Fall of Money Manager Capitalism: A Minskian Approach," *Cambridge Journal of Economics* 33 (2009) — Minskian framework for reading the post-1979 financial transformation as the unintended consequence of deregulation-plus-disinflation.

### The break with organized labor

- **Joseph A. McCartin**, *Collision Course: Ronald Reagan, the Air Traffic Controllers, and the Strike that Changed America* (Oxford, 2011) — the strike came in August 1981 under Reagan but is read here as the climax of a sequence that began with Carter's failure to deliver on Labor Law Reform in 1978. PATCO had endorsed Carter in 1980 and then Reagan; the book explains why.
- **Kim Phillips-Fein**, *Invisible Hands: The Businessmen's Crusade Against the New Deal* (Norton, 2009) — the corporate counter-mobilization (Business Roundtable founded 1972, Chamber of Commerce reorganization, Powell Memo 1971) that made labor-law reform unpassable by 1978. Carter was governing into an organized business resistance that postwar Democrats had not faced.
- **Nelson Lichtenstein**, *State of the Union: A Century of American Labor* (Princeton, 2002; rev. 2013) — long-arc labor history that places the late 1970s as the inflection point at which union density entered terminal decline. The chapter on the Carter years is direct on the missed opportunity.

### The Kennedy challenge and the intra-party road not taken

- **Timothy Stanley**, *Kennedy vs. Carter: The 1980 Battle for the Democratic Party's Soul* (University Press of Kansas, 2010) — the standard account of Ted Kennedy's 1980 primary challenge. Frames it as the last serious attempt to push the Democratic Party back toward its New Deal–Great Society lineage and treats Kennedy's defeat as a major event in the longer pivot.
- **Patrick Andelic**, *Donkey Work: Congressional Democrats in Conservative America, 1974–1994* (University Press of Kansas, 2019) — congressional history of the same period; argues that the Democratic congressional caucus had already split between northern liberals, southern conservatives, and a new Atari/neoliberal cohort (Gary Hart, Paul Tsongas, Tim Wirth) by the late 1970s, constraining Carter's room to govern from the left.
- **Edward M. Kennedy**, address to the 1980 Democratic National Convention ("The Dream Shall Never Die," August 12, 1980) — the rhetorical statement of the alternative the party did not take. Read alongside Stanley.

### Carter's break, in his own terms, and sympathetic accounts

- **Stuart E. Eizenstat**, *President Carter: The White House Years* (Thomas Dunne / St. Martin's, 2018) — at over 900 pages the definitive insider account, by the man who ran Carter's domestic policy. Defends most of the deregulation as overdue anti-cartel reform and defends Carter's economic team against the Stein/Cowie critique. Indispensable counterweight.
- **Burton I. Kaufman and Scott Kaufman**, *The Presidency of James Earl Carter, Jr.* (University Press of Kansas, 1993; rev. 2006) — standard scholarly biography in the Lawrence University Press presidential series. Measured rather than sympathetic; useful as a baseline.
- **Jimmy Carter**, *Keeping Faith: Memoirs of a President* (Bantam, 1982) — Carter's own account. Notable for what it does and does not treat as central: the deregulatory acts get less space than Camp David and the Iran hostages.
- **Kevin Mattson**, *"What the Heck Are You Up To, Mr. President?": Jimmy Carter, America's "Malaise," and the Speech That Should Have Changed the Country* (Bloomsbury, 2009) — book-length treatment of the July 15, 1979 "Crisis of Confidence" speech. Argues the speech itself was politically successful (initial polling rose) and that the subsequent cabinet purge a week later destroyed its credibility. Sympathetic to Carter as a moralist of limits in a culture that punished moralists of limits.
- **Leo P. Ribuffo**, *His Truth Is Marching On: Jimmy Carter and the Culture of the 1970s* (University of Kansas, forthcoming/posthumous; portions in earlier essays) — Ribuffo, the leading academic historian of the religious right, was working on a major Carter biography until his death in 2018. The published essays argue that Carter's evangelical-progressive framework had no political constituency by the late 1970s.

### The counterfactuals: what an alternative would have looked like

- **Robert B. Reich**, *The Next American Frontier* (Times Books, 1983) — published just after the Carter years, this is the founding text of the "industrial policy" alternative that the Atari Democrats and later the Roosevelt Institute tradition would develop. Argues that the U.S. needed an active national strategy for high-value manufacturing rather than acceptance of deindustrialization. Read as the policy Carter could plausibly have adopted and did not.
- **Lester C. Thurow**, *The Zero-Sum Society: Distribution and the Possibilities for Economic Change* (Basic, 1980) — the other major industrial-policy text of the immediate post-Carter moment.
- **Robert Kuttner**, *Revolt of the Haves: Tax Rebellions and Hard Times* (Simon & Schuster, 1980) — contemporaneous critique of the Proposition 13 tax revolt and the Carter administration's failure to mount a defense of the public sector. Kuttner has been writing this argument for forty years; *Going Big: FDR's Legacy, Biden's New Deal, and the Struggle to Save Democracy* (New Press, 2022) is the late-career restatement that explicitly contrasts Biden's industrial-policy turn with what Carter could have done.
- **Mike Konczal**, *Freedom From the Market: America's Fight to Liberate Itself from the Grip of the Invisible Hand* (New Press, 2021) — recovers the American republican-democratic tradition of decommodification (free public land, free public education, free public libraries, the Postal Savings System, the New Deal) as the resource that Carter-era Democrats could have drawn on instead of the consumer-welfare frame they adopted from the antitrust academy.
- **Brent Cebul**, *Illusions of Progress: Business, Poverty, and Liberalism in the American Century* (University of Pennsylvania, 2023) — argues that the Democratic Party's reliance on public-private partnerships from the 1940s onward had already hollowed out the institutional capacity that a 1970s-counterfactual industrial policy would have required. Sobering on the counterfactual.
- **N. D. B. Connolly et al.**, *Beyond the New Deal Order*, Gary Gerstle, Nelson Lichtenstein, and Alice O'Connor, eds. (University of Pennsylvania, 2019) — essay collection rereading the New Deal political coalition through the lens of what came after. Several essays bear directly on the Carter pivot.

### The broader neoliberal turn (international and intellectual context)

- **David Harvey**, *A Brief History of Neoliberalism* (Oxford, 2005) — places the Carter–Reagan–Thatcher sequence within a class-power framework: neoliberalism as the restoration of capital's share after the 1970s profit squeeze. The widely-cited macro-thesis against which the Stein/Cowie domestic-politics histories are sometimes contrasted, sometimes integrated.
- **Quinn Slobodian**, *Globalists: The End of Empire and the Birth of Neoliberalism* (Harvard, 2018) — intellectual history of the Geneva-school neoliberals (Hayek, Röpke, Haberler, Petersmann) and the project of insulating global market order from democratic politics through international institutions. Background for understanding the international constraints Carter perceived himself to be operating under.
- **Daniel Stedman Jones**, *Masters of the Universe: Hayek, Friedman, and the Birth of Neoliberal Politics* (Princeton, 2012) — political-intellectual history of how neoliberal ideas moved from think tanks (AEI, Heritage, Cato) into policy circles in the late 1970s.
- **Angus Burgin**, *The Great Persuasion: Reinventing Free Markets since the Depression* (Harvard, 2012) — Mont Pèlerin and the longer intellectual history.
- **Wolfgang Streeck**, *Buying Time: The Delayed Crisis of Democratic Capitalism* (Verso, 2014) — places the late 1970s policy turn (across OECD) as the moment at which states began to finance their commitments through inflation, then through public debt, then through private debt — Streeck's "three phases" of how rich democracies have postponed the conflict between democracy and capitalism. The Volcker shock is the pivot.
- **Mark Blyth**, *Austerity: The History of a Dangerous Idea* (Oxford, 2013) — intellectual history of the austerity argument that became hegemonic after 1979.
- **Fred Block and Margaret R. Somers**, *The Power of Market Fundamentalism: Karl Polanyi's Critique* (Harvard, 2014) — Polanyian framework for reading the Carter-era turn as a "great transformation" in reverse.

### What we can learn now: the post-2016 / post-2020 looking-back literature

- **Thomas Frank**, *Listen, Liberal: Or, What Ever Happened to the Party of the People?* (Metropolitan, 2016) — polemical history of the Democratic Party's professional-class capture, traced to the late-1970s break with labor. The most-read popular statement of the Stein/Cowie thesis.
- **Felicia Wong and Mike Konczal**, *New Rules for the 21st Century: Corporate Power, Public Power, and the Future of the American Economy* (Roosevelt Institute, 2019) — programmatic statement of the post-neoliberal turn in mainstream Democratic policy thinking. Implicit Carter counterfactual throughout.
- **Mehrsa Baradaran**, *The Color of Money: Black Banks and the Racial Wealth Gap* (Belknap/Harvard, 2017) — read alongside the deregulation literature for the differential racial consequences of the late-1970s financialization.
- **Helen Thompson**, *Disorder: Hard Times in the 21st Century* (Oxford, 2022) — argues that the energy, monetary, and democratic crises of the 2010s and 2020s descend directly from unresolved 1970s shifts, of which the Carter-Volcker sequence is the U.S. component.
- **Adam Tooze**, *Crashed: How a Decade of Financial Crises Changed the World* (Viking, 2018) — places the post-2008 trajectory as the unwinding of the financial order that the Carter-era deregulation set in motion.
- **Eric Levitz**, ongoing writing in *New York* magazine and now *Vox*, on the post-neoliberal turn in Democratic policy thinking — useful for tracking the current intra-Democratic argument about how much of the Carter inheritance should be repudiated.
