# Post-2008 Debates in Economics

The companion topic to [[2008-financial-crisis-and-fallout]] covers the political economy of the crisis and its aftermath — bailouts, foreclosures, Tea Party, Occupy, the road to Trump. This topic covers the parallel and partly overlapping debate *inside economics* over what the crisis revealed about the discipline itself. The collapse of September 2008 had not been forecast or modeled by the dominant pre-crisis macroeconomic framework — the dynamic stochastic general equilibrium (DSGE) tradition descended from Robert Lucas, Edward Prescott, and the New Classical school — and the policy response was carried out using older Keynesian tools that mainstream graduate training had treated as superseded for thirty years. The post-mortem reactivated a series of disputes that had been quiet since the 1970s and 1980s: New Keynesian against New Classical, mainstream Keynesian against post-Keynesian, mainstream economics against Marxist and other heterodox traditions.

The main fault lines: the "saltwater–freshwater" methodological dispute (Paul Krugman and Brad DeLong against Robert Lucas, John Cochrane, and Eugene Fama) over whether the crisis demonstrated the failure of the rational-expectations program; the stimulus-size debate (Christina Romer, Krugman, DeLong-Summers against the political constraint that produced the $787 billion American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009); the household-debt-and-housing failure thesis (Atif Mian and Amir Sufi's *House of Debt*); the post-Keynesian revival around Hyman Minsky's financial-instability hypothesis (L. Randall Wray, Steve Keen); the Modern Monetary Theory current that emerged from post-Keynesian and chartalist roots into mainstream policy debate (Wray, Stephanie Kelton, Pavlina Tcherneva); the Marx revival in popular and academic culture (David Harvey, Richard Wolff, *Jacobin*) and the internal heterodox counter-argument that the revival was textualist and unserious (Mike Beggs's 2011 *Jacobin* essay "Zombie Marx"); Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* and the return of distribution as a central macro question; and the secular-stagnation hypothesis revived by Lawrence Summers in 2013 as a framework for understanding the slow recovery.

A specific subsidiary dispute runs through several of these strands: the question of method. [[robinson-joan|Joan Robinson]]'s 1953 "Open Letter from a Keynesian to a Marxist" — recirculated by Brad DeLong in the late-2020s — argued that to understand Marx one had to use him as an analytical apparatus rather than as scripture: "in your bones," not "in your mouth." The 2026 reissue is not nostalgic; it is positioned in an ongoing argument with academic Marxists whose engagement with the post-2008 economy DeLong regards as text-worshipping rather than analytic. Mike Beggs's 2011 "Zombie Marx" had made the same argument from inside the heterodox left a decade earlier. The methodological dispute — what does it mean to "work with" Keynes, Marx, or Minsky rather than to quote them — is woven through the substantive disputes below.

A note on framing. The substantive policy claim that ran through the New Keynesian side of the debate — that fiscal expansion in a liquidity trap raises output, that the 2009 stimulus was insufficient, that the 2010–11 pivot to austerity caused durable damage via hysteresis — is now broadly accepted in the mainstream macro literature; Olivier Blanchard's writings as IMF chief economist after 2010 and his 2010 paper "Rethinking Macroeconomic Policy" mark the institutional acceptance. The methodological claim — that DSGE macro is structurally unable to model what the 2008 crisis was — remains live. Paul Romer's 2016 paper "The Trouble with Macroeconomics" is the sharpest in-house statement; it has not produced a paradigm change.

## Annotated bibliography

### The saltwater–freshwater dispute

- **Paul Krugman**, "How Did Economists Get It So Wrong?" *New York Times Magazine* (September 2, 2009) — the opening salvo, written from the saltwater (New Keynesian, coastal-university) side. Argues that the freshwater school (Chicago, Minnesota, Rochester) mistook mathematical elegance for empirical truth and lost the capacity to think about depressions. The article that defined the public terms of the post-2008 macro dispute.
- **John H. Cochrane**, "How did Paul Krugman get it so wrong?" (University of Chicago Booth, September 2009; widely circulated as PDF) — the canonical freshwater response. Defends the rational-expectations program and the efficient-markets hypothesis; charges Krugman with caricature. The exchange is the central primary document of the dispute.
- **Robert E. Lucas Jr.**, "In Defence of the Dismal Science," *The Economist* (August 6, 2009) — the brief Lucas statement of the freshwater position: it is in the nature of the efficient-markets hypothesis that crises cannot be predicted, so the failure to predict is not a failure of the theory.
- **Brad DeLong**, *Grasping Reality* blog (2008–present) — the running saltwater commentary; DeLong's posts and the comment threads constitute one of the largest archives of the dispute. His 2017 paper with J. Bradford DeLong and Lawrence Summers, "Concrete Economics," and his 2022 book *Slouching Towards Utopia* extend the argument into book form.
- **Ricardo J. Caballero**, "Macroeconomics after the Crisis: Time to Deal with the Pretense-of-Knowledge Syndrome," *Journal of Economic Perspectives* 24 (Fall 2010) — MIT macroeconomist's in-house critique; argues the discipline mistook internal coherence for empirical relevance.
- **Paul M. Romer**, "The Trouble with Macroeconomics" (working paper, September 2016) — the most prominent in-house indictment of DSGE macro from a Nobel-tier economist (Romer received the prize in 2018). Argues that the field has retreated into "mathiness" and identification strategies that paper over deep theoretical problems. The post-2016 reference for the methodological critique.
- **Olivier Blanchard**, "Do DSGE Models Have a Future?" Peterson Institute (August 2016) — the qualified defense from inside the mainstream; concedes most of Romer's critique while arguing the framework is reformable.

### The stimulus debate and the case for a larger response

- **Christina D. Romer and Jared Bernstein**, "The Job Impact of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Plan" (Council of Economic Advisers, January 9, 2009) — the public Obama transition memo that projected unemployment peaking at 8% with the proposed stimulus. Unemployment in fact peaked at 10% in October 2009. The document at the center of every later debate about the adequacy of the 2009 response.
- **Christina D. Romer**, "The Case for Fiscal Stimulus: The Likely Effects of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act" (March 2009), and her later "Lessons from the New Deal" and 2011 *American Economic Review* lectures — the CEA chair's contemporaneous and retrospective writings. Read in the post-2010 literature as confirmation that the response was knowingly inadequate.
- **Noam Scheiber**, *The Escape Artists: How Obama's Team Fumbled the Recovery* (Simon & Schuster, 2012) — the political-journalism reconstruction of the internal Romer–Summers–Geithner debates. Documents Larry Summers's role in narrowing Romer's larger stimulus options before they reached the President.
- **J. Bradford DeLong and Lawrence H. Summers**, "Fiscal Policy in a Depressed Economy," *Brookings Papers on Economic Activity* (Spring 2012) — the central theoretical statement of the case for sustained fiscal expansion in a liquidity trap. The hysteresis argument: in a depression, fiscal contraction does durable damage to potential output, so the effective long-run multiplier is much larger than the standard literature assumed.
- **Olivier J. Blanchard and Daniel Leigh**, "Growth Forecast Errors and Fiscal Multipliers" (IMF Working Paper, January 2013) — the empirical paper showing that the IMF's 2010–12 forecasts systematically underestimated the contractionary effects of European austerity. The institutional document marking the IMF's pivot away from the pre-2010 austerity consensus.
- **Laurence Ball, Brad DeLong, and Lawrence Summers**, "Fiscal Policy and Full Employment" (Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, April 2014) — the formalized hysteresis case, post-Brookings.
- **Robert J. Shiller**, *The Subprime Solution: How Today's Global Financial Crisis Happened, and What to Do about It* (Princeton, 2008) — early call for direct mortgage-relief intervention from the Yale behavioral-finance economist; foreshadowed the Mian-Sufi critique.
- **Joseph E. Stiglitz**, *Freefall* (Norton, 2010) — the Nobelist's contemporaneous brief against the Bush-Obama response; argued for bank nationalization and direct mortgage relief. See also the 2008 topic.

### The household-debt and housing-failure thesis

- **Atif Mian and Amir Sufi**, *House of Debt: How They (and You) Caused the Great Recession, and How We Can Prevent It from Happening Again* (University of Chicago, 2014) — the standard academic statement of the household-debt thesis. Documents (via county-level data) that the depth of the post-2008 recession tracks the collapse of household balance sheets in highly-leveraged areas; argues that direct mortgage write-downs would have produced a faster and more equitable recovery than the bank-recapitalization approach actually pursued. The single most important academic statement on the housing failure; cross-listed in the 2008 topic.
- **Atif Mian, Amir Sufi, and Francesco Trebbi**, "Resolving Debt Overhang: Political Constraints in the Aftermath of Financial Crises," *American Economic Journal: Macroeconomics* 6 (2014) — the political-economy companion paper; documents that financial crises across countries are systematically followed by political polarization and the rise of extremist parties. The empirical link from the household-debt thesis to the populism literature.
- **Amir Sufi and Atif Mian**, ongoing work at the Becker Friedman Institute and elsewhere (2014–present) — the continuing empirical program on debt, consumption, and macro instability.

### Minsky and the post-Keynesian revival

- **Hyman P. Minsky**, *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy* (Yale, 1986; 2nd ed. McGraw-Hill, 2008) — the central statement of the financial-instability hypothesis. Stable conditions produce risk-taking that converts hedge finance into speculative finance and finally into Ponzi finance; collapse follows. Marginal in the macro mainstream for two decades after publication, reactivated by 2008. The 2008 reissue with foreword by Henry Kaufman marked the revival.
- **Hyman P. Minsky**, *John Maynard Keynes* (Columbia, 1975) — Minsky's reading of Keynes; the source for the post-Keynesian claim that the mainstream "neoclassical synthesis" (Samuelson, Solow, Hicks) domesticated Keynes by stripping out the financial-instability material.
- **L. Randall Wray**, *Why Minsky Matters: An Introduction to the Work of a Maverick Economist* (Princeton, 2016) — the standard introductory exposition by Minsky's student and the leading post-Keynesian-into-MMT figure.
- **Steve Keen**, *Debunking Economics: The Naked Emperor Dethroned* (2nd ed., Zed, 2011) and *Can We Avoid Another Financial Crisis?* (Polity, 2017) — the Australian post-Keynesian's brief against mainstream macro; one of the few economists to publicly forecast the housing collapse before 2008. The 2011 *Debunking Economics* extends Sraffa's capital-controversy critique and Robinson's into a full-spectrum attack on neoclassical micro and macro.
- **Anwar Shaikh**, *Capitalism: Competition, Conflict, Crises* (Oxford, 2016) — the most comprehensive recent statement of the classical-and-Marxian "real competition" framework as an alternative to neoclassical economics; integrates Keynes and Marx through Sraffa.
- **Wynne Godley and Marc Lavoie**, *Monetary Economics: An Integrated Approach to Credit, Money, Income, Production and Wealth* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2007; 2nd ed. 2012) — the technical statement of the stock-flow-consistent post-Keynesian framework; the methodological alternative to DSGE pushed by the Levy Institute group.
- **Levy Economics Institute of Bard College**, Working Papers series (1986–present) — the institutional home of post-Keynesian and Minskyan macro since Minsky's last years there.

### Modern Monetary Theory

- **L. Randall Wray**, *Modern Money Theory: A Primer on Macroeconomics for Sovereign Monetary Systems* (2nd ed., Palgrave Macmillan, 2015) — the textbook statement of MMT, building on Wray's *Understanding Modern Money* (1998). Argues that a sovereign currency issuer faces an inflation constraint but no financing constraint; deficits are a policy choice, not a market discipline.
- **Stephanie Kelton**, *The Deficit Myth: Modern Monetary Theory and the Birth of the People's Economy* (PublicAffairs, 2020) — the popular-press statement that brought MMT into the post-2016 Democratic-policy conversation. Kelton served as chief economist on the Senate Budget Committee minority staff under Bernie Sanders.
- **Pavlina R. Tcherneva**, *The Case for a Job Guarantee* (Polity, 2020) — the standard short statement of the MMT-adjacent job-guarantee proposal.
- **William Mitchell, L. Randall Wray, and Martin Watts**, *Macroeconomics* (Red Globe Press, 2019) — the first MMT-grounded undergraduate macro textbook; the institutional statement that MMT has reached textbook stage.
- **Doug Henwood**, "Modern Monetary Theory Isn't Helping," *Jacobin* (February 21, 2019) — representative of the heterodox-left skepticism toward MMT; argues the framework underestimates capital-account constraints and political-economy limits on Treasury-Fed coordination.
- **Lawrence H. Summers**, "The Left's embrace of modern monetary theory is a recipe for disaster," *Washington Post* (March 4, 2019) — the central mainstream-Keynesian rejection; Summers's argument is that MMT collapses the distinction between monetary and fiscal policy in ways that produce instability.

### The Marx revival and its discontents

- **David Harvey**, *The Limits to Capital* (1982; revised ed. Verso, 2006), *A Companion to Marx's* Capital (Verso, 2010), *A Companion to Marx's* Capital, *Volume Two* (Verso, 2013), *The Enigma of Capital and the Crises of Capitalism* (Oxford, 2010) — the geographer's long-running project to make Marx's *Capital* accessible and to apply it to contemporary urban and financial geography. Harvey's free online video lectures on *Capital* (Volume 1, 2008; Volume 2, 2011; widely circulated) drew exceptional audiences during and after the crisis and shaped a generation of Marx readers. The central figure in the popular Marx revival.
- **Richard D. Wolff**, *Capitalism Hits the Fan: The Global Economic Meltdown and What to Do About It* (Olive Branch, 2010), *Democracy at Work: A Cure for Capitalism* (Haymarket, 2012) — the University of Massachusetts Amherst economist's popular-press intervention. Wolff's lecture circuit and *Economic Update* radio show extended the Marx revival to broader audiences.
- **Mike Beggs**, "Zombie Marx," *Jacobin* (2011) — the central internal-heterodox critique of the Marx revival. Beggs argued that the popular *Capital*-revival treats the text as a closed analytical system rather than as a contribution to a longer tradition in classical political economy, much of whose substantive questions have been better addressed by post-Keynesians and by mainstream macro than by twenty-first-century Marxists. The 2011 statement of the methodological dispute that DeLong revisited in his 2026 reissue of [[robinson-joan|Joan Robinson]]'s 1953 "Open Letter from a Keynesian to a Marxist."
- **Joan Robinson**, "An Open Letter from a Keynesian to a Marxist" (1953; recirculated by Brad DeLong, 2026) — the 1953 founding statement of the methodological position the Beggs essay extended. Recirculated by DeLong as a polemical document against contemporary academic self-styled Marxists. See [[robinson-joan]].
- **John E. Roemer**, *A General Theory of Exploitation and Class* (Harvard, 1982), *Free to Lose: An Introduction to Marxist Economic Philosophy* (Harvard, 1988), *Analytical Foundations of Marxian Economic Theory* (Cambridge, 1981) — the central figure in Analytical Marxism. Roemer reformulated Marxian categories (exploitation, class) using neoclassical and game-theoretic tools. Repeatedly named (by Steven Durlauf and others) as the exemplar of "Marx as toolkit" against "Marx as scripture."
- **Samuel Bowles and Herbert Gintis**, *Schooling in Capitalist America* (Basic, 1976) and the long subsequent program — the other principals in the U.S. Marxian-tradition academic economics that Durlauf cites alongside Roemer. Bowles's *The Moral Economy* (Yale, 2016) and the joint Bowles-Gintis *A Cooperative Species* (Princeton, 2011) extend the program into behavioral and evolutionary territory.
- **Michael Heinrich**, *An Introduction to the Three Volumes of Karl Marx's* Capital (Monthly Review, 2012) — the leading recent German-language Marx reading translated into English; the textual-scholarly counterweight to Harvey, often preferred by readers interested in the *Neue Marx-Lektüre* tradition.
- **Robert Brenner**, *The Economics of Global Turbulence* (Verso, 2006) and his 2009 essay "What Is Good for Goldman Sachs Is Good for America" (Center for Social Theory and Comparative History, UCLA) — the Marxist long-wave account that anticipated the crisis as the latest phase of a post-1973 secular decline in OECD profitability.
- **Andrew Kliman**, *The Failure of Capitalist Production: Underlying Causes of the Great Recession* (Pluto, 2012) — the temporal-single-system-interpretation Marxist account; argues that the crisis confirms Marx's tendency-of-the-rate-of-profit-to-fall thesis. Representative of the textual-Marxist position the Beggs essay criticizes.
- *Jacobin* magazine (founded 2010 by Bhaskar Sunkara) — the institutional center of the post-2008 popular Marx revival in the U.S. The Beggs essay's appearance there in 2011 is itself a marker of how broad the magazine's editorial range was.

### Piketty and the return of distribution

- **Thomas Piketty**, *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (Belknap/Harvard, 2014; French original *Le Capital au XXIe siècle*, Seuil, 2013) — the bestseller that brought wealth-inequality data into the post-2008 macro conversation. The r > g argument: when the rate of return on capital exceeds the growth rate, inherited wealth accumulates faster than earned income, and the distribution of wealth widens. The empirical apparatus rests on the World Inequality Database (WID.world) project assembled with Anthony Atkinson, Emmanuel Saez, Gabriel Zucman, and Facundo Alvaredo.
- **Thomas Piketty, Emmanuel Saez, and Gabriel Zucman**, "Distributional National Accounts: Methods and Estimates for the United States," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 133 (May 2018) — the central methodological paper of the inequality-statistics program.
- **Thomas Piketty**, *Capital and Ideology* (Belknap/Harvard, 2020) — the longer, more historical sequel; engages with non-Western inequality regimes and proposes a "participatory socialism" policy program.
- **Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman**, *The Triumph of Injustice: How the Rich Dodge Taxes and How to Make Them Pay* (Norton, 2019) — the policy companion to the Piketty data program, focused on U.S. tax-avoidance structures. See also [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]].
- **Branko Milanovic**, *Global Inequality: A New Approach for the Age of Globalization* (Belknap/Harvard, 2016) — the "elephant chart" book, situating the rich-country distributional story in a global frame.
- **Anthony B. Atkinson**, *Inequality: What Can Be Done?* (Harvard, 2015) — the late statement of the British inequality economist whose decades of work made the Piketty program possible; cross-listed under [[atkinson-tony]].
- **Auten and Splinter**, "Income Inequality in the United States: Using Tax Data to Measure Long-term Trends" (Treasury working paper, 2024) — the most prominent recent challenge to the Piketty-Saez-Zucman U.S. series; argues that adjustments for tax reform, retirement income, and underreporting substantially reduce the measured rise in top income shares.

### Secular stagnation and the slow recovery

- **Lawrence H. Summers**, "U.S. Economic Prospects: Secular Stagnation, Hysteresis, and the Zero Lower Bound," *Business Economics* 49 (April 2014) — Summers's revival of the 1938 Alvin Hansen "secular stagnation" thesis as a framework for the post-2008 slow recovery. Argues the equilibrium real interest rate has fallen below zero and that the zero lower bound on nominal rates leaves the economy chronically demand-constrained.
- **Coen Teulings and Richard Baldwin (eds.)**, *Secular Stagnation: Facts, Causes and Cures* (VoxEU/CEPR, 2014) — the consolidated volume of academic responses to Summers; a one-stop reference for the secular-stagnation literature.
- **Gauti B. Eggertsson, Neil R. Mehrotra, and Lawrence H. Summers**, "Secular Stagnation in the Open Economy," *American Economic Review: Papers and Proceedings* 106 (May 2016), and "A Model of Secular Stagnation: Theory and Quantitative Evaluation," *American Economic Journal: Macroeconomics* 11 (2019) — the formalized model.
- **Robert J. Gordon**, *The Rise and Fall of American Growth: The U.S. Standard of Living since the Civil War* (Princeton, 2016) — the supply-side alternative to the Summers demand-side stagnation story. Argues the post-1970 productivity slowdown reflects the exhaustion of the second industrial revolution's transformative gains; the post-2008 slowdown is not a financial-crisis effect but the continuation of a long secular pattern.
- **Olivier Blanchard**, *Fiscal Policy under Low Interest Rates* (MIT Press, 2023) — the consolidated statement of the post-2008 fiscal-policy reassessment; argues that persistently low r minus g makes debt sustainability concerns less binding than the 1990s and 2000s consensus assumed.

### The COVID response as a test of the post-2008 lessons

The 2020–22 pandemic policy response is now read across the literature as the partial vindication and partial over-application of the post-2008 fiscal-and-monetary critique. The household-targeted, fast, and large response — the $2.2 trillion CARES Act of March 2020, the $900 billion supplemental of December 2020, the $1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan of March 2021, the Federal Reserve's roughly $5 trillion balance-sheet expansion, the eviction moratorium and the mortgage forbearance built into CARES, the temporary fully-refundable monthly Child Tax Credit in the ARP — reflects the Mian-Sufi household-balance-sheet critique and the DeLong-Summers hysteresis argument applied. The 2021–22 inflation surge (CPI peaking at 9.1% in June 2022) then produced an internal mainstream-Keynesian dispute over whether the ARP component of the response was too large, with Lawrence Summers, Jason Furman, and (later) Olivier Blanchard arguing yes against Janet Yellen, Jared Bernstein, Joseph Stiglitz, Mike Konczal, and Paul Krugman arguing the inflation was primarily supply-driven and would have happened without the ARP at roughly its observed magnitude.

- **Adam Tooze**, *Shutdown: How Covid Shook the World's Economy* (Viking, 2021) — the central scholarly statement that the COVID response was the partial vindication of the 2008 critique: this time bailouts reached households as well as firms, the response was faster and larger, and the underlying recovery was correspondingly more equitable. The book also documents the financial-system response, particularly the Federal Reserve's March 2020 intervention in the Treasury and corporate-bond markets, as the resolution of the unfinished post-2008 financial-architecture work.
- **Christina D. Romer and David H. Romer**, "Why Some Times Are Different: Macroeconomic Policy and the Aftermath of Financial Crises," *Economica* 84 (2017) — the analytical framework distinguishing financial-crisis recessions (where recovery requires aggressive policy) from non-financial recessions. Read in the COVID context as the framework that informed the Powell Fed's March 2020 decision to move faster and larger than in 2008.
- **Jerome H. Powell**, FOMC press conferences and speeches, March–August 2020, especially the Jackson Hole speech of August 27, 2020 introducing Flexible Average Inflation Targeting — the institutional record of the Fed adopting the post-2008 critique. The shift from inflation targeting to FAIT was explicitly motivated by the post-2008 slow-recovery experience and the need to commit to running the economy "hot" before raising rates.
- **Lawrence H. Summers**, "The Biden stimulus is admirably ambitious. But it brings some big risks, too," *Washington Post* (February 4, 2021) — the public break with the Biden economic team's ARP. Summers, the central voice for *larger* stimulus in 2009, argued that the ARP at $1.9 trillion was several multiples of the output gap and would produce significant inflation. The op-ed is the inflection point in the post-2008 Keynesian consensus.
- **Olivier J. Blanchard**, "In Defense of Concerns over the $1.9 Trillion Relief Plan," Peterson Institute (February 18, 2021), and subsequent PIIE writings 2022–23 — the parallel break from inside the IMF-tradition Keynesian camp. Blanchard's *Fiscal Policy under Low Interest Rates* (2023) integrates the post-2008 and post-COVID lessons.
- **Jason Furman**, "Why Did (Almost) No One See the Inflation Coming?" *International Monetary Fund* F&D (March 2022), and his subsequent papers — the Harvard / former CEA chair's reconstruction of why mainstream Keynesian forecasts missed the 2021 inflation; argues for a combination of fiscal overshoot, supply-chain disruption, and underestimation of labor-market tightness.
- **Janet Yellen** and **Jared Bernstein**, public statements 2021–22 — the Biden administration defense; treats the ARP scale as appropriate insurance against a repeat of the 2010s slow recovery and the resulting political damage, with the inflation as a manageable transitional cost.
- **Joseph E. Stiglitz and Dean Baker**, "Inflation and Inflation Reduction: Causes and Cures," Roosevelt Institute (2022) — the heterodox-aligned defense of the ARP; argues the 2021–22 inflation was predominantly supply-shock-driven (energy, semiconductors, automobiles, supply-chain disruption) and that the demand-side contribution was small.
- **Mike Konczal**, Roosevelt Institute working papers 2021–23, and his book *Freedom from the Market* (New Press, 2021) — the policy-shop case for the ARP and for the Build Back Better / Inflation Reduction Act program as the institutional consolidation of the post-2008 lessons.
- **Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco**, Adam Hale Shapiro, "How Much Do Supply and Demand Drive Inflation?" *FRBSF Economic Letter* (June 21, 2022) and follow-up letters — the central in-house attempt to decompose the 2021–22 inflation into demand and supply contributions. The Shapiro decomposition has become the most-cited single empirical reference in the dispute.
- **Atif Mian, Ludwig Straub, and Amir Sufi**, "The Saving Glut of the Rich" (NBER, 2020) and related papers — Mian and Sufi extending the household-debt program to the question of why the post-2008 environment of low rates persisted; relevant to understanding the macro environment the COVID response entered.
- **Paul Krugman**, *New York Times* columns 2020–23 — the running saltwater commentary on the COVID response. Krugman supported the ARP in early 2021, conceded inflation concerns through 2022, and by 2023 was running a partial-mea-culpa series on what mainstream Keynesianism had gotten wrong about the inflation.
- **Inflation Reduction Act of 2022** (Public Law 117-169, signed August 16, 2022) — the legislative settlement; combined deficit-reducing tax provisions with the largest U.S. climate investment to date. Read across the literature as the institutional consolidation of the post-2008 industrial-policy turn and as the political answer to the inflation critique of the ARP.

### The reassessment of mainstream macro

- **Olivier Blanchard, Giovanni Dell'Ariccia, and Paolo Mauro**, "Rethinking Macroeconomic Policy," IMF Staff Position Note SPN/10/03 (February 2010) — the institutional acceptance, from inside the IMF, of much of the saltwater critique: that pre-crisis macro was overconfident in inflation-targeting central banks, underweighted financial-stability considerations, and underestimated the role of fiscal policy at the zero lower bound.
- **Olivier Blanchard and Lawrence H. Summers (eds.)**, *Evolution or Revolution? Rethinking Macroeconomic Policy after the Great Recession* (MIT Press, 2019) — the consolidated decade-later assessment.
- **George A. Akerlof**, "What They Were Thinking Then: The Consequences for Macroeconomics during the Past 60 Years," *Journal of Economic Perspectives* 33 (Fall 2019) — the Nobel laureate's reflective account of how macro got where it did and where it should now go.
- **Andrew G. Haldane** (Chief Economist, Bank of England), various speeches 2012–2021, especially "The Dappled World" (GLS Shackle Lecture, 2016) — the central-bank insider critique; argues for a pluralist methodological pivot away from representative-agent DSGE toward agent-based and complexity-based modeling.
- **J. Doyne Farmer**, *Making Sense of Chaos: A Better Economics for a Better World* (Yale, 2024) — the consolidated statement of the agent-based-and-complexity alternative; Farmer is the Santa Fe Institute–trained economist whose post-2008 collaboration with Haldane and the Bank of England has been the central institutional vehicle for this current.
- **Dani Rodrik**, *Economics Rules: The Rights and Wrongs of the Dismal Science* (Norton, 2015) — defends the pluralist-models view of economics ("economics is a collection of models") against both the DSGE-monoculture critique and the heterodox-replacement program. The middle position.

### Heterodox surveys and the discipline-level critique

- **Steve Keen**, *Debunking Economics: The Naked Emperor Dethroned* (2nd ed., Zed, 2011) — see also under post-Keynesian. The most-circulated single heterodox textbook intervention of the post-2008 period; the second edition is rewritten to make 2008 the central case.
- **Ha-Joon Chang**, *Economics: The User's Guide* (Bloomsbury, 2014) — the Cambridge institutionalist's pluralist introduction; presents nine schools of economic thought as equally valid starting points. Companion to Chang's earlier *23 Things They Don't Tell You About Capitalism* (Bloomsbury, 2010).
- **The Institute for New Economic Thinking** (INET; founded 2009, George Soros initial funder) — the institutional vehicle for the post-2008 reform-of-economics agenda; funds research, conferences, and the *Oxford Review of Economic Policy*'s reform-issue series.
- **Rethinking Economics** (UK; founded 2013) and the **Post-Crash Economics Society** (University of Manchester; 2012) — the student-organized post-2008 curriculum-reform movements; documented in the Manchester *Econocracy* report (2017) by Joe Earle, Cahal Moran, and Zach Ward-Perkins.
- **Adam Tooze**, *Chartbook* (substack, 2020–present) — Tooze's running commentary on the post-2008 macro-and-political-economy conversation; the most-circulated newsletter in the space. Tooze is included in the 2008 fallout topic for the political-economy side and listed here for the economics-methodology side.
- **Brad DeLong**, *Grasping Reality* (substack, 2020–present, after the earlier blog 2002–2020) — DeLong's running saltwater-Keynesian commentary. His 2026 reissue of the [[robinson-joan|Robinson Open Letter]] is the specific occasion of this topic page.
