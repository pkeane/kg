# The Afterlife of Occupy Wall Street

Occupy Wall Street began as a small encampment in Zuccotti Park, a privately owned public space three blocks from the New York Stock Exchange, on 17 September 2011. The action had been called by the Canadian magazine *Adbusters* in July of that year, organized in part by veterans of the global-justice movement of the late 1990s and of the anti–Iraq War mobilizations, and was seeded by anarchists and activists working in the tradition of horizontal, leaderless general-assembly organizing. Within weeks, "Occupy" encampments had spread to several hundred cities in North America and Europe. The slogan "We are the 99%," first used in a Tumblr blog of working-people's testimonies launched in late August, became the rhetorical signature of the movement. The Zuccotti Park encampment was cleared by the New York Police Department on 15 November 2011; most other camps were cleared in the following weeks. By the spring of 2012 the movement had, in the most visible sense, ended.

The interpretive question — whether Occupy was a brief failed protest moment or the opening of a longer political realignment — has been contested since the eviction itself. The case for a substantial afterlife has been made by participants ([[graeber-david|David Graeber]], [[taylor-astra|Astra Taylor]]), by journalists who followed the post-Occupy organizing networks ([[jaffe-sarah|Sarah Jaffe]]), by figures whose later political projects drew on Occupy-trained personnel ([[sanders-bernie|Bernie Sanders]] and his organizers), and by scholars who have read the 2011–2020 American left as a continuous formation ([[taylor-keeanga-yamahtta|Keeanga-Yamahtta Taylor]]). The case for limited or rhetorical impact has been made, in qualified form, by movement historians who note the absence of durable institutions ([[kauffman-la|L. A. Kauffman]]) and, in sharper form, by critics of horizontalist movement design ([[dean-jodi|Jodi Dean]], [[white-micah|Micah White]]) and by writers skeptical of the proposition that protest without institutional follow-through produces lasting change ([[frank-thomas|Thomas Frank]]).

Several effects can be documented without entering the interpretive dispute. The phrases "the 99%" and "the 1%" entered American political vocabulary in the autumn of 2011 and remained in routine use; polling on inequality as a salient political concern shifted in the months that followed. The Strike Debt working group, formed out of Occupy in 2012, became Rolling Jubilee, which became the Debt Collective; a documented institutional lineage extends from a 2011 encampment to the debt-cancellation organizing of the early 2020s. The Democratic Socialists of America grew from roughly 6,500 dues-paying members in 2014 to roughly 90,000 by 2021, with Occupy-trained organizers a substantial part of the early cadre. The Sanders 2016 primary organization has been described by its own staff ([[sanders-bernie|Sanders]], Faiz Shakir, Becky Bond, Zack Exley) as drawing heavily on Occupy alumni. None of this settles whether Occupy itself caused these effects or whether the post-2008 political environment would have produced them in any case, but the personnel and organizational continuities are matters of record.

## Annotated bibliography

### From inside the movement

- **[[graeber-david|David Graeber]]**, *The Democracy Project: A History, a Crisis, a Movement* (Spiegel & Grau, 2013) — written by the anthropologist and anarchist organizer who was central to the September 2011 General Assembly that planned the Zuccotti Park action. Graeber's account places Occupy within a longer tradition of horizontal, consensus-based organizing reaching back through the global justice movement of the late 1990s to Spanish anarchism and the New Left. He defends the movement's refusal of demands and of designated leaders as principled rather than failed, and argues that the rhetorical and personnel effects of Occupy were already substantial by the time the book went to press.
- **[[graeber-david|David Graeber]]**, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (Melville House, 2011) — published two months before the Zuccotti Park encampment. Became one of the movement's reference texts. The argument that debt, rather than market exchange, is the originary economic relationship and that debt cancellation is a recurring instrument of political renewal supplied much of the conceptual frame for the post-Occupy debt-resistance organizing.
- **n+1 / Astra Taylor et al., eds.**, *Occupy! Scenes from Occupied America* (Verso, 2011) — the first anthology, assembled in real time from contributors including [[taylor-astra|Astra Taylor]], [[taylor-keeanga-yamahtta|Keeanga-Yamahtta Taylor]], Mark Greif, Doug Henwood, and many of the working-group participants. A primary document of the movement's first three months.
- **Writers for the 99%**, *Occupying Wall Street: The Inside Story of an Action That Changed America* (OR Books, 2011) — collaborative chronicle by a group of journalists and organizers embedded in Zuccotti Park.

### The case for a long political afterlife

- **[[jaffe-sarah|Sarah Jaffe]]**, *Necessary Trouble: Americans in Revolt* (Nation Books, 2016) — the standard journalistic synthesis of the post-2008 American left. Traces continuities of personnel, tactics, and demands from Occupy through Strike Debt, the Chicago Teachers Union, the Fight for $15, the Movement for Black Lives, the climate-justice movement, and the early Sanders coalition. The argument is empirical rather than polemical: that a measurable network of people moved across these organizations and that the network's existence is itself a substantive afterlife.
- **[[klein-naomi|Naomi Klein]]**, *No Is Not Enough: Resisting Trump's Shock Politics and Winning the World We Need* (Haymarket, 2017) — the broader cultural-shift argument. Klein's framing is that Occupy authorized the public re-introduction of words and concepts (inequality, oligarchy, the 99%) that had been absent from mainstream American political discourse since the 1930s, and that this rhetorical authorization made the policy debates of the late 2010s possible. See also her *Nation* essays of 2011–2012, collected in part in *The Klein Doctrine* anthology.
- **[[sanders-bernie|Bernie Sanders]]**, *Our Revolution* (Thomas Dunne, 2016) and *Where We Go from Here* (Thomas Dunne, 2018) — the central insider claim that Occupy made the 2016 primary campaign possible by changing what could be said and by training the staff who later ran it. Sanders is explicit and consistent on this point.
- **Faiz Shakir, Becky Bond, Zack Exley**, *Rules for Revolutionaries: How Big Organizing Can Change Everything* (Chelsea Green, 2017) — the operational account by senior staff of the Sanders 2016 campaign. The book describes the "distributed organizing" model the campaign used, traces it directly to volunteer networks that crystallized during Occupy, and treats the personnel continuity as the organizational backbone of the campaign.
- **[[taylor-keeanga-yamahtta|Keeanga-Yamahtta Taylor]]**, *From #BlackLivesMatter to Black Liberation* (Haymarket, 2016) — argues that BLM, which emerged in 2013–2014, was made possible in part by the post-Occupy organizing infrastructure and by the Occupy-authorized public language of structural critique, even where the Movement for Black Lives explicitly distinguished itself from Occupy on race.
- **Michael Levitin**, *Generation Occupy: Reawakening American Democracy* (Counterpoint, 2021) — written for the tenth anniversary by a former Occupy press-team participant. The most sustained book-length argument that the movement's afterlife has been substantial and identifiable across the decade since.

### The case for limited or qualified impact

- **[[kauffman-la|L. A. Kauffman]]**, *Direct Action: Protest and the Reinvention of American Radicalism* (Verso, 2017) — sympathetic to the post-1960s direct-action tradition but documents, with precision, the organizational dissolution of Occupy after the November 2011 evictions and the absence of durable institutions to receive the movement's energy. Kauffman's later *How to Read a Protest* (University of California, 2018) is a related work.
- **[[frank-thomas|Thomas Frank]]**, *Listen, Liberal* (Metropolitan, 2016) and *The People, No: A Brief History of Anti-Populism* (Metropolitan, 2020) — skeptical of the claim that rhetorical shifts translate into institutional change. Frank's argument is not that Occupy was unimportant but that the Democratic Party's professional class systematically absorbed and neutralized the post-Occupy energy.
- **[[dean-jodi|Jodi Dean]]**, *Crowds and Party* (Verso, 2016) — the most sustained left critique of Occupy's horizontalist movement form. Dean argues that the refusal of leadership, demands, and party structure left Occupy a "crowd" rather than a political subject and that the movement exemplified the failure mode of what she calls "communicative capitalism" — protest as media spectacle without a vehicle for durable political contestation.
- **[[white-micah|Micah White]]**, *The End of Protest: A New Playbook for Revolution* (Knopf Canada, 2016) — White was one of the *Adbusters* editors who issued the original July 2011 call. The book is in substantial part a self-critique: an argument that Occupy and the cycle of horizontalist mass protests it represented have run their course and that revolutionary politics requires different organizational forms.
- **[[bray-mark|Mark Bray]]**, *Translating Anarchism, Translating Capitalism* (a 2013 article in *ROAR Magazine*, expanded in *Translating Anarchism: The Spanish Anarchist Exiles* manuscript, 2013) — Bray, an Occupy press-team member at Zuccotti Park, became a critic of the movement's media-driven self-presentation. Useful as a participant-critic perspective from inside.

### Documented organizational lineages

- **Astra Taylor, Hannah Appel, and the Debt Collective**, *Can't Pay, Won't Pay: The Case for Economic Disobedience and Debt Abolition* (Haymarket, 2020) — the manifesto of the Debt Collective. The institutional chain Strike Debt (2012) → Rolling Jubilee (2012, which bought and cancelled approximately $32 million of distressed consumer debt) → the Debt Collective (2014–) is a continuous lineage from the Occupy working groups, and the Debt Collective's organizing on student debt fed directly into the public debate that culminated in the Biden administration's 2022 student-debt cancellation actions.
- **Andrew Ross**, *Creditocracy and the Case for Debt Refusal* (OR Books, 2014) — the intellectual companion to the Strike Debt / Debt Collective project. Ross was a Strike Debt participant; the book extends Graeber's argument in *Debt* into a concrete contemporary American program of debt resistance.
- **Maurice Mitchell and the Movement for Black Lives organizers**, post-Occupy interviews — Mitchell, who later directed the Working Families Party, was active in Occupy the Hood (an Occupy offshoot in 2011) before moving to Ferguson in 2014. Several public interviews (in *Dissent*, *The Nation*, *In These Times*) describe the personnel migration from Occupy to the Movement for Black Lives.
- **Bhaskar Sunkara**, *The Socialist Manifesto* (Basic, 2019) — Sunkara founded *Jacobin* in 2010, before Occupy, but documents the magazine's growth and the Democratic Socialists of America's growth in the post-Occupy years as a continuous political-cultural project. The DSA membership trajectory from roughly 6,500 in 2014 to roughly 90,000 by 2021 is the most-cited single quantitative indicator of the Occupy afterlife.

### Comparative and contextual

- **Manuel Castells**, *Networks of Outrage and Hope: Social Movements in the Internet Age* (Polity, 2012) — places Occupy in the global comparative frame with the Arab Spring uprisings of 2011, the Spanish *Indignados* (15-M, May 2011), and the Greek Syntagma Square protests. Castells's argument is that these were facets of a single networked-media-enabled mobilization cycle. Disputed but the foundational comparative statement.
- **Todd Gitlin**, *Occupy Nation: The Roots, the Spirit, and the Promise of Occupy Wall Street* (HarperCollins, 2012) — written within months of the eviction by the SDS-veteran sociologist who had also written the standard book on 1960s American radicalism. Gitlin reads Occupy with explicit comparison to SDS and is candid about the structural problems shared by the two movements.
- **[[piven-frances-fox|Frances Fox Piven]]**, *Who's Afraid of Frances Fox Piven?* (The New Press, 2011) and essays in *The Nation* — Piven's longer argument (developed in her 1977 *Poor People's Movements*, with Richard Cloward) that mass disruptive movements achieve their effects through disruption itself, not through formal organization, is a load-bearing framework for the pro-impact reading of Occupy. Piven was an explicit defender of the movement in 2011–2012.
- **Sidney Tarrow**, *The Language of Contention: Revolutions in Words, 1688–2012* (Cambridge, 2013) — the contentious-politics scholar's framework for tracking the diffusion of political language. The "1% / 99%" framing is one of his late-2012 case studies.

### Recent retrospectives

- **Mike Konczal**, *Freedom from the Market: America's Fight to Liberate Itself from the Grip of the Invisible Hand* (The New Press, 2021) — places Occupy in the longer twentieth-century argument over the politics of decommodification. Includes a sustained late chapter on the post-Occupy debt and housing organizing.
- **Astra Taylor and Leah Hunt-Hendrix**, *Solidarity: The Past, Present, and Future of a World-Changing Idea* (Pantheon, 2024) — a recent overview of solidarity politics that draws explicitly on the Occupy moment and on the organizing networks that followed.
- *Dissent*, *Jacobin*, *n+1*, and *The Nation*, ten-year retrospectives published in September–November 2021 — collectively the most accessible single-document survey of how the post-Occupy left assessed the movement at its decade mark.
