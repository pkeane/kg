# How to Tax the Wealthy

The moral and distributional case for taxing concentrated wealth — that it threatens democratic equality, that large fortunes are substantially the product of luck and inheritance rather than desert, that public goods require revenue — is made across a large literature and is summarized in [[inequality]] and [[justice-as-fairness]]. This page is concerned with the narrower and more technical question that the moral case usually leaves open: *by what instrument*. Most disagreement among people who agree the wealthy should pay more is not about whether, but about the choice of tax base, the rate, the method of valuation, the treatment of unrealized gains, and the problem of enforcement across borders.

A recurring theme organizes the mechanism literature. A tax is only as effective as its base and its administration, and the income of the very wealthy is largely *unrealized*: it accrues as the rising market value of assets that are never sold, and therefore never appears as taxable income. The strategy summarized as "buy, borrow, die" — hold appreciating assets, borrow against them to fund consumption, and pass them to heirs at death — allows large fortunes to grow across a lifetime while generating little realized income to tax. Much of the technical debate is about how to reach value that the conventional income tax does not.

The proposals divide roughly into those that tax a *flow* (income, including the optimal-top-rate tradition and the taxation of capital gains and rents) and those that tax a *stock* (annual net worth, inheritance, or land value). They differ in administrative difficulty, in vulnerability to avoidance and capital flight, and in legal and constitutional standing. The annotated bibliography below is organized by mechanism rather than by author.

## Annotated bibliography

### The classic answer: tax land and economic rent

- **[[george-henry|Henry George]]**, *Progress and Poverty* (1879). The case for a tax on the unimproved value of land — the "single tax." George's argument, built on [[ricardo-david|Ricardo]]'s theory of rent, is that location value is created by the surrounding community rather than the owner, and that because land is fixed in supply and cannot be moved or hidden, a tax on its value does not discourage production. This is the efficiency property — no deadweight loss — that has kept land taxation alive in economics long after the "single tax" movement faded.
- **[[paine-thomas|Thomas Paine]]**, *Agrarian Justice* (1797). An early specification of a stock tax with a defined use: a levy on inherited landed property, framed as recovering the "ground rent" owed to the commons, funding a lump-sum stakeholder grant at age 21 and a pension thereafter.
- **[[stiglitz-joseph|Joseph Stiglitz]]**, *The Price of Inequality* (2012) and subsequent work. Stiglitz argues that much top-end income is economic rent — returns to monopoly, financial position, and land — rather than to productive contribution, and that taxing rents and land is therefore both more equitable and more efficient than taxing labor.

### Progressive income taxation and the optimal top rate

- **James Mirrlees**, "An Exploration in the Theory of Optimum Income Taxation" (*Review of Economic Studies*, 1971). The foundational formal treatment of how to set income tax rates given the tradeoff between redistribution and the disincentive to work. Mirrlees received the Nobel Prize in 1996; the framework underlies all later optimal-tax arguments.
- **Peter Diamond** and **Emmanuel Saez**, "The Case for a Progressive Tax: From Basic Research to Policy Recommendations" (*Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 2011). Applying the Mirrlees framework to U.S. data, the authors derive an optimal top marginal income tax rate in the region of 70 percent — far above current rates and consistent with the 91–94 percent statutory top rates the United States maintained in the 1940s and 1950s.

### Inheritance and estate taxation

- **[[piketty-thomas|Thomas Piketty]]**, *Capital and Ideology* (2020). Progressive inheritance taxation as a central instrument, paired with a universal capital endowment for young adults — redistributing the *stock* of inherited wealth rather than only taxing income flows.
- **Lily Batchelder**, "What Should Society Expect from Heirs? A Proposal for a Comprehensive Inheritance Tax" (*Tax Law Review*, 2009). Argues for taxing inheritance in the hands of the *recipient*, as income, rather than taxing the estate of the deceased — the design [[rawls-john|Rawls]] also favored, on the ground that an inheritance is an unearned windfall to the heir.
- **[[rawls-john|John Rawls]]**, *A Theory of Justice* (1971) and *Justice as Fairness: A Restatement* (2001). Endorses inheritance and gift taxation levied on the recipient, and a proportional expenditure (consumption) tax, as instruments for limiting the intergenerational concentration of wealth and securing fair equality of opportunity. See [[justice-as-fairness]].

### The wealth tax

- **[[piketty-thomas|Thomas Piketty]]**, *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2014). The proposal for a progressive *global* tax on net wealth. Piketty treats the global scope not as utopian decoration but as the mechanism's load-bearing element: without international coordination and financial transparency, capital relocates to escape a national levy.
- **[[saez-zucman|Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman]]**, *The Triumph of Injustice* (2019). The most detailed contemporary "how" account. They design the annual progressive wealth tax (the basis of Elizabeth Warren's 2020 proposal — two percent on net worth above $50 million, with a billionaire surtax) and specify the supporting machinery: mark-to-market valuation, a national wealth registry, an "exit tax" on expatriating capital, country-by-country corporate reporting, and a rebuilt enforcement apparatus. Their claim is that prior wealth taxes failed because of bad design — exemptions, low thresholds, weak enforcement — not because the instrument is unworkable.

### The realization problem: capital gains and the step-up loophole

- **Saez and Zucman**, *The Triumph of Injustice* (2019), and **ProPublica**, "The Secret IRS Files" (2021, Jesse Eisinger, Jeff Ernsthausen, and Paul Kiel). The journalism documented, from leaked returns, that several of the wealthiest Americans paid "true tax rates" near zero in some years because their wealth grew as unrealized gains. The reform proposals that follow target realization directly: taxing capital gains at the same rate as labor income; "mark-to-market" or accrual taxation of gains as they accumulate rather than at sale; and repeal of the **step-up in basis** at death, which currently resets an asset's cost basis to its market value for heirs and so erases unrealized gains from capital-gains tax entirely. Closing step-up is the narrowest, most administrable lever aimed at "buy, borrow, die."

### Enforcement and international coordination

- **Gabriel Zucman**, *The Hidden Wealth of Nations* (2015). Estimates that roughly eight percent of global household financial wealth sits in tax havens, largely unrecorded, and argues that a global financial registry and automatic cross-border information exchange are prerequisites for taxing the wealthy at all — the base must be made visible before it can be taxed.
- **Gabriel Zucman**, *A Blueprint for a Coordinated Minimum Effective Taxation Standard for Ultra-High-Net-Worth Individuals* (G20 report, 2024). Proposes a coordinated minimum tax on the world's roughly three thousand billionaires, set at two percent of wealth, designed so that no single jurisdiction bears the flight risk alone — the international answer to the capital-mobility objection.

### Objections: feasibility, flight, and principle

- **Lawrence Summers** and **Natasha Sarin**, op-eds and papers (2019). Argue that wealth-tax revenue estimates are overstated, that valuation and avoidance make an annual net-worth tax hard to administer, and that reform should instead strengthen the income tax — raising capital-gains rates, taxing gains at death, and funding IRS enforcement. A critique from within the pro-progressivity camp: agreement on the goal, dissent on the instrument.
- **Gerald Auten** and **David Splinter**, "Income Inequality in the United States: Using Tax Data to Measure Long-Term Trends" (*Journal of Political Economy*, 2024). After adjusting for under-reported income, taxes, and transfers, the authors find a substantially smaller rise in top income shares than the Piketty–Saez–Zucman series reports — a dispute over the *magnitude* of the problem the taxes are meant to address.
- **The constitutional question (United States).** Whether a federal tax on net wealth is a "direct tax" requiring apportionment among the states under Article I has no settled answer; the Supreme Court's decision in *Moore v. United States* (2024) upheld a specific tax on unrealized foreign earnings but declined to resolve the broader realization and wealth-tax questions.
- **[[nozick-robert|Robert Nozick]]**, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974). The principled objection. On the entitlement theory of justice, holdings acquired and transferred justly are owned outright, and redistributive taxation of them is illegitimate — "taxation of earnings from labor is on a par with forced labor." The strongest statement of the position that the question is not *how* to tax the wealthy but *whether* it can be just at all. See [[justice-as-fairness]] for the Rawls–Nozick debate.
