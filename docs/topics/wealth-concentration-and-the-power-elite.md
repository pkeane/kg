# Wealth Concentration and the Power Elite

This page gathers the literature that documents and analyzes the concentration of wealth in a small fraction of the population and the conversion of that wealth into political and social power. It is the *descriptive and diagnostic* counterpart to [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]], which collects the *prescriptive* literature on what to do about it; figures who appear in both — [[winters-jeffrey|Winters]], [[harrington-brooke|Harrington]], [[mayer-jane|Mayer]], [[gilens-page|Gilens and Page]] — are treated here as documentation of the phenomenon rather than as arguments for a tax instrument.

The literature runs along several distinct lines that are easy to conflate. One is the **muckraking catalogue** of who is rich and how they got that way, from the Gilded Age exposés through Ferdinand [[lundberg-ferdinand|Lundberg]]'s *The Rich and the Super-Rich* (1968) to its contemporary heirs. A second is the **power-structure tradition** in sociology — C. Wright [[mills-c-wright|Mills]], G. William [[domhoff-g-william|Domhoff]] — which asks not who is wealthy but who actually governs, and answers that an interlocking elite drawn from corporations, the executive branch, and the military does. A third is the **empirical-measurement** tradition — [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]], [[saez-zucman|Saez and Zucman]] — that built the long-run statistical series on top wealth and income shares. A fourth documents the **machinery of preservation**: the trusts, foundations, offshore structures, and family offices through which large fortunes are insulated from taxation and dispersal across generations. A fifth, on **wealth and political voice**, asks what the resulting concentration does to a democracy.

These lines do not agree on a single thesis, and some of their authors would reject each other's framing — the formal economists who built the top-income series do not generally describe their object as a "power elite," and the power-structure sociologists predate and stand apart from the inequality-statistics literature. What the page assembles is the body of work an informed reader would consult to answer the question Lundberg posed: how concentrated is wealth, how is it kept concentrated, and what does it buy.

## Annotated bibliography

### The muckraking catalogue: who is rich and how

- **Gustavus Myers**, *History of the Great American Fortunes* (1909). The Progressive-era ancestor of the genre: a three-volume documentation of how the largest American fortunes — Astor, Vanderbilt, Gould, the railroad and land fortunes — were assembled, emphasizing land grants, public franchises, and political favor rather than thrift. Myers also wrote a *History of the Supreme Court* and a *History of Bigotry in the United States*.
- **Matthew Josephson**, *The Robber Barons: The Great American Capitalists, 1861–1901* (1934). The Depression-era narrative history that fixed the phrase "robber baron" in general usage, treating the post-Civil-War industrialists (Rockefeller, Carnegie, Morgan, Gould) as a class that captured the machinery of the state.
- **Ferdinand [[lundberg-ferdinand|Lundberg]]**, *America's 60 Families* (1937). The book that made Lundberg's name: an argument that a small number of intermarried wealthy families constituted a de facto American plutocracy, exercising power through ownership, philanthropy, and the press. A New Deal-era document, hostile to the concentration of economic power its author saw the period's politics contesting.
- **Ferdinand [[lundberg-ferdinand|Lundberg]]**, *The Rich and the Super-Rich: A Study in the Power of Money Today* (1968). The book most readers mean when they cite Lundberg: an 800-page catalogue of American wealth holdings, the trust-and-foundation machinery that preserves them, the tax provisions that shelter them, and the influence of money over politics and the press. Sprawling, polemical, and heavily documented; it sold widely and was, in effect, an updating of *America's 60 Families* for the postwar period. No single later book has been generally acknowledged as its direct successor, a point reviewers of its reissues have made repeatedly.

### The power-structure tradition in sociology

- **Thorstein [[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]]**, *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899). The founding analysis of the rich as a *class* with a culture: "conspicuous consumption" and "conspicuous leisure" as the means by which a propertied class displays and reproduces status. The intellectual ancestor of every later account of the social, as opposed to merely economic, dimension of great wealth.
- **C. Wright [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]**, *The Power Elite* (1956). The central text of the tradition. Mills argues that postwar American power is concentrated in an interlocking directorate drawn from three institutional hierarchies — the large corporation, the executive branch and its agencies, and the senior military — whose members share social origins, schooling, and clubs, and circulate among the three domains. The book displaced the pluralist account of dispersed, competing interest groups for a generation of critics and gave the phrase "power elite" to the language.
- **Floyd Hunter**, *Community Power Structure: A Study of Decision Makers* (1953). The local-level companion to Mills, and in part its methodological model: a study of "Regional City" (Atlanta) using reputational methods to identify a small business-dominated group that set the agenda behind the formal apparatus of municipal government. The book that opened the mid-century "community power debate" between elite and pluralist accounts.
- **E. Digby Baltzell**, *Philadelphia Gentlemen* (1958) and *The Protestant Establishment: Aristocracy and Caste in America* (1964). A sociology of the WASP upper class — the boarding schools, clubs, and registers (Baltzell popularized the acronym "WASP") through which an old-stock Protestant elite maintained itself, and an argument that its refusal to assimilate new elites (notably Jews and Catholics) was hardening it into a closed caste.
- **G. William [[domhoff-g-william|Domhoff]]**, *Who Rules America?* (1967, and many subsequent editions through the 2020s). The book that carried the Mills program into empirical social science and kept it current for half a century. Domhoff documents the social cohesion of the upper class (schools, clubs, the Social Register, intermarriage), the "policy-planning network" of foundations, think tanks, and policy-discussion groups through which corporate wealth shapes the agenda, and the mechanisms by which that class achieves its aims in government. The standard modern reference for the power-structure approach.

### Measuring the concentration: the inequality-statistics tradition

This strand is treated more fully under [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]] and [[inequality]]; the entries here are the works that established *how concentrated* wealth is.

- **Thomas [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]**, *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2014). The synthesis built on the long-run top-income and top-wealth series Piketty assembled with Anthony Atkinson and Emmanuel Saez. Its central empirical finding is that wealth concentration, having fallen during the mid-century "great compression," returned by the early twenty-first century toward Gilded Age levels, driven by a tendency for the return on capital to exceed the growth rate (the "*r > g*" argument).
- **Emmanuel Saez** and **Gabriel [[zucman-gabriel|Zucman]]**, "Wealth Inequality in the United States since 1913" (*Quarterly Journal of Economics* 131, 2016). The paper that reconstructed U.S. wealth concentration across a century from capitalized income-tax data, finding the top 0.1 percent's share of household wealth roughly tripling from the late 1970s to the 2010s. The empirical core of the "super-rich" claim.
- **Edward N. Wolff**, *Top Heavy: The Increasing Inequality of Wealth in America and What Can Be Done About It* (1995) and *A Century of Wealth in America* (2017). The long-running American wealth-survey scholarship, drawing on the Survey of Consumer Finances rather than tax data; Wolff is the standard source for the distribution of net worth, including the role of housing versus financial assets across the distribution.

### The machinery of preservation: trusts, foundations, offshore

These works document the specifically modern form of Lundberg's central subject — the legal and professional apparatus that keeps fortunes concentrated. Several reappear in [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]] as obstacles to taxation.

- **Chuck [[collins-chuck|Collins]]**, *The Wealth Hoarders: How Billionaires Pay Millions to Hide Trillions* (2021). The closest contemporary analogue to Lundberg in subject and tone. Collins — an Oscar Mayer heir who gave away his inheritance — reports from inside what he calls the "wealth defense industry": the lawyers, accountants, and wealth managers who deploy dynasty trusts, family offices, shell companies, and offshore structures to entrench hereditary fortunes. His earlier *Born on Third Base* (2016) is the memoir-argument companion.
- **Brooke [[harrington-brooke|Harrington]]**, *Capital without Borders: Wealth Managers and the One Percent* (2016). An ethnography of the wealth-management profession, written after the author trained and certified as a practitioner, documenting the trusts and offshore vehicles through which large fortunes are placed beyond the reach of taxation and creditors. Her *Offshore: Stealth Wealth and the New Colonialism* (2024) extends the account to the jurisdictions that host the structures.
- **Jeffrey [[winters-jeffrey|Winters]]**, *Oligarchy* (2011). A comparative theory of oligarchy defined by the politics of wealth defense: Winters argues that what unites oligarchs across very different regimes is the deployment of wealth to protect wealth, and that American oligarchs, secure in their property, concentrate their political resources on an "income defense industry" devoted to minimizing taxation.
- **Gabriel [[zucman-gabriel|Zucman]]**, *The Hidden Wealth of Nations: The Scourge of Tax Havens* (2015). Estimates that roughly eight percent of global household financial wealth is held in tax havens, largely unrecorded in official statistics — so that conventional measures understate top wealth shares. The empirical case that a large part of the super-rich's holdings is, by design, invisible.
- **David Callahan**, *The Givers: Wealth, Power, and Philanthropy in a New Gilded Age* (2017). On the political dimension of large-scale philanthropy: how mega-foundations and living donors convert fortunes into influence over public policy, education, and science, exercising a form of power that is tax-advantaged and democratically unaccountable. The contemporary treatment of the foundation question Lundberg raised in 1968.

### Wealth and political voice

The works here document the conversion of concentrated wealth into political power. They are covered at greater length, in the context of the taxation debate, under [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]].

- **Martin [[gilens-page|Gilens]]** and **Benjamin Page**, "Testing Theories of American Politics" (*Perspectives on Politics* 12, 2014), with Gilens, *Affluence and Influence* (2012). Across roughly 1,800 policy questions, find that economic elites and business groups have substantial independent influence on U.S. federal policy while the preferences of average citizens have little once elite preferences are accounted for.
- **Larry [[bartels-larry|Bartels]]**, *Unequal Democracy: The Political Economy of the New Gilded Age* (2008). Finds U.S. senators' voting far more responsive to high-income constituents than to middle- or low-income ones.
- **Jacob [[hacker-jacob|Hacker]]** and **Paul Pierson**, *Winner-Take-All Politics* (2010). Argues that the post-1970s surge in top incomes was produced by organized political action — business mobilization, the decline of unions — rather than by impersonal market forces.
- **Jane [[mayer-jane|Mayer]]**, *Dark Money: The Hidden History of the Billionaires Behind the Rise of the Radical Right* (2016). Documents the donor network built by Charles and David Koch and allied fortunes — foundations, think tanks, academic centers, advocacy groups — and its program of tax and regulatory change.
- **Anand [[giridharadas-anand|Giridharadas]]**, *Winners Take All: The Elite Charade of Changing the World* (2018). Argues that elite philanthropy and "win-win" social-impact initiatives redirect demands for structural redistribution into voluntary action that leaves the distribution of wealth untouched.

### The contemporary super-rich: journalism and reportage

- **Kevin [[phillips-kevin|Phillips]]**, *Wealth and Democracy: A Political History of the American Rich* (2002). A long-historical account, by the Republican strategist turned critic, of the recurring American cycle of wealth concentration and political reaction, situating the late-twentieth-century rise of the rich against the Gilded Age and the 1920s. Phillips's *Boiling Point* (1993) is the earlier statement.
- **Chrystia [[freeland-chrystia|Freeland]]**, *Plutocrats: The Rise of the New Global Super-Rich and the Fall of Everyone Else* (2012). A reported study of the transnational ultra-wealthy as an emerging class detached from national loyalties, by the journalist who later became Canada's finance minister and deputy prime minister.
- **Peter S. Goodman**, *Davos Man: How the Billionaires Devoured the World* (2022). The *New York Times* economics correspondent's account of the World Economic Forum class and the argument that "stakeholder capitalism" functions to forestall redistribution — a thesis adjacent to Giridharadas.
- **Matthew [[stewart-matthew|Stewart]]**, *The 9.9 Percent: The New Aristocracy That Is Entrenching Inequality and Warping Our Culture* (2021), developed from his 2018 *Atlantic* essay "The Birth of a New American Aristocracy." Shifts the focus from the top 0.1 percent to the affluent professional class just below it, arguing that this larger group hoards opportunity through housing, schooling, and assortative marriage.
- **Michael Mechanic**, *Jackpot: How the Super-Rich Really Live — and How Their Wealth Harms Us All* (2021). Reportage on the daily life, anxieties, and advisory apparatus of the very wealthy, drawing on the wealth-management and "family office" world that Harrington and Collins analyze.
- **Peter Phillips**, *Giants: The Global Power Elite* (2018). An explicit attempt to update C. Wright Mills and Domhoff for the era of transnational finance, identifying the directors of the largest asset-management firms as a globally integrated power elite. Treated as activist-scholarship rather than as a work in the formal social-science tradition of *Who Rules America?*
