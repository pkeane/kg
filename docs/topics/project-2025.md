# Who Created Project 2025?

Project 2025 is the umbrella name for a presidential-transition initiative organized by the Heritage Foundation and a coalition of allied conservative organizations, launched publicly in April 2022 in anticipation of a possible second Donald Trump administration. Its most-discussed product is *Mandate for Leadership: The Conservative Promise*, a roughly 900-page edited volume published by Heritage in April 2023 and edited by Paul Dans and Steven Groves, with a foreword by Heritage president Kevin Roberts. *Mandate for Leadership* is the ninth edition of a Heritage transition-document series that began in 1981 with the original *Mandate for Leadership* prepared for the incoming Reagan administration; the 2023 edition is by far the most extensive and the most explicitly oriented toward an executive-branch restructuring agenda.

The initiative had four announced pillars: the policy book (*Mandate*); a personnel database of vetted conservatives to be appointed to political and Schedule F positions; a "Presidential Administration Academy" to train those appointees; and a "180-Day Playbook" of day-by-day executive actions, kept largely private. Project 2025 became a 2024 campaign issue when Democratic and journalistic attention to specific chapters — abortion-pill restrictions, the dismantling of the Department of Education, the Schedule F revival, the proposed reorganization of the Department of Justice — produced wide public hostility to the project; Trump publicly disavowed it, project director Paul Dans resigned in late July 2024, and Heritage continued the work in lower-profile form. After Trump's November 2024 victory and 2025 inauguration, large portions of Project 2025's personnel and policy program were operationalized in early executive orders and appointments, including Russell Vought's return to OMB and the reinstatement of Schedule F in revised form (EO 14171, January 20, 2025).

The question of whether Project 2025 is a coherent intellectual project or, in essence, a patronage and policy-grab dressed in theoretical language is genuinely contested in serious commentary across the political spectrum. The honest reading is that it is both: the personnel database and the chapter-by-chapter agency-by-agency directives are the practical instrument of a transition that has clearly identifiable beneficiaries — the contributing organizations, the appointees, the donor coalition — and the framing apparatus draws on several distinct intellectual traditions (unitary-executive constitutionalism, post-liberal and "national" conservatism, Christian-nationalist political theology, Claremont-school Lincolnian-Straussian thought) without fully integrating them. Critics on the right (David French, Damon Linker, Yuval Levin) and on the left (Adam Serwer, Jamelle Bouie, Jonathan Chait) have produced sustained analyses; the entries below collect both the primary materials and the analytic reception.

## Annotated bibliography

### Primary documents

- **Paul Dans and Steven Groves (eds.)**, *Mandate for Leadership: The Conservative Promise* (Heritage Foundation, April 2023). The 920-page core text. Thirty chapters, each devoted to a department or agency, written by single authors or small teams drawn from Heritage and from allied institutions. The chapters are uneven in tone — some are technical policy memoranda, others are explicitly ideological — and the project's coherence is more institutional than intellectual: what unites them is the assumption of an incoming conservative administration committed to dismantling what the text calls "the administrative state" and consolidating executive control over agency personnel. Full PDF available on Heritage's website during the 2023–24 rollout; subsequently more restricted.
- **Kevin Roberts**, foreword to *Mandate for Leadership* (April 2023). Roberts frames the project as a "Second American Revolution" and articulates the four-pillar policy frame (family/community, economy, sovereignty, individual freedom). The foreword is the closest thing the document has to a unifying ideological statement.
- **Kevin Roberts**, *Dawn's Early Light: Taking Back Washington to Save America* (Broadside Books / HarperCollins, October 2024). Roberts's book-length argument, originally scheduled for September 2024 with a foreword by JD Vance; the foreword was removed and the publication date pushed back during the campaign-season backlash. The book extends the *Mandate* framing into a broader post-liberal indictment of American institutions.
- **The Project 2025 advisory board roster** — the public list of (at various dates) between 100 and 110 conservative organizations that endorsed the project. Heritage maintained the list at project2025.org through 2024; archived versions are preserved by SourceWatch (Center for Media and Democracy) and the Wayback Machine. The list is the clearest single document of the project's institutional coalition.
- **Project 2025 Personnel Database** — the online portal for vetted appointee candidates, run by Heritage. Inaccessible to outside researchers in operation; described in detail in journalistic accounts (Heidi Przybyla, Politico; Andy Kroll, ProPublica).

### Authors and project leadership

- **Kevin Roberts** — president of the Heritage Foundation since late 2021, formerly president of Wyoming Catholic College and the Texas Public Policy Foundation. Catholic, post-liberal-adjacent in self-presentation, integralist-curious. The chief public face of Project 2025 after Dans's resignation.
- **Paul Dans** — Project 2025 director from its April 2022 launch through his resignation in late July 2024, a transition lawyer who served in the Trump 1 Office of Personnel Management. Edited the *Mandate* volume with Groves.
- **Spencer Chretien** — associate director of Project 2025; Trump 1 special assistant to the president. Continued at Heritage after Dans's departure.
- **Steven Groves** — co-editor of *Mandate for Leadership*; Heritage's Margaret Thatcher Fellow, Trump 1 special assistant and deputy press secretary.
- **Russell Vought** — author of the Executive Office of the President chapter and one of the project's intellectual architects. Director of the Office of Management and Budget in Trump 1 (2020–21) and again in Trump 2 (2025– ). Founded the Center for Renewing America (CRA) in 2021. CRA's policy papers, especially Vought's writings on the "radical constitutional vision" and the dismantling of the administrative state, are the most explicit theoretical extension of the *Mandate* framework. The personnel pipeline from CRA into Trump 2 — Mark Paoletta, Wesley Denton, others — is one of the clearest cases of Project 2025 operationalized.
- **Chapter authors** (selected): **Christopher Miller** (Department of Defense; former acting Secretary of Defense, Trump 1); **Ben Carson** (Housing and Urban Development; former Secretary, Trump 1); **Roger Severino** (Department of Health and Human Services; Heritage's Domestic Policy Studies director); **Hans von Spakovsky** (election-administration sections; Heritage's election-law program); **Adam Candeub** (Federal Communications Commission); **Mandy Gunasekara** (Environmental Protection Agency; former EPA chief of staff, Trump 1); **William Perry Pendley** (Department of the Interior); **Jonathan Berry** (Department of Labor); **Bernard McNamee** (Federal Energy Regulatory Commission); **Diana Furchtgott-Roth** (Department of Energy); **Mark Paoletta** (OMB legal sections). The roster overlaps substantially with Trump 1 alumni and with Heritage / CRA / America First Legal staff.

### Funders and institutional backers

The Heritage Foundation is a 501(c)(3) organization that does not publicly disclose donors. The funding picture below is assembled from investigative reporting and from disclosure in donor and foundation 990s on the giving side. No single donor is documented as the "funder of Project 2025"; the picture is of a long-cultivated network rather than a discrete grant.

- **The Heritage Foundation** itself — annual revenue approximately $90–100 million across recent years (per its IRS Form 990 filings, available on ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer). Project 2025 was funded through Heritage's general operations, supplemented by specific gifts solicited for the initiative.
- **The Project 2025 advisory board organizations** — including the Center for Renewing America (Vought); America First Legal (Stephen Miller); the Conservative Partnership Institute (Mark Meadows, Cleta Mitchell); the Claremont Institute (Ryan Williams); the Family Research Council (Tony Perkins); Hillsdale College; Turning Point USA (Charlie Kirk); the Center for Immigration Studies; the Heartland Institute; the American Principles Project; and roughly a hundred others. Each is independently funded; the coalition functions as a federation rather than a single budgetary entity. SourceWatch and CMD's *Project 2025 Coalition Tracker* (2023–24) document the membership and the cross-organizational personnel overlap.
- **DonorsTrust and Donors Capital Fund** — donor-advised funds that function as the primary dark-money pass-through for the conservative philanthropic infrastructure. Documented (the nonprofit investigative outlet) and the Center for Media and Democracy have shown Heritage to be a regular recipient. Donor identities behind DonorsTrust contributions are not disclosed by design.
- **The Lynde and Harry Bradley Foundation** and **Bradley Impact Fund** — Milwaukee-based; longtime Heritage funders. Public 990s.
- **The Sarah Scaife Foundation** — Pittsburgh-based; Scaife-family money; consistent Heritage giving over decades.
- **The Diana Davis Spencer Foundation**, **the Adolph Coors Foundation**, and **the Searle Freedom Trust** — long-term Heritage donors documented in 990 filings.
- **The Leonard Leo network** — Marble Freedom Trust (recipient of the 2021 Barre Seid transfer of approximately $1.6 billion in shares of Tripp Lite), the 85 Fund, and affiliated vehicles. Leo's direct funding of Heritage is less documented than his funding of judicial-confirmation and Federalist Society-adjacent work, but several advisory-board organizations (CRA, America First Legal) have received Leo-network grants. Andy Kroll (ProPublica), Anna Massoglia (OpenSecrets), Heidi Przybyla (Politico), and the *Guardian*'s investigative partnership with Documented have produced the most detailed reporting.
- **Council for National Policy** — the umbrella networking organization through which much of the donor-and-organization coordination occurs. **Anne Nelson**, *Shadow Network: Media, Money, and the Secret Hub of the Radical Right* (Bloomsbury, 2019), is the foundational book on the CNP; the network it documents is recognizably the institutional precursor of the Project 2025 coalition.
- **Investigative outlets producing the bulk of the funding reporting** — Documented (Brendan Fischer, Andrew Perez); the Center for Media and Democracy / SourceWatch (Lisa Graves, Don Wiener); ProPublica (Andy Kroll, Justin Elliott, Andrea Bernstein); Politico (Heidi Przybyla); *Mother Jones* (Andy Kroll's earlier work, Nina Lakhani); the *Guardian* US (Stephanie Kirchgaessner, Peter Stone); OpenSecrets (Anna Massoglia, Brendan Quinn); *Rolling Stone* (Andy Kroll, Tim Dickinson).

### Theoretical underpinnings

The intellectual material the project draws on does not form a single doctrine. Four distinct currents are visible, sometimes in tension with one another.

**Unitary-executive constitutionalism**

- **Steven Calabresi and Christopher Yoo**, *The Unitary Executive: Presidential Power from Washington to Bush* (Yale, 2008). The Federalist Society-aligned legal-academic statement of the doctrine: Article II vests *the* executive power in *the* President, and Congress cannot constitutionally constrain that vesting by insulating administrators from presidential removal. The doctrinal foundation for the Schedule F program and for the *Mandate*'s personnel chapters.
- **Philip Hamburger**, *Is Administrative Law Unlawful?* (University of Chicago, 2014) and *The Administrative Threat* (Encounter, 2017). The longer revisionist project against the legitimacy of the modern administrative state, beyond the unitary-executive question. Hamburger is at Columbia Law; his Center for the Study of the Administrative State is funded substantially by Leo-network sources.
- **Russell Vought**, *A Christian and Constitutional Recovery of Our Republic* (Center for Renewing America position papers, 2021–24). Vought's articulation of "radical constitutionalism": the president as the sole accountable head of the executive branch, the administrative state as constitutionally illegitimate, the appropriations power as the lever to reshape it. Available at the CRA website.
- **Schedule F** — Executive Order 13957 (October 21, 2020), rescinded by Biden in January 2021, reinstated in revised form as "Schedule Policy/Career" by Executive Order 14171 (January 20, 2025). The operational mechanism the unitary-executive doctrine produces.

**National conservatism and post-liberalism**

- **Yoram Hazony**, *The Virtue of Nationalism* (Basic Books, 2018) and *Conservatism: A Rediscovery* (Regnery, 2022). The Edmund Burke Foundation's National Conservatism conferences (since 2019) are the principal venue at which Project 2025-adjacent figures (Vance, Hawley, Roberts, Vermeule) have articulated the post-liberal program in public.
- **Patrick Deneen**, *Why Liberalism Failed* (Yale, 2018) and *Regime Change: Toward a Postliberal Future* (Sentinel, 2023). The Notre Dame political theorist's case that classical liberalism is internally self-undermining and that a post-liberal "common-good conservatism" should replace it. Roberts has cited Deneen in speeches.
- **Adrian Vermeule**, *Common Good Constitutionalism: Recovering the Classical Legal Tradition* (Polity, 2022). The Harvard Law integralist's argument that constitutional interpretation should be substantively oriented toward the common good as classically understood, not toward originalist proceduralism. Adjacent to but not identical with Project 2025's working constitutionalism; sympathetic in spirit.
- **Sohrab Ahmari**, *Tyranny, Inc.: How Private Power Crushed American Liberty — and What to Do About It* (Forum Books, 2023). The post-liberal right's economic-populist current; argues for a stronger anti-corporate state from the right. Not formally part of Project 2025 but indicative of the broader intellectual context.

**Christian nationalism and political theology**

- **Stephen Wolfe**, *The Case for Christian Nationalism* (Canon Press, 2022). The most cited contemporary statement of an explicit Christian-nationalist political program. Wolfe is post-Reformed Presbyterian rather than Catholic; his version of the project differs from Roberts's Catholic-integralist sensibility, but the political alignment is substantial.
- **Andrew Whitehead and Samuel Perry**, *Taking America Back for God: Christian Nationalism in the United States* (Oxford, 2020). The sociologists' quantitative documentation of Christian nationalism as a measurable American political identity.
- **Katherine Stewart**, *The Power Worshippers: Inside the Dangerous Rise of Religious Nationalism* (Bloomsbury, 2019) and *Money, Lies, and God: Inside the Movement to Destroy American Democracy* (Bloomsbury, 2025). Investigative reporting on the institutional and financial infrastructure of religious-nationalist politics; *Money, Lies, and God* is the most direct journalistic treatment of the Project 2025 ecology to date.
- **Tim Alberta**, *The Kingdom, the Power, and the Glory: American Evangelicals in an Age of Extremism* (HarperCollins, 2023). Alberta is an evangelical Christian and former chief political correspondent at Politico; his account is from inside the world he reports on. Useful for understanding why the evangelical political mobilization has held under Trump despite the figure's evident distance from evangelical moral codes.
- **Sarah Posner**, *Unholy: How White Christian Nationalists Powered the Trump Presidency, and the Devastating Legacy They Left Behind* (Random House, 2020).
- **Talia Lavin**, *Wild Faith: How the Christian Right Is Taking Over America* (Hachette, 2024).
- **Albert Mohler** (Southern Baptist Theological Seminary) — the most institutionally significant Southern Baptist intellectual voice supportive of the project's Christian-nationalist framings; "The Briefing" podcast and *World Opinions*.

**The Claremont / Straussian current**

- **Michael Anton**, "The Flight 93 Election" (*Claremont Review of Books*, September 2016) and *The Stakes: America at the Point of No Return* (Regnery, 2020). Anton was a Trump 1 NSC official and is a Claremont senior fellow; his work is the most rhetorically explicit statement of the Claremont right's reading of the American crisis.
- **Glenn Ellmers**, "Conservatism Is No Longer Enough" (*American Mind*, March 2021) — the much-discussed essay arguing that most Americans are "not Americans in any meaningful sense" and that conservatism must give way to something more confrontational.
- **The Claremont Institute** — its Lincoln Fellows program is a significant pipeline for younger movement intellectuals; Ryan Williams is the current president. The Institute's relationship to Project 2025 is one of intellectual proximity rather than direct authorship; several Claremont-orbit figures appear in the personnel database.
- **Hillsdale College** — Larry Arnn, president; supplies a parallel training network. The Kirby Center in Washington links the Hillsdale-Claremont milieu to the policy-organization milieu.

### Reception and the "is it a grift?" question

The serious commentary divides not by left-right but by the prior question of *how seriously to take the document's own intellectual self-presentation*. The "grift" reading treats the unitary-executive framing as a rationalization for a patronage operation; the "serious theory" reading treats Project 2025 as the operational instrument of a coherent reactionary state-theory. Both readings have substantial supporting evidence.

- **Damon Linker**, "Notes from the Middleground" (Substack, 2022– ) and earlier *The Theocons: Secular America Under Siege* (Doubleday, 2006). Linker is the most thorough non-left analyst of the post-liberal right; he takes the intellectual content seriously while remaining outside it. The Substack's archive is the single most useful running commentary on the post-liberal-to-Project-2025 progression.
- **David French**, *New York Times* columns (2023– ) and his earlier *Divided We Fall* (St. Martin's, 2020). French is an evangelical conservative and former constitutional lawyer; his critique is from the inside-but-departed position. Reads Project 2025 as both ideologically coherent and substantively dangerous.
- **Yuval Levin** (American Enterprise Institute, *National Affairs* editor), various essays. Levin is a center-right institutionalist sympathetic to many of the right's diagnoses but explicitly opposed to the unitary-executive program. His engagement is the most informative right-of-center critique of the *Mandate*'s constitutional argument.
- **Ross Douthat**, *New York Times* columns (2023– ). Douthat takes the post-liberal intellectual current seriously and has been a measured interlocutor; his critique focuses on the gap between the post-liberal aspirations and the actual personnel and program of the second Trump administration.
- **Adam Serwer**, *The Atlantic* columns (2023– ) and *The Cruelty Is the Point* (One World, 2021). The single most-cited left-of-center analyst on Project 2025-as-state-project. Serwer reads the document as a serious authoritarian program whose intellectual scaffolding is real but secondary to its operational intent.
- **Jamelle Bouie**, *New York Times* columns and podcast (2023– ). Reads Project 2025 through American political-development frames; the racial and democratic-erosion dimensions; the document as continuous with longer Republican counter-majoritarian patterns.
- **Jonathan Chait**, *The Atlantic* (formerly *New York* magazine), various 2023–25. The skeptic-of-the-intellectual-pretensions position: Chait reads the post-liberal theory as a thin overlay on a fundamentally conventional Republican policy agenda of tax cuts, deregulation, and judicial conservatism, given an authoritarian shell.
- **Sarah Jones**, *New York* magazine, various 2024– . Reporting and analysis with particular attention to the Christian-nationalist dimension and to the lived experience of the affected populations.
- **Michelle Goldberg**, *New York Times* columns; her earlier *Kingdom Coming: The Rise of Christian Nationalism* (Norton, 2006) is one of the early statements of the longer arc into which Project 2025 fits.
- **Niskanen Center** — policy analyses, especially on the Schedule F and civil-service dimensions. The most detailed center-right policy critique.
- **The Bulwark** (Charlie Sykes, Sarah Longwell, Jonathan V. Last, Tim Miller, William Kristol) — the never-Trump conservative outlet's running coverage; *The Next Level* podcast is a useful weekly tracker.

### Implementation and the second term

Project 2025's transition from document to government has been documented in real time by political journalism since January 2025. The picture as of mid-2026 is partial and contested; for current developments, *New York Times*, *Washington Post*, *ProPublica*, *Politico*, *Government Executive*, and the *Federal News Network* are the principal sources.

- **Russell Vought** confirmed as OMB director (February 2025); the OMB has been the primary instrument of executive-branch restructuring.
- **Schedule F revival** — Executive Order 14171 ("Restoring Accountability to Policy-Influencing Positions Within the Federal Workforce"), January 20, 2025, creating the "Schedule Policy/Career" classification. Implementation through OPM rulemaking through 2025.
- **The Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE)** — created by executive order January 20, 2025, initially co-led by Elon Musk and Vivek Ramaswamy; Ramaswamy departed early 2025 for the Ohio governor's race; Musk's role ended with the public Trump-Musk break in May–June 2025. DOGE's relationship to the *Mandate*'s personnel and structural recommendations is partial — it operated outside the Heritage / CRA pipeline in important respects — but functionally advanced several of the same goals.
- **Agency-level rollbacks and reorganizations** — EPA (Lee Zeldin), HHS (Robert F. Kennedy Jr., a non-Project 2025 selection but operating partly with Project 2025 personnel), Department of Education (dismantling efforts under Linda McMahon), Department of Justice (Pam Bondi). Reporting by Lisa Friedman (NYT), Coral Davenport (NYT), Sheryl Gay Stolberg (NYT), and others tracks the chapter-by-chapter implementation.
- **The personnel database in operation** — the most opaque element, but the personnel patterns in agency staffing have been shown by ProPublica and Politico to track the database substantially.
- **Litigation tracking** — Just Security, Lawfare, and the Brennan Center for Justice maintain ongoing trackers of legal challenges to Schedule F and to specific agency actions.
