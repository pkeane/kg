# The Rise of MAGA — A Systems View

The election of Donald Trump in 2016, his loss to Joe Biden in 2020, his retention of Republican Party control during four years out of office, his return to the presidency with a popular-vote plurality and an expanded multi-racial coalition in 2024, and the consolidation of MAGA as the dominant ideological and organizational current of one of America's two major parties are not adequately explained by any single cause. The temptation to choose one — economic anxiety, racial backlash, Russian interference, Hillary Clinton's campaign errors, Comey, Facebook, Fox, inflation, Biden's late withdrawal, the post-COVID incumbent disadvantage — is itself the analytical problem. A **systems view** does not propose a deeper single cause; it proposes that the rise (and the second rise) is the product of several long-running subsystems whose trajectories happened to couple in a way that made the outcome much more probable than any one of them alone would have made it.

This page does not try to adjudicate the contributing weights, which remain contested in the political-science literature. It collects the subsystems and the scholarship that has documented each, and notes where the literature has converged and where it has not. The framing draws on the broader [[systems-theory-and-politics|systems-analytic tradition]] without committing to a Luhmannian or Eastonian vocabulary; the more proximate models are Theda Skocpol's "three-legged stool" account of the Tea Party and Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson's concept of plutocratic populism.

The interpretive payoff is straightforward. If MAGA's rise required the coupling of (roughly) seven or eight subsystems, then any opposition strategy that addresses only one — better messaging, better candidates, better fact-checking — is structurally insufficient. The same logic applies in the other direction: a left-populist movement that hopes to displace it (see [[democratic-socialism-and-scale]]) needs to either build its own equivalent of each subsystem or restructure the field on which they operate.

## Annotated bibliography

### The systems-view books on MAGA itself

- **Theda Skocpol and Vanessa Williamson**, *The Tea Party and the Remaking of Republican Conservatism* (2012) — the pre-Trump template. Three coupled components: grassroots activists organized through local chapters, a conservative media ecosystem (Fox, talk radio) that supplied them with frames and emotional fuel, and a national donor network (Koch-funded Americans for Prosperity, FreedomWorks) that supplied money and infrastructure. The argument is that all three were needed, and that no single one could have produced the movement. The methodological model for almost everything below.
- **Theda Skocpol and Caroline Tervo (eds.)**, *Upending American Politics: Polarizing Parties, Civic Resistance, and the Trump Presidency* (2020) — extends the Skocpol framework into the Trump period; adds the post-2016 "resistance" infrastructure as a counter-mobilization.
- **Yochai Benkler, Robert Faris, and Hal Roberts**, *Network Propaganda: Manipulation, Disinformation, and Radicalization in American Politics* (2018) — the most explicitly systems-analytic book on the rise. Large-scale network analysis of millions of news stories and links during the 2015–2018 cycle. Central empirical finding: the U.S. media system is **asymmetric**. The right-wing media ecosystem is structurally distinct from the centrist/left ecosystem (denser, more insular, more self-referential) and operates by different feedback dynamics. The asymmetry, not "social media" or "fake news" in general, is the relevant unit of analysis.
- **Jacob [[hacker-jacob|Hacker]] and Paul [[pierson-paul|Pierson]]**, *Let Them Eat Tweets: How the Right Rules in an Age of Extreme Inequality* (2020) — the **plutocratic populism** thesis. A coalition that pursues an economic agenda unpopular on its own merits (regressive taxation, deregulation, entitlement cuts) can be made durable by coupling it to an ethno-cultural grievance machinery that delivers votes for reasons unrelated to the economic agenda. Generalizes the U.S. case to right-wing parties across unequal democracies. Effectively a systems-level account, though not labeled as one.
- **Hacker and Pierson**, *Winner-Take-All Politics* (2010) — the prequel; documents the policy-and-institutional construction of the inequality conditions Trump exploited.

### The economic-shock subsystem

- **David [[autor-david|Autor]], David Dorn, and Gordon Hanson**, "The China Syndrome: Local Labor Market Effects of Import Competition in the United States," *American Economic Review* (2013), and the follow-up "Importing Political Polarization" (2020) — the empirical case that commuting-zone-level exposure to Chinese import competition predicts subsequent political polarization and Republican vote share, including Trump-specific gains. The most-cited piece of evidence for the "economic shock" element.
- **Anne [[case-anne|Case]] and Angus [[deaton-angus|Deaton]]**, *Deaths of Despair and the Future of Capitalism* (2020) — the mortality data. Rising mid-life mortality among non-college whites (suicide, alcohol, opioid overdose) concentrated in the same regions that swung hardest to Trump. Argues the underlying cause is the collapse of meaning-bearing working-class institutions.
- **Robert Wuthnow**, *The Left Behind: Decline and Rage in Rural America* (2018) — sociological account of small-town decline as a structure of feeling, not just an economic statistic.

### The cultural / demographic subsystem

- **Pippa [[norris-pippa|Norris]] and Ronald [[inglehart-ronald|Inglehart]]**, *Cultural Backlash: Trump, Brexit, and Authoritarian Populism* (2019) — comparative-quantitative. Treats Western right populism as a single cross-national phenomenon driven by long-run post-materialist value change and the counter-mobilization of older, less-educated cohorts who experience the change as cultural loss. The "silent revolution" provoked a "silent counter-revolution."
- **Michael [[tesler-michael|Tesler]]**, *Post-Racial or Most-Racial? Race and Politics in the Age of Obama* (2016) — quantitative evidence that racial attitudes became more strongly aligned with partisan vote choice during the Obama years, setting the conditions for Trump's coalition.
- **Robert P. [[jones-robert-p|Jones]]**, *The End of White Christian America* (2016) and *White Too Long* (2020) — the demographic-religious shift. White Christian share of the U.S. population dropped below 50% around 2014; the loss of demographic dominance is treated as a precipitating condition for the politics that followed.
- **Arlie Russell [[hochschild-arlie|Hochschild]]**, *Strangers in Their Own Land: Anger and Mourning on the American Right* (2016) — ethnographic. The "deep story" frame — being cut in front of in line — supplies the qualitative texture the quantitative accounts compress.
- **Katherine J. [[cramer-katherine|Cramer]]**, *The Politics of Resentment: Rural Consciousness in Wisconsin and the Rise of Scott Walker* (2016) — place-based identity, the rural-urban resentment dynamic that maps onto the partisan one.

### The media subsystem

- **Yochai Benkler et al.**, *Network Propaganda* (2018) — as above. The central reference.
- **Reece Peck**, *Fox Populism: Branding Conservatism as Working Class* (2019) — focused study of Fox News's branding strategy and its construction of an oppositional "real America."
- **Kathleen Hall Jamieson**, *Cyberwar: How Russian Hackers and Trolls Helped Elect a President* (2018) — the case for nontrivial Russian-information-operations impact, methodologically careful about what can and cannot be claimed.
- **Jen Schradie**, *The Revolution That Wasn't: How Digital Activism Favors Conservatives* (2019) — the under-noticed argument that digital-organizing infrastructure tends to advantage right-wing movements with hierarchical organizational forms over left-wing ones with horizontal forms.
- **Eitan Hersh**, *Politics Is for Power* (2020) — adjacent; the critique of "political hobbyism" as the dominant mode of left engagement and a structural disadvantage against organized right mobilization.

### The plutocratic / donor subsystem

- **Jane [[mayer-jane|Mayer]]**, *Dark Money: The Hidden History of the Billionaires Behind the Rise of the Radical Right* (2016) — investigative history of the Koch donor network and the construction of the conservative donor-and-think-tank infrastructure from the 1970s.
- **Nancy MacLean**, *Democracy in Chains: The Deep History of the Radical Right's Stealth Plan for America* (2017) — controversial in its specifics but influential on the argument that the modern right's institutional strategy has a coherent intellectual origin in James Buchanan and the Virginia school of public choice.
- **Hacker and Pierson**, *Let Them Eat Tweets* (2020) — as above; the systems-level synthesis.

### The party subsystem

- **Daniel Schlozman and Sam Rosenfeld**, *The Hollow Parties: The Many Pasts and Disordered Present of American Party Politics* (2024) — the central recent statement. American parties are *organizationally* hollow — porous, easily captured by movements, incapable of sustained programmatic discipline — and that hollowness is what allowed Trump to take over the GOP. The book is also useful for the symmetrical argument about what the Democratic Party could and could not do in response.
- **Geoffrey Kabaservice**, *Rule and Ruin* (2012) — the long history of the moderate Republican Party's destruction from the Goldwater insurgency forward.
- **Sam Tanenhaus**, *The Death of Conservatism* (2009) and his subsequent essays — the longer ideological-historical frame.
- **E.J. Dionne, Norman Ornstein, and Thomas Mann**, *One Nation After Trump* (2017) and the earlier *It's Even Worse Than It Looks* (2012) — institutionalist accounts of GOP asymmetric polarization.

### The institutional subsystem

- **Steven [[levitsky-steven|Levitsky]] and Daniel [[ziblatt-daniel|Ziblatt]]**, *How Democracies Die* (2018) — comparative; treats the erosion of guardrail norms (mutual toleration, institutional forbearance) as the mechanism of contemporary democratic backsliding. Trump as case rather than cause.
- **Levitsky and Ziblatt**, *Tyranny of the Minority: Why American Democracy Reached the Breaking Point* (2023) — the counter-majoritarian institutions of the U.S. system (Electoral College, Senate, judiciary, filibuster) amplify minority rule when a major party radicalizes. Essential for the question of why a coalition that has rarely won a popular majority for the presidency since 1988 nonetheless governs.
- **Jacob Grumbach**, *Laboratories Against Democracy* (2022) — state-level evidence that GOP policy radicalism has been nationally coordinated and concentrated where institutional vulnerabilities (gerrymandered legislatures, weak unions) allowed it.

### The comparative populism subsystem

- **Cas [[mudde-cas|Mudde]]**, *The Far Right Today* (2019) — the standard contemporary mapping of European and global far-right parties; Trump as one node in a transnational family.
- **Anne [[applebaum-anne|Applebaum]]**, *Twilight of Democracy: The Seductive Lure of Authoritarianism* (2020) — the *clerc-trahi* argument; why educated conservatives became authoritarian sympathizers.
- **Federico Finchelstein**, *From Fascism to Populism in History* (2017) — comparative-historical; the relationship between interwar fascism and 21st-century populism, careful about both continuities and breaks.

### The 2024 reelection and the second term

The 2024 election added analytical problems the 2016 literature had not anticipated. Trump won the popular vote (the first Republican to do so since 2004) by roughly 1.5 points, with measurable gains among Latino voters (especially men), Asian American voters, Black men under 45, and young voters of all races. The post-2016 framework of "white working-class backlash" did not adequately describe a coalition that had become substantially more multi-racial than the 2016 version. The post-2020 framework of Trump as a defeated and discrediting figure did not describe a candidate who returned with a popular-vote plurality and unified Republican control of Congress.

- **Patrick Ruffini**, *Party of the People: Inside the Multiracial Populist Coalition Remaking the GOP* (2023) — the most-cited pre-2024 account of what was already visible in 2020 county-level results and in 2022 midterm patterns: a class-realignment of non-white working-class voters toward the Republican Party, especially among men. The 2024 result largely confirmed his projection.
- **Ruy Teixeira and John Judis**, *Where Have All the Democrats Gone?* (2023) — the auto-critique of their own 2002 *Emerging Democratic Majority* thesis. The professional-class capture of the Democratic Party drove non-college voters of all races away. Read alongside Ruffini.
- **Tim Alberta**, *The Kingdom, the Power, and the Glory: American Evangelicals in an Age of Extremism* (2023) — the evangelical consolidation behind Trump as a movement rather than an electoral bloc. Useful for the second-term context in which Christian-nationalist framings became governmentally operative rather than rhetorical.
- **John Ganz**, *When the Clock Broke: Con Men, Conspiracists, and How America Cracked Up in the Early 1990s* (2024) — pre-history of MAGA in the Buchanan / Perot / militia milieu of 1991–1994. Useful for placing 2024 in a longer arc than the 2016-as-origin framing assumes.
- **The "tech right" literature** — *not yet consolidated in book form* as of mid-2026, but the alignment of Elon Musk, Marc Andreessen, Peter Thiel, David Sacks, and the broader Silicon Valley-adjacent venture-capital class with Trump in 2024 added a donor and media subsystem that did not exist in 2016. Key primary documents: Andreessen's "Techno-Optimist Manifesto" (October 2023), Musk's October 2022 acquisition of Twitter and subsequent reshaping of its algorithmic ranking, and the *All-In* podcast as the network hub. Quinn Slobodian's *Hayek's Bastards: The Populist Right's Neoliberal Roots* (2025) provides the closest book-length intellectual genealogy.
- **Project 2025** (Heritage Foundation, *Mandate for Leadership: The Conservative Promise*, 2023) — the governmental blueprint executed in the first months of the second term. Read as the institutional artifact through which the donor and think-tank subsystems made the second term substantively different from the first, which had lacked an equivalent playbook.
- **Anne Applebaum**, *Autocracy, Inc.: The Dictators Who Want to Run the World* (2024) — extends the comparative frame to the post-2024 environment; treats the U.S. second term as one node in a transnational authoritarian network with material economic ties.
- **Inflation and the post-COVID incumbent problem.** Comparative work (especially by political economist John Burn-Murdoch in his *Financial Times* columns, drawing on the Bank for International Settlements and the V-Dem data) showed that *every* incumbent party in the developed world that faced election in 2024 lost vote share; the U.S. was a particularly severe instance of a global pattern. This complicates strictly American explanations of the 2024 result. **Larry Bartels**, "How the World's Voters Punished Incumbents in 2024" (*Journal of Democracy*, 2025), is the political-science version.

The systems-frame reading of 2024: the subsystems documented above (economic, cultural, media, party, plutocratic, institutional) had continued to operate during the Biden interregnum, in some cases intensifying (the tech-right alignment, the Project 2025 institutional preparation), while the Democratic coalition's countervailing subsystems weakened (declining union density continuing, professional-class capture deepening, no equivalent media-ecosystem construction). The post-COVID inflation cycle then supplied the proximate trigger that an already-tilted system converted into a popular-vote win. The 2024 result is not a separate phenomenon from the 2016 rise; it is the continuation of the same subsystem dynamics, with one or two subsystems strengthened.

### Skeptical and revisionist accounts

- **Musa al-Gharbi**, *We Have Never Been Woke* (2024) — argues the "symbolic capitalist" professional-class diagnosis of Trump voters as racially resentful is itself a class-interested misreading; complicates the cultural-backlash literature from inside the academy that produced it.
- **Matt Karp**, "The Politics of a Second Gilded Age" (*Jacobin*, 2017) and subsequent essays — class-first reading; argues that the racial-backlash thesis underweights material conditions and over-credits a particular professional-class narrative.
- **Larry Bartels and Christopher Achen**, *Democracy for Realists* (2016) — pre-Trump but central. Voters are not issue-driven rational deliberators; they are group-identity members whose policy preferences follow their group attachments. The cognitive-substructure account behind much of the literature above.

### Synthesizing reflections

- **Adam Tooze**, *Crashed: How a Decade of Financial Crises Changed the World* (2018) — the 2008 financial-crisis aftermath as the macroeconomic environment for Trump and the European far right; treats the populist wave as the downstream political consequence of an inadequately resolved financial crisis.
- **Wolfgang Streeck**, *Buying Time: The Delayed Crisis of Democratic Capitalism* (2014) and *How Will Capitalism End?* (2016) — the systems-level diagnosis from the German political-economy tradition: the tension between capitalist accumulation and democratic legitimacy is structural, and the contemporary right populism is one of the symptoms of that tension breaking through.

## The unresolved questions

- *Weighting.* The political-science literature has not converged on the relative weights of the subsystems. Reasonable scholars assign primary responsibility to different ones, and the field's empirical methods are not adequate to settle the dispute.
- *Counterfactuals.* Whether 2016 would have gone differently without Comey, without Russia, without the Electoral College, without Cambridge Analytica, is genuinely uncertain. The systems frame's strength is that it does not require these counterfactuals to make its argument; its weakness is that it can absorb almost any new finding without revision.
- *Charisma and contingency.* Trump's particular media talent and the 2016 primary's fragmentation are residuals the systems frame underweights. Honest treatments acknowledge them rather than absorbing them into structure.
- *Endogeneity.* Several subsystems are themselves outputs of earlier choices by other subsystems (donor networks built the media ecosystem; the media ecosystem reshaped voter preferences; voter preferences reshaped the party). Disentangling them empirically is genuinely hard.
