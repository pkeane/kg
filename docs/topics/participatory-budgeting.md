# Participatory Budgeting

Participatory budgeting (PB) is a process in which residents of a city, district, or institution directly decide how to allocate a portion of a public budget through a structured cycle of neighborhood assemblies, delegate meetings, and a final vote. The model was developed in **Porto Alegre, Brazil** beginning in 1989 under the Workers' Party (PT) administration of mayor Olívio Dutra, and was institutionalized over the following decade. The United Nations included Porto Alegre's PB among its "best practices" in urban governance at the 1996 Habitat II conference in Istanbul.

The Porto Alegre model allocated a share of the municipal capital budget — typically infrastructure spending such as paving, sewerage, and school construction — through annual regional and thematic assemblies. Delegates elected at the assemblies negotiated priorities with the municipal administration and ratified the final plan. By the late 1990s, tens of thousands of residents were participating each year, and Brazilian and international observers reported that the process disproportionately drew participation from lower-income neighborhoods. The Porto Alegre process declined after the PT lost the mayoralty in 2004 and was effectively dismantled by subsequent administrations.

The model spread first to other Brazilian cities, then across Latin America, and from the early 2000s to Europe (notably Spain, Portugal, Italy, France, Germany, and Poland), North America, and parts of Africa and Asia. Estimates by the World Bank and by Yves Sintomer and collaborators put the number of municipalities running some form of PB worldwide at well over 7,000 by the mid-2010s. The first U.S. PB was in Chicago's 49th Ward in 2009, organized by Alderman Joe Moore with technical support from the Participatory Budgeting Project; New York City began PB in city council districts in 2011 and at one point ran the largest PB process in North America.

The literature treats PB as a case in several overlapping debates: on deliberative and participatory democracy ([[habermas-jurgen]], [[dewey-john]], Carole Pateman, Archon Fung); on the relationship between popular mobilization and the state in Latin America's "Pink Tide" period; on the institutional design of common-pool and public-good provision (Elinor Ostrom); and on the limits of participation when the share of the budget under deliberation is small or when redistributive authority is held elsewhere. The case is also discussed under [[post-neoliberal-political-economy]] as one of the more institutionally elaborated experiments in democratic economic decision-making.

## Annotated bibliography

### Foundational case studies of Porto Alegre

- **Rebecca Abers**, *Inventing Local Democracy: Grassroots Politics in Brazil* (2000). One of the first English-language monographs on Porto Alegre. Abers reconstructs the formation of the assemblies, the role of neighborhood associations, and the conditions under which low-income residents took up the new institutional channels.
- **Gianpaolo Baiocchi**, *Militants and Citizens: The Politics of Participatory Democracy in Porto Alegre* (2005). An ethnographic study based on extended fieldwork with assembly participants. Baiocchi documents how the process selected and trained delegates, how disputes between neighborhoods were resolved, and how PT militants and unaffiliated residents interacted within the assemblies.
- **Boaventura de Sousa Santos**, "Participatory Budgeting in Porto Alegre: Toward a Redistributive Democracy," *Politics & Society* 26:4 (1998). The early influential theoretical framing, which presented Porto Alegre as a case of "high-intensity democracy" and tied it to the Workers' Party's broader political project.

### Comparative and global studies

- **Brian Wampler**, *Participatory Budgeting in Brazil: Contestation, Cooperation, and Accountability* (2007) and *Activating Democracy in Brazil: Popular Participation, Social Justice, and Interlocking Institutions* (2015). Wampler compares PB outcomes across Brazilian municipalities and identifies institutional and political conditions — mayoral commitment, civil-society density, design choices — under which the process produces measurable redistributive effects.
- **Yves Sintomer, Carsten Herzberg, and Anja Röcke**, *Participatory Budgeting in Europe: Democracy and Public Governance* (2016). The standard comparative study of PB's European diffusion, with a typology distinguishing Porto Alegre-style redistributive PB from "consultative," "proximity," and "neoliberal-modernization" variants in which the process is detached from redistributive aims.
- **Anwar Shah** (ed.), *Participatory Budgeting* (World Bank, 2007). A practitioner-oriented volume that introduced PB to development-policy audiences and accelerated diffusion through World Bank technical assistance programs.
- **Michael Touchton and Brian Wampler**, "Improving Social Well-Being Through New Democratic Institutions," *Comparative Political Studies* 47:10 (2014). Quantitative study reporting that Brazilian municipalities running PB show higher spending on sanitation and health and lower infant mortality, controlling for confounding variables.

### Theoretical frames

- **Archon Fung** and **Erik Olin Wright** (eds.), *Deepening Democracy: Institutional Innovations in Empowered Participatory Governance* (2003). Places Porto Alegre alongside the Kerala People's Plan Campaign and Chicago community policing and school councils. Fung and Wright propose a framework — "Empowered Participatory Governance" — emphasizing practical orientation, bottom-up deliberation, and state-backed devolution of authority.
- **Carole Pateman**, "Participatory Democracy Revisited," *Perspectives on Politics* 10:1 (2012). Returns to the participatory-democracy tradition associated with her *Participation and Democratic Theory* (1970) and treats PB as the most institutionally elaborated contemporary instance of the position.
- **Hélène Landemore**, *Open Democracy: Reinventing Popular Rule for the Twenty-First Century* (2020). Argues for "open democracy" built around lottery-selected mini-publics, deliberative assemblies, and participatory institutions including PB; treats Porto Alegre as evidence that ordinary citizens can govern complex budgetary questions.
- **Josh Lerner**, *Making Democracy Fun: How Game Design Can Empower Citizens and Transform Politics* (2014). Lerner, a co-founder of the U.S. Participatory Budgeting Project, draws on extensive practitioner experience to analyze how procedural design choices shape participation rates and demographic representativeness.

### Critical assessments

- **Benjamin Goldfrank**, *Deepening Local Democracy in Latin America: Participation, Decentralization, and the Left* (2011). Comparative study of PB-style experiments in Porto Alegre, Caracas, and Montevideo. Goldfrank argues that PB requires sustained left-of-center political support, dense civil society, and a meaningful share of the budget to retain its redistributive character; absent these conditions it tends to drift toward consultation.
- **Ernesto Ganuza** and **Gianpaolo Baiocchi**, "The Power of Ambiguity: How Participatory Budgeting Travels the Globe," *Journal of Public Deliberation* 8:2 (2012). Argues that PB's global diffusion has been enabled by the procedural form's detachability from its original redistributive politics, and that many adopting jurisdictions use PB to legitimate small-scale consultation rather than redistribute power.
- **Donata Secondo** and **Pam Jennings**, "Participatory Budgeting in the United States: Lessons Learned" (working papers and reports through the Participatory Budgeting Project, 2010s). Document the recurring U.S. pattern of PB processes governing only discretionary capital allocations within council districts, typically 1–2% of municipal capital spending.

### Background and adjacent literatures

- **Elinor Ostrom**, *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action* (1990). Not on PB directly, but the design principles Ostrom identifies — clearly defined boundaries, congruence between rules and local conditions, collective-choice arenas, graduated sanctions — are routinely cited in the PB design literature.
- **[[habermas-jurgen|Jürgen Habermas]]**, *Between Facts and Norms* (1992). The standard contemporary statement of deliberative democracy as a normative framework, frequently cited as theoretical background for PB even though Habermas himself did not write on the case.
- **[[dewey-john|John Dewey]]**, *The Public and Its Problems* (1927). The classical American statement of the position that democracy is a way of life realized through local publics, not only a method for selecting representatives. Cited by Fung, Wright, and Landemore among others as background.
