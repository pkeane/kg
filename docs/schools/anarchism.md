# Anarchism

The political tradition that holds the state — and coercive hierarchy more generally — to be both unnecessary and illegitimate, and that aims at a society coordinated through voluntary association rather than through command. Pierre-Joseph Proudhon was the first to adopt the word as a self-description, in *What Is Property?* (1840); over the following century anarchism developed less as a single doctrine than as a family of positions sharing an anti-statism and, in most cases, an anti-capitalism.

Within the 19th-century socialist movement, anarchism defined itself against [[marx-karl|Marx]]. The conflict between Mikhail Bakunin and Marx over the role of the state and of a centralized party split the First International in 1872: where Marxists held that the proletariat must capture state power before dissolving it, anarchists argued that a revolutionary state would entrench a new ruling class rather than wither away. The tradition then differentiated internally — mutualism (Proudhon), collectivist anarchism (Bakunin), anarchist communism ([[kropotkin-peter|Kropotkin]]), anarcho-syndicalism (revolutionary unions as both the means of struggle and the skeleton of the future society), individualist anarchism (Max Stirner, Benjamin Tucker), and Christian anarchism ([[tolstoy-leo|Tolstoy]]). Anarcho-capitalism, a mid-20th-century American development, applies the anti-statism to the defense of private property and markets; the older tradition, which is anti-capitalist, does not generally recognize it as continuous with itself.

Anarchism was a mass movement in parts of Europe and the Americas between the 1870s and the 1930s, with strongholds in Spain, Italy, Russia, Argentina, and the immigrant labor movement of the United States. A wave of assassinations in the 1890s — "propaganda of the deed" — gave the movement its lasting public image and provoked sustained state repression. The Spanish Revolution of 1936, in which the anarcho-syndicalist CNT-FAI collectivized large parts of Catalonia and Aragon, was the high-water mark of organized anarchism; [[orwell-george|Orwell]] documented its defeat in *Homage to Catalonia* (1938). Eclipsed after the Second World War by social democracy and Marxism-Leninism, anarchism revived from the 1960s onward — through [[bookchin-murray|Murray Bookchin]]'s social ecology, the anti-globalization movement, the writing of [[graeber-david|David Graeber]], and the assembly forms of Occupy.

## Key strands

- Mutualism (Proudhon)
- Collectivist anarchism (Bakunin)
- Anarchist communism (Kropotkin)
- Anarcho-syndicalism
- Individualist anarchism (Stirner, Tucker)
- Christian anarchism (Tolstoy)
