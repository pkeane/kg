# Atari Democrats

Journalistic label that surfaced in the early 1980s for a cohort of younger congressional Democrats who broke with the party's New Deal industrial and labor framework and proposed instead a strategy organized around high technology, productivity, human capital, fiscal restraint, and market-oriented regulatory reform. The named members in most accounts are Gary Hart (Colorado), Paul Tsongas (Massachusetts), Tim Wirth (Colorado), Al Gore (Tennessee), Bill Bradley (New Jersey), Bruce Babbitt (Arizona), and Michael Dukakis (Massachusetts). The name — after the video-game manufacturer — is generally attributed to Chris Matthews, then an aide to Speaker Tip O'Neill, though sourcing for the first use is uneven.

The "neoliberal" self-description was made explicit by Charles Peters in "A Neoliberal's Manifesto" (*The Washington Monthly*, May 1983) and given book-length contemporary treatment in Randall Rothenberg, *The Neoliberals: Creating the New American Politics* (Simon & Schuster, 1984). Peters's use of the term is specifically American and pre-dates the now-dominant Hayek/Mont Pèlerin sense ([[neoliberalism]]); it referred to liberals who accepted markets and competition as legitimate organizing tools while retaining liberal ends. Patrick Andelic's *Donkey Work: Congressional Democrats in Conservative America, 1974–1994* (University Press of Kansas, 2019) treats the Atari/neoliberal group as the third faction inside the post-Watergate Democratic caucus, alongside northern liberals and southern conservatives, and argues the split left Jimmy Carter without a stable majority for an old-Democratic-style program — relevant background for [[what-should-carter-have-done-instead]].

The standard policy texts of the formation are Lester Thurow, *The Zero-Sum Society* (Basic, 1980) and [[reich-robert|Robert Reich]], *The Next American Frontier* (Times Books, 1983), both of which argued for an active national strategy for high-value manufacturing and human-capital investment in place of either New Deal industrial defense or pure market deregulation. The line of institutional descent runs from this congressional cohort through the Democratic Leadership Council (founded 1985 under Al From) to the Bill Clinton administration; the post-2016 [[post-neoliberal-politics-in-america|post-neoliberal turn]] inside the Democratic Party is in large part an argument with this inheritance.

## Features

- High technology and information industries as the engine of growth
- Productivity and human-capital investment over industrial-base defense
- Fiscal restraint; suspicion of deficit-financed stimulus
- Acceptance of deregulation in transport, finance, and communications
- Skepticism of the AFL-CIO industrial-union model and its claims on Democratic policy

## Secondary sources

- Charles Peters, "A Neoliberal's Manifesto," *The Washington Monthly* (May 1983) — the founding self-description.
- Randall Rothenberg, *The Neoliberals: Creating the New American Politics* (Simon & Schuster, 1984) — contemporary book-length portrait.
- Patrick Andelic, *Donkey Work: Congressional Democrats in Conservative America, 1974–1994* (University Press of Kansas, 2019) — congressional history that places the Atari cohort as one of three factions in the post-Watergate caucus.
- Lily Geismer, *Left Behind: The Democrats' Failed Attempt to Solve Inequality* (PublicAffairs, 2022) — traces the line from this 1980s formation through the Clinton-era "New Democrats" to the contemporary Democratic policy argument.
- Matt Stoller, *Goliath: The 100-Year War Between Monopoly Power and Democracy* (Simon & Schuster, 2019) — read this as the anti-monopoly Brandeisian critique of the Atari/neoliberal trajectory.
