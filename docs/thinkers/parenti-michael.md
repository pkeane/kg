# Michael Parenti

American political scientist, lecturer, and author working in an unreconstructed Marxist tradition focused on class power, US foreign policy, and the political economy of mass media. Born 1933 in East Harlem to an Italian-American working-class family, Parenti took his PhD in political science at Yale in 1962 and taught at the University of Illinois, Sarah Lawrence, Howard, the University of Vermont, and elsewhere. He was denied tenure at Vermont in 1972, a decision he and supporters attributed to his anti-Vietnam War organizing; he subsequently left academic employment and built a career as an independent scholar, full-time author, and lecture-circuit speaker. He ran for Congress in Vermont in 1974 on the Liberty Union Party ticket, the same small left party that ran Bernie Sanders for statewide office in the same period.

Parenti's textbook *Democracy for the Few* (first edition 1974; now in a tenth edition published by Cengage) is his most widely assigned work, used in undergraduate American politics courses for half a century. The book argues, against the pluralist consensus then dominant in American political science, that US political institutions function primarily to protect concentrated wealth and corporate power, and that formal democratic procedures coexist with — and partly conceal — a structural oligarchy. *Inventing Reality: The Politics of News Media* (1986) and *Make-Believe Media: The Politics of Entertainment* (1992) extend the analysis to journalism and Hollywood, treating mass-media output as the product of corporate ownership, advertiser dependence, and ideological convention rather than of professional neutrality. The argument runs parallel to, but is developed independently of, the propaganda-model account [[chomsky-noam|Chomsky]] and Edward Herman published in *Manufacturing Consent* (1988).

A second body of work concerns US empire and its historiography. *Against Empire* (1995) and *The Face of Imperialism* (2011) treat post-Cold-War US military and economic intervention as a continuous imperial project rather than a series of discrete crises. *Blackshirts and Reds: Rational Fascism and the Overthrow of Communism* (1997) defends the historical record of 20th-century communist states against what Parenti calls "anti-communist" historiography, and argues that the dismantling of Eastern Bloc economies after 1989 produced large declines in living standards that Western commentary minimized. His *To Kill a Nation: The Attack on Yugoslavia* (2000) is a sustained polemic against the NATO intervention in Kosovo and against the Western press treatment of Slobodan Milošević; this work, and Parenti's broader defense of state-socialist regimes, has been the most contested part of his record and has separated him from much of the post-1989 academic Left. *The Assassination of Julius Caesar: A People's History of Ancient Rome* (2003) applies the class-conflict framework to the late Roman Republic, reading Caesar as a reformer killed by the Senatorial oligarchy.

Parenti is the father of the journalist and historian Christian Parenti (*Lockdown America*, *Tropic of Chaos*). His lectures, distributed first on community radio and Pacifica, have had a long second life on YouTube.

## Key themes

- US political institutions as instruments of concentrated wealth, not neutral arbiters
- Mass media as ideologically patterned by corporate ownership and advertiser dependence
- US foreign policy as a continuous imperial project across the Cold War and post-Cold-War periods
- Defense of the historical record of state-socialist regimes against what he calls anti-communist orthodoxy
- Class as the organizing axis of political analysis, with race and gender treated as articulated through it rather than displacing it
