# Ted Nelson

American sociologist, philosopher, and information-technology pioneer (Theodore Holm Nelson, born 17 June 1937, Chicago). He coined the words "hypertext" and "hypermedia" around 1963, publishing them in 1965, to name non-sequential writing in which a reader follows links among documents rather than a single fixed order.

In 1960 Nelson began Project Xanadu, a design for a universal networked document store, or "docuverse." Its distinctive features were two-way links that could not break, "transclusion" (including content in a document by reference to its original source rather than by copying), version management, and a built-in micropayment and attribution scheme he called transcopyright. Xanadu was never completed in the form Nelson intended and became a long-running example of unrealized software ambition. Nelson has been a sustained critic of the World Wide Web, which implemented one-way, breakable links and, in his view, merely simulated paper rather than realizing the deeper structure of connected writing.

His double book *Computer Lib / Dream Machines* (1974), self-published and printed back-to-back, argued that ordinary people should understand and control computers; it became an influential text in personal-computing culture. *Literary Machines* (1981) is the fullest statement of the Xanadu design. Nelson coined "intertwingularity" for his claim that knowledge is so deeply interconnected that hierarchical and sequential structures misrepresent it — a position that places him, with Vannevar Bush and Douglas [[engelbart-douglas|Engelbart]], among the principal sources of the networked, link-based approach to knowledge that runs through the [[tools-for-thought]] tradition.

## Key ideas

- **Hypertext** and **hypermedia**: non-sequential, link-connected writing (terms he coined)
- **Project Xanadu**: a universal docuverse with unbreakable two-way links, transclusion, and versioning
- **Transclusion** and **transcopyright**: quoting by live reference, with attribution and micropayment built in
- **Intertwingularity**: the claim that knowledge resists hierarchical and sequential ordering

## Key works

- *Computer Lib / Dream Machines* (1974)
- *Literary Machines* (1981)
