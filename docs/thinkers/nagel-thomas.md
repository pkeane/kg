# Thomas Nagel

American philosopher, University Professor at NYU, whose work has ranged across philosophy of mind, ethics, political philosophy, and meta-philosophy. He is known for two short essays — "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974) and "Moral Luck" (1979) — and for a series of books that recur to the limits of objective description and the irreducible weight of the first-person standpoint.

"What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (*Philosophical Review* 83:4, 1974) argues that subjective experience is irreducible to any third-person description. There is something it is like to be a bat — the felt character of bat experience, structured by echolocation in a way utterly unlike anything human — and no amount of objective neurophysiological information about bat brains will tell us what that is. The argument generalizes: physicalism cannot be vindicated by showing that mental phenomena reduce to physical ones, because the subjective character of experience is precisely what such a reduction would have to capture and cannot. The essay frames the territory David Chalmers would later name the "hard problem" of consciousness.

*The View from Nowhere* (Oxford, 1986) is Nagel's most systematic statement of the broader theme. The human standpoint, he argues, is doubly constituted: by the subjective, engaged perspective of the individual living their life, and by the objective, detached perspective the mind can take on itself and the world. Neither perspective is reducible to the other; living a human life means continuously negotiating the tension between them. Ethics, free will, the meaning of life, the prospects of physicalism — all turn on how this tension is handled, and Nagel argues that it cannot be dissolved in either direction.

His political philosophy (*The Possibility of Altruism*, 1970; *Equality and Partiality*, 1991) develops a related theme: morality requires the partial weight of one's own life and projects to be made answerable to an impartial standpoint without being collapsed into it. *Mind and Cosmos* (Oxford, 2012) presents a controversial argument that neo-Darwinian reductionism cannot accommodate consciousness, cognition, or value, and that some form of teleological natural law is required; the book was received critically by the philosophical mainstream.

Nagel's argument that the first-person standpoint cannot be eliminated from a complete account of the world places him in the family of positions surveyed in [[situated-knowledge]], though he arrives there from analytic philosophy of mind rather than from phenomenology or feminist epistemology.

## Key ideas

- The irreducibility of subjective experience to objective description
- "What is it like to be...?" as test of phenomenal consciousness
- The view from nowhere and the view from somewhere as ineliminable poles
- Agent-relative reasons and the partiality of moral life
- Moral luck — the dependence of moral judgment on factors outside the agent's control
- Critique of neo-Darwinian reductionism (*Mind and Cosmos*)
