# Emmanuel Saez & Gabriel Zucman

Two economists at the University of California, Berkeley whose joint and individual work has supplied much of the empirical and technical apparatus behind contemporary proposals to tax wealth. Emmanuel Saez (b. 1972), winner of the John Bates Clark Medal in 2009, built the modern series of U.S. top-income shares with [[piketty-thomas|Thomas Piketty]]. Gabriel Zucman (b. 1986), Clark Medalist in 2023, specializes in the measurement of hidden and offshore wealth. With Piketty they developed "distributional national accounts," a method that allocates the whole of national income across the population so that the distribution adds up to macroeconomic totals rather than relying on survey samples that miss the very top.

Zucman's *The Hidden Wealth of Nations* (2015) estimated that roughly eight percent of global household financial wealth is held in tax havens, much of it unrecorded, and argued for a global financial registry to make such holdings visible and taxable. Their joint book *The Triumph of Injustice* (2019) — subtitled *How the Rich Dodge Taxes and How to Make Them Pay* — moves from documentation to mechanism. Where most arguments for taxing the wealthy stop at the moral or distributional case, Saez and Zucman specify instruments: an annual progressive wealth tax assessed on net worth (they designed the proposal Elizabeth Warren ran on in 2020), mark-to-market valuation of assets, a national wealth registry, an "exit tax" on capital that attempts to expatriate, country-by-country corporate reporting, and a minimum corporate tax to close the offshoring channel. They argue that the historical decline in tax progressivity is a policy choice, not a technical inevitability, and that enforcement capacity — a rebuilt IRS, automatic international information exchange — is as decisive as statutory rates.

Their headline empirical claims are contested. The finding that in 2018 the four hundred richest Americans paid a lower total tax rate than the bottom half of households drew methodological objections from economists including Lawrence Summers and Natasha Sarin over the treatment of tax incidence and government transfers, and the related top-income series has been challenged by Gerald Auten and David Splinter, who find a smaller rise in top shares after adjustments. Revenue estimates for the Warren wealth tax were likewise disputed. The disputes concern magnitude and feasibility rather than the basic direction of the trend. More recently Zucman authored a 2024 report for the Brazilian presidency of the G20 proposing a coordinated minimum tax on the world's billionaires, extending the book's logic from the national to the international scale.

## Key ideas

- Distributional national accounts: a top-to-bottom income distribution consistent with macro totals
- Offshore wealth and tax havens as a measurable, large share of global wealth
- The progressive wealth tax as a specific, administrable instrument
- Enforcement, valuation, and an exit tax as the mechanics that make a wealth tax collectable
- International coordination (a global minimum tax on billionaires) against capital flight
