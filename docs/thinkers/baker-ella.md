# Ella Baker

American civil-rights organizer (Ella Josephine Baker, 13 December 1903, Norfolk, Virginia – 13 December 1986, New York City). She grew up in rural North Carolina, where her grandmother — born into slavery — told her stories of refusing the master's chosen marriage and being whipped for it. She graduated as valedictorian from Shaw University in 1927, moved to Harlem, and worked through the Depression with cooperative-economics groups, the Workers Education Project of the WPA, and the consumer-cooperative movement before joining the NAACP in 1940. She served as a national field secretary traveling through the Jim Crow South, then as director of branches from 1943 to 1946, building local chapters and developing the organizing methods she would use for the rest of her life.

In 1957 Baker helped Bayard Rustin and Stanley Levison found the Southern Christian Leadership Conference and served as its first executive director — though always uneasily, as the SCLC was structured around the charismatic leadership of [[king-mlk|Martin Luther King Jr.]] and the Black Baptist clergy, an arrangement Baker considered both undemocratic in itself and a poor match for what mass movements actually require. "Strong people don't need strong leaders," she said. After the Greensboro sit-ins of February 1960, Baker convened a meeting of student activists at her alma mater, Shaw University, over Easter weekend 1960. From that meeting emerged the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC), which Baker insisted must remain independent of the SCLC and of the adult civil-rights organizations — student-led, decentralized, group-centered. SNCC, more than any other organization of the period, embodied Baker's distinctive approach to political work.

That approach has come to be called "participatory democracy" or "group-centered leadership" in distinction from the "leader-centered group." Its premises: that ordinary people, especially the most marginalized, are capable of analyzing their own situations and devising their own strategies; that the role of the organizer is to facilitate that process rather than to direct it; that the building of durable democratic institutions matters more than the achievement of particular legislative wins; and that the long, slow work of registering voters, holding meetings, and developing local leadership is the substance of social change, of which mass demonstrations are only the visible surface. The model was carried forward in the Mississippi Freedom Summer of 1964 (which Baker advised), the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party, and the work of Bob Moses, Fannie Lou Hamer, and many in [[forman-james-jr|James Forman]]'s generation of SNCC staff.

Baker continued organizing through her seventies — for the Southern Conference Educational Fund, in support of Angela Davis's defense committee, and for the anti-Apartheid movement. She gave few interviews, wrote no book, and is consequently less remembered than the men whose movements she built; the standard treatment is Barbara Ransby's *Ella Baker and the Black Freedom Movement: A Radical Democratic Vision* (University of North Carolina Press, 2003).

## Key ideas

- Group-centered leadership in contrast to charismatic leader-centered organizations
- Participatory democracy as both a method and an end
- Local organizing and leadership development as the substance of social movements
- "Strong people don't need strong leaders"
- The capacity of ordinary, often poor and rural Black Southerners to analyze and direct their own struggle
