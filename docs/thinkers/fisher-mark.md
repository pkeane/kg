# Mark Fisher

British cultural theorist, music critic, and blogger. Fisher was born in Leicester in 1968, took his undergraduate degree in English and philosophy at the University of Hull, and completed his PhD at the University of Warwick, where he was a member of the Cybernetic Culture Research Unit (CCRU), the group around Sadie Plant and Nick Land in the mid-1990s. From 2003 he wrote the blog *k-punk*, which became one of the central sites of UK left-cultural commentary in the blog era and trained Fisher's distinctive voice — accessible, polemical, equally at home with continental philosophy and with British pop music. He taught at the FE college City Lit and at Goldsmiths, University of London, in the Department of Visual Cultures, from 2011 until his death by suicide in January 2017. He co-founded Zero Books with Tariq Goddard in 2009 and, after a 2014 split, the successor imprint Repeater Books.

Fisher's best-known work is the short book *Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative?* (Zero Books, 2009). The phrase names what Fisher took to be the dominant ideological condition of the post-1989 West — the widely shared, often unconscious sense that capitalism is the only viable economic system and that imagining a coherent alternative is no longer possible. The book builds on Fredric [[jameson-fredric|Jameson]]'s and Slavoj Žižek's frequently-cited line that it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism, and develops a set of related diagnoses: "business ontology" (the assumption that anything legitimate must be organized like a business), "reflexive impotence" (the political passivity of those who know the system is bad and assume nothing can be done), and "depressive hedonia" (a mode of consumer life in which pleasure is constant and satisfaction unreachable). The book draws on Fisher's experience as a further-education teacher to describe the lived texture of these conditions in British schools, hospitals, and offices reshaped by managerial audit culture.

*Ghosts of My Life: Writings on Depression, Hauntology and Lost Futures* (Zero, 2014) collects Fisher's essays on music and film and develops the concept of *hauntology* he adapted from Derrida and applied to British popular music of the 2000s — the dubstep producer Burial, the Ghost Box record label, the BBC Radiophonic Workshop — as the symptom of "the slow cancellation of the future," a culture unable to produce new forms and instead endlessly recombining the past. *The Weird and the Eerie* (Repeater, 2016) is a short study of two aesthetic categories worked through writers including H. P. Lovecraft, M. R. James, Daphne du Maurier, and Margaret Atwood.

Late in his life Fisher began work on a project he called *Acid Communism*, an attempt to recover the consciousness-altering and collective dimensions of 1960s and 1970s counterculture as resources for a future left politics; only the unfinished introduction was completed, published posthumously in *k-punk: The Collected and Unpublished Writings* (Repeater, 2018, edited by Darren Ambrose). His final Goldsmiths lectures appeared as *Postcapitalist Desire: The Final Lectures* (Repeater, 2020, edited by Matt Colquhoun). His 2013 polemic "Exiting the Vampire Castle," published on the *North Star* website, was a sharp critique of what he saw as a moralizing call-out culture on the social-media left; it was widely circulated and remains contested.

## Key themes

- Capitalist realism as the dominant post-1989 structure of feeling
- Hauntology — the cultural inability to produce new futures, audible in 21st-century British popular music
- The reshaping of public-sector work by managerial audit culture
- Mental illness, and specifically depression, as politically structured rather than merely individual
- Acid communism — counterculture as an unrealized resource for the left
