# Ha-Joon Chang

South Korean institutional and developmental economist. Chang took his undergraduate degree at Seoul National University and his PhD at the University of Cambridge under Robert Rowthorn, joining the Cambridge Faculty of Economics in 1990 and teaching there until 2022, when he moved to the School of Oriental and African Studies (SOAS), University of London, as professor of economics. He shared the 2003 Gunnar Myrdal Prize and the 2005 Wassily Leontief Prize, the latter recognizing his work advancing the frontiers of economic thought against the prevailing free-market orthodoxy.

Chang works in a heterodox tradition that draws on Friedrich List, Joseph Schumpeter, [[keynes-jm|Keynes]], and [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]], and that takes the East Asian developmental state — South Korea, Taiwan, Japan — as a central empirical referent. His scholarly book *Kicking Away the Ladder: Development Strategy in Historical Perspective* (Anthem, 2002) argues that today's rich countries did not in fact develop under the free-trade and laissez-faire policies they now press on poorer countries through the WTO, IMF, and World Bank; Britain in the 18th and 19th centuries, the United States from Hamilton through the late 19th century, and Germany and France through their industrializations all used high tariffs, infant-industry protection, state-supported credit, and active industrial policy. The title borrows Friedrich List's 1841 image of the developed nation "kicking away the ladder" by which it climbed, so that others cannot follow. The book has become a standard reference in the heterodox literature on development.

*Bad Samaritans: The Myth of Free Trade and the Secret History of Capitalism* (2007) restates the *Kicking Away the Ladder* argument for a general audience and extends it to contemporary trade and intellectual-property policy. *23 Things They Don't Tell You About Capitalism* (2010) is a popular work that takes apart a set of propositions Chang treats as ideological commonplaces of post-1980 economics (among them: that the free market exists, that companies should be run in the interest of their owners, that we live in a post-industrial age, and that making rich people richer makes the rest of us richer). *Economics: The User's Guide* (Penguin, 2014) is an introductory text built around the deliberate pluralism Chang advocates within the discipline — neoclassical, Keynesian, Marxist, Schumpeterian, institutionalist, behavioural, and other schools presented side by side rather than as a single mainstream with deviations. *Edible Economics: A Hungry Economist Explains the World* (Allen Lane, 2022) uses individual foods as devices to introduce economic ideas to non-specialist readers.

Chang has been a long-standing critic of the Washington Consensus and of what he characterizes as the discipline's collapse into a single neoclassical framework since the 1980s. His work overlaps in policy conclusions, though not in method, with [[stiglitz-joseph|Stiglitz]]'s critique of IMF and World Bank conditionality.

## Key themes

- Historical record of state intervention, protection, and industrial policy in the development of today's rich economies
- Critique of the Washington Consensus and of free-trade conditionality imposed on developing countries
- The East Asian developmental state as a model of state–market coordination
- Pluralism within economics — multiple legitimate theoretical schools rather than a single mainstream
- The institutional and political construction of markets, against the picture of markets as natural
