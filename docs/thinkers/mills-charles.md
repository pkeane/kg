# Charles W. Mills

Anglophone Caribbean philosopher (Charles Wade Mills, 3 January 1951, London – 20 September 2021, Evanston, Illinois). Born in London to Jamaican parents and raised in Jamaica, he took his B.Sc. at the University of the West Indies and his doctorate in philosophy at the University of Toronto in 1985. He taught at the University of Oklahoma, the University of Illinois at Chicago, and Northwestern University before moving in 2016 to the CUNY Graduate Center, where he held a Distinguished Professorship until his death from cancer in 2021.

Mills' central project was the development of a critical philosophy of race within and against the analytic political-philosophy tradition. *The Racial Contract* (Cornell, 1997), his best-known work, reads the canonical social-contract tradition — Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant — as having concealed a second, racial contract by which "whites" constituted themselves as the full political subjects and non-whites as a population external to the moral and political community. The familiar abstractions of liberal contract theory — the social contract, the state of nature, the original position — operate, on Mills' reading, as if race were not constitutive of the modern political order it claims to describe. The book has been widely used in graduate political-philosophy syllabi as the standard internal critique of liberalism's racial blindness.

In subsequent work Mills argued increasingly that the right response to liberal contractarianism's racial occlusions is not to abandon it but to repair it. *Black Rights/White Wrongs: The Critique of Racial Liberalism* (Oxford, 2017) made the case for what he called "black radical liberalism" — a liberalism that takes its own normative commitments seriously enough to undo the racial exclusions on which it was actually built. His ongoing argument with [[rawls-john|John Rawls]] was the central thread: Mills proposed that Rawls's apparatus, especially the original position, could be retooled to address racial injustice as a "non-ideal" matter, against Rawls's own decision to bracket race and other historical injustices in setting out his ideal theory. The disagreement was over whether ideal theory in Rawls's sense, by abstracting from race, makes the resulting principles unable to do the work they are supposed to do.

Mills' other books include *Blackness Visible: Essays on Philosophy and Race* (1998), *From Class to Race* (2003), and *Contract and Domination* (2007, with Carole Pateman, joining his analysis of the racial contract to Pateman's of the sexual contract). At the time of his death he was president-elect of the American Philosophical Association.

## Key ideas

- The racial contract as the suppressed second contract on which the social contract tradition was built
- The "epistemology of ignorance" — structured, motivated white misunderstanding of the racial order
- Black radical liberalism — Rawls retooled for non-ideal racial injustice
- The critique of "ideal theory" as a method that occludes its own racial premises
