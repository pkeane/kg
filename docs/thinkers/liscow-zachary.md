# Zachary Liscow

American legal academic and economist, Professor of Law at Yale Law School. He holds a J.D. from Yale and a Ph.D. in economics from the University of California, Berkeley, with an A.B. from Harvard in economics and environmental science and public policy. From 2022 to 2023 he served as Chief Economist at the White House Office of Management and Budget under the Biden administration, heading OMB's Office of Economic Policy.

His academic work sits at the intersection of tax law, public economics, and the contemporary "state capacity" research program. "Redistribution for Realists" (*Iowa Law Review* 107, 2022) argues against the standard economic prescription that redistribution should be carried out through the tax-and-transfer system while other policy is set on efficiency grounds alone. Liscow contends that ordinary citizens evaluate policy by notions of desert, reciprocity, and procedural fairness rather than by the size of an after-tax transfer, so a politics built only on optimal taxation is politically inert; the alternative he develops is to embed distributional considerations directly in spending and regulatory choices. The argument bears on the literature in [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]] by giving a partial account of *why* the technically workable tax instruments documented there have so seldom been enacted.

A second strand of his work concerns the cost of building physical infrastructure in the United States. With Leah Brooks he documented that interstate highway costs roughly tripled in real terms from the 1960s to the 1980s ("Infrastructure Costs," *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics*, 2023). With Cailin Slattery and William Nober ("State Capacity and Infrastructure Costs," 2023) he argues that thin in-house engineering and contracting capacity at state departments of transportation, rather than wages or materials, accounts for much of the U.S. excess. "Getting Infrastructure Built: The Law and Economics of Permitting" (*Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 2025) is the synthesis statement. The line is widely cited in the "abundance" / supply-side progressive policy conversation alongside [[pahlka-jennifer|Jennifer Pahlka]]'s work on administrative implementation.

While at OMB Liscow led the 2023 revision of Circular A-4, the federal standard for regulatory benefit-cost analysis — its first substantive rewrite since 2003 — introducing explicit distributional weighting and lowering the social discount rate from 7 percent to 2 percent on a real-rate basis. He returned to Yale in 2024.

## Key ideas

- "Redistribution for realists": citizens' fairness intuitions, not optimal-tax theory, set the political constraint on redistribution
- Distributional weighting inside spending and regulatory decisions, not only the tax code
- U.S. infrastructure costs as a state-capacity problem
- Permitting and procurement as binding constraints on public investment
