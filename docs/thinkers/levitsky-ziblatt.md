# Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt

Two political scientists at Harvard whose joint work has become the standard reference for the contemporary literature on democratic erosion. **Steven R. Levitsky** (b. 17 January 1968) holds the David Rockefeller Professorship of Latin American Studies at Harvard and directs the David Rockefeller Center for Latin American Studies. His earlier work, including *Competitive Authoritarianism: Hybrid Regimes after the Cold War* (Cambridge, 2010, with Lucan Way), built the analytic category of regimes that retain formal democratic institutions while systematically violating their norms — a category that, when *How Democracies Die* appeared, was made available for application to the United States. **Daniel Ziblatt** (b. 1972) is Eaton Professor of the Science of Government at Harvard and also leads a research group at the Berlin Social Science Center. His *Conservative Parties and the Birth of Democracy* (Cambridge, 2017) won the American Political Science Association's Woodrow Wilson Prize; its central argument is that the durability of democracy in Europe in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries depended on the willingness of established conservative parties to accept democratic competition rather than abandon the system when they began to lose.

Their joint book *How Democracies Die* (Crown, 2018) draws on the comparative literature on democratic breakdown — interwar Europe, postwar Latin America, Erdoğan's Turkey, Orbán's Hungary, Chávez's Venezuela — to argue that contemporary democracies seldom collapse through coups; they erode under elected leaders who weaponize the rules against the system. The authors identify two democratic norms whose breakdown is the leading indicator of erosion: *mutual toleration* (treating one's political opponents as legitimate competitors rather than as enemies) and *institutional forbearance* (refraining from using available legal powers in ways that, although technically permitted, would damage the system). They argue that in the United States these norms held — for white Americans — through most of the twentieth century but began breaking down in the 1990s and accelerated after 2010, with the Republican Party as the principal driver.

*Tyranny of the Minority: Why American Democracy Reached the Breaking Point* (Crown, 2023) extends the analysis to U.S. counter-majoritarian institutions — the Senate's malapportionment, the Electoral College, gerrymandering, the filibuster, lifetime judicial tenure — and argues that the combination of these institutions with a radicalized minority party allows a shrinking electoral base to retain national power, removing the ordinary democratic incentive to broaden appeal and intensifying the incentive to entrench. The book is the most explicit treatment in the joint authorship of how American constitutional design itself contributes to the erosion the earlier book described.

## Key ideas

- Democracies die under elected leaders, not through coups
- Mutual toleration and institutional forbearance as the two soft norms whose breakdown precedes erosion
- Competitive authoritarianism as a regime category distinct from democracy and from full authoritarianism
- Counter-majoritarian institutions as accelerants of erosion when paired with a minoritarian party
- Conservative parties' willingness to accept electoral defeat as the historical foundation of stable democracy
