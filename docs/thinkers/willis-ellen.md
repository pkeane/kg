# Ellen Willis

American essayist, pop-music critic, and radical feminist. She was the first pop-music critic at *The New Yorker*, writing the magazine's "Rock, Etc." column from 1968 to 1975, and went on to write for the *Village Voice*, *Rolling Stone*, *The Nation*, *Salon*, *Slate*, and other publications. Her work joined two subjects that were usually kept apart: the close criticism of popular music, and the political and psychological stakes of sexual freedom. In both she defended pleasure — and the cultural forms that delivered it — against what she saw as the puritanism of the right and the left alike.

Willis treated rock and roll as a serious cultural and political phenomenon rather than a diversion. Her 1967 essay on Bob Dylan was one of the first extended pieces of American rock criticism; she later wrote on Janis Joplin, the Velvet Underground, the Rolling Stones, and the relation between popular music and the 1960s counterculture, arguing that the music carried real liberatory content even as the industry around it commodified that content. Her criticism drew on the Frankfurt School and on Wilhelm Reich's linkage of sexual repression and political authority: she held that mass culture was neither simply false consciousness nor simply free, but a contested field where genuine desires and their commercial capture were entangled.

In 1969 she co-founded the radical-feminist group Redstockings with Shulamith Firestone. Within the feminist movement she became a central figure on the "pro-sex" side of the 1980s "sex wars," opposing the anti-pornography feminism of Andrea Dworkin and Catharine MacKinnon; her essay "Lust Horizons: Is the Women's Movement Pro-Sex?" (*Village Voice*, 1981) is often credited with introducing the term "pro-sex feminism." She argued that a feminism organized around the suppression of male desire and the policing of sexual expression would reproduce the very repression it claimed to oppose, and that sexual freedom was integral, not incidental, to women's liberation.

She was a democratic socialist and a consistent critic of both consumer capitalism and the therapeutic, conformist culture she saw it producing — the subject of *Don't Think, Smile!* (1999). In 1995 she founded the cultural reporting and criticism program in the journalism department at New York University, where she taught until her death from lung cancer in 2006. Her writing on the cheapness of urban life as a precondition for the 1960s cultural ferment is one strand of her cultural criticism; see [[affordable-housing-and-the-arts]] and [[counterculture-origins]].

## Key themes

- Pop music as serious cultural and political criticism, not mere entertainment
- Pleasure and desire as political categories; the entanglement of authentic desire and commercial capture
- Sexual freedom as integral to feminism; the "pro-sex" position in the feminist sex wars
- The puritanism of both the right and the left as a target of critique
- Mass culture as a contested field rather than simple false consciousness

## Key works

- *Beginning to See the Light: Pieces of a Decade* (1981)
- *No More Nice Girls: Countercultural Essays* (1992)
- *Don't Think, Smile! Notes on a Decade of Denial* (1999)
- *Out of the Vinyl Deeps: Ellen Willis on Rock Music* (2011, ed. Nona Willis Aronowitz, posthumous)
- *The Essential Ellen Willis* (2014, ed. Nona Willis Aronowitz, posthumous; winner of the National Book Critics Circle Award for criticism)
