# Eric Foner

American historian (b. 7 February 1943, New York City). The leading living historian of nineteenth-century America, especially of slavery, the Civil War, Reconstruction, and the long history of American freedom. He took his B.A. at Columbia in 1963, a second B.A. at Oriel College, Oxford (where he was a Kellett Fellow), and his Ph.D. at Columbia in 1969 under Richard Hofstadter, whose last doctoral student he was. He has spent his entire teaching career at Columbia, where he is DeWitt Clinton Professor Emeritus of History. He has served as president of the American Historical Association, the Organization of American Historians, and the Society of American Historians.

Foner's first book, *Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men: The Ideology of the Republican Party before the Civil War* (Oxford, 1970), drawn from the dissertation, recovered the moral and economic vision of the antebellum Republican Party — that free labor was both more productive and more dignified than slave labor — as the ideology around which the Northern coalition that fought the Civil War was built. *Tom Paine and Revolutionary America* (1976) extended Foner's interest in radical-democratic American thought back into the Revolutionary period.

His central work is *Reconstruction: America's Unfinished Revolution, 1863–1877* (Harper & Row, 1988), the synthesis that, with [[du-bois-web|W. E. B. Du Bois's]] *Black Reconstruction in America* (1935), established what is now the standard scholarly account: that Reconstruction was a genuine if brief experiment in interracial democracy, that the federal government's withdrawal in 1877 was a political choice rather than a historical inevitability, and that the violent overthrow of that experiment by white Southern Democrats produced the long Jim Crow regime. The book displaced the earlier Dunning-school treatment that had cast Reconstruction as a corrupt and vindictive Northern imposition.

*The Story of American Freedom* (1998) traced the changing meanings of "freedom" across American history, from the Revolution through the Cold War. *The Fiery Trial: Abraham Lincoln and American Slavery* (2010) won the Pulitzer Prize, Bancroft Prize, and Lincoln Prize for its account of Lincoln's slow, conditional, and finally radical movement on slavery. *Gateway to Freedom* (2015) covered the New York-area Underground Railroad. *The Second Founding: How the Civil War and Reconstruction Remade the Constitution* (2019) read the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments as a constitutional refounding whose intended reach was constrained almost immediately by the Supreme Court and only partially recovered in the twentieth century.

His widely used textbook *Give Me Liberty! An American History* (first published 2004, in continuous revision since) has shaped two decades of college U.S. history teaching.

## Key ideas

- Reconstruction as an unfinished revolution and a genuine experiment in interracial democracy
- The Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments as a "second founding" of the United States
- Free-labor ideology as the moral and economic basis of the Northern war effort
- "Freedom" as a contested American keyword with shifting content across regimes
