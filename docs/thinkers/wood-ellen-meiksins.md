# Ellen Meiksins Wood

Political theorist and historian, born in New York to Latvian Jewish parents who had escaped the Nazi occupation, and based for most of her career at York University in Toronto. With Robert Brenner and George Comninel she is one of the founders of what is now called **Political Marxism** — a current within Marxist historiography that treats capitalism not as the natural unfolding of commerce or human acquisitiveness but as a specific, late, and originally English transformation of the relations between landlords, tenants, and the state. For a decade she co-edited *Monthly Review* with Harry Magdoff and Paul Sweezy, and her later books were published by Verso.

Her central historical argument, worked out across *The Pristine Culture of Capitalism* (1991) and *The Origin of Capitalism* (1999; revised as *The Origin of Capitalism: A Longer View*, 2002), is that capitalism did not arise from the gradual quantitative expansion of merchant activity in towns — the conventional "commercialization model" shared, she argued, by liberal and much Marxist historiography alike — but from a qualitative change in the English countryside between roughly the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. English landlords, unlike their continental counterparts, had lost the direct extra-economic means of extracting surplus from peasants and increasingly leased land competitively to tenant farmers, who in turn had to produce for the market in order to retain their leases. The result was a society in which producers were for the first time *market-dependent* rather than merely market-engaged — compelled by purely economic imperatives of competition and productivity, with no household subsistence to fall back on. From that agrarian base, she argued, capitalism's distinctive logic of accumulation, productivity-driven competition, and dispossession spread outward and eventually globally. The argument builds on Brenner's 1976 "Agrarian Class Structure and Economic Development in Pre-Industrial Europe" and the ensuing **Brenner Debate**, and stands against [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]-influenced and world-systems accounts in which capitalism is older, more diffuse, and primarily commercial.

*Democracy Against Capitalism: Renewing Historical Materialism* (1995) generalizes the historical claim into a methodological one: the separation of "the economic" from "the political" is itself a product of capitalist social relations, not a neutral analytic distinction, and recovering historical materialism means reconstructing how that separation came about rather than presupposing it. *The Retreat from Class: A New "True" Socialism* (1986), which won the Deutscher Memorial Prize, is a sharp polemic against the post-Marxism of Ernesto Laclau and Chantal Mouffe and the strand of British Eurocommunism associated with *Marxism Today*; Wood read these as abandoning class for a politics of discursive coalition. *Empire of Capital* (2003) extends the framework to international relations, arguing that capitalist empire works through the imposition of market dependence on formally sovereign states rather than through territorial conquest. Her last major project was a two-volume social history of Western political thought — *Citizens to Lords* (2008) and *Liberty and Property* (2012) — read against the property regimes and class relations of the societies that produced it.

Wood's prose is unornamented, polemical without being baroque, and consistently lucid. She insisted that **capitalism has a beginning** — and that its origin in a specific English agrarian conjuncture means it could have been otherwise, and could still be otherwise. The political stake of her historical work is that argument.

## Key themes

- Agrarian origins of capitalism in early modern England
- Market *dependence* versus market *engagement* — capitalism as compulsion, not opportunity
- Political Marxism and the Brenner Debate
- The capitalist separation of "the economic" from "the political"
- Critique of post-Marxism and the "retreat from class"
- Capitalist empire as imposed market dependence rather than territorial rule
- Western political thought read against property and class
