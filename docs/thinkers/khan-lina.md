# Lina Khan

American legal scholar and antitrust regulator (Lina M. Khan, b. 3 March 1989, London). Born to Pakistani parents in the United Kingdom, she moved with her family to the United States at the age of eleven. She took her B.A. in political theory at Williams College (2010), worked as a researcher at the New America Foundation under Barry Lynn at what became the Open Markets Institute, and entered Yale Law School in 2014, taking the J.D. in 2017. From June 2021 to January 2025 she served as chair of the Federal Trade Commission, nominated by President Joseph R. Biden and confirmed at the age of thirty-two — the youngest chair in the agency's history. She is now associate professor of law at Columbia Law School.

Khan's defining academic work, written as a student note at Yale, is "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox," *Yale Law Journal* 126 (2017): 710–805. The article argued that the dominant "consumer welfare standard" in American antitrust — the doctrine established by Robert Bork's *The Antitrust Paradox* (1978) and the Chicago School, under which anticompetitive conduct is identified by short-run price effects to consumers — was structurally incapable of recognizing the harms posed by Amazon's documented strategy of sustained below-cost pricing, vertical integration, and platform self-preferencing. The harms, Khan argued, were precisely the ones the older multidimensional antitrust tradition associated with [[brandeis-louis|Louis Brandeis]] had been designed to police: dependence of suppliers and competitors on a dominant intermediary, the structural displacement of independent firms, and the political and economic consequences of concentrated market power. The piece is widely credited with catalyzing the contemporary "neo-Brandeisian" revival in American antitrust thought.

"The Separation of Platforms and Commerce," *Columbia Law Review* 119 (2019): 973–1098, developed the structural argument: where a single firm operates both a marketplace and competes on that marketplace, the conflict of interest is irreducible and the appropriate remedy is structural separation, on the model of the historical separation of banking from commerce.

At the FTC Khan put the framework into administrative practice. The commission, jointly with the Department of Justice Antitrust Division, issued revised Merger Guidelines in December 2023 that for the first time in decades formally restored consideration of structural and labor-market effects alongside price. The FTC under Khan moved to ban non-compete clauses in employment contracts (April 2024, subsequently challenged in court); finalized the "click-to-cancel" rule simplifying subscription cancellation (2024); brought Section 5 cases against Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft; and challenged the Kroger–Albertsons supermarket merger and the Microsoft acquisition of Activision Blizzard. The tenure was contested by the regulated industries and by Democratic-aligned figures who held that her enforcement program was too ambitious; it was concluded by the change of administration in January 2025. The institutional record will be litigated for years.

## Key ideas

- The consumer welfare standard is structurally inadequate to recognize the harms of platform monopolies
- Vertical integration combined with dominant platform position is the central contemporary antitrust problem
- Structural separation of platform and commerce as the appropriate remedy
- Antitrust as a multidimensional doctrine concerned with concentration as such, not only with consumer prices
