# John Searle

American philosopher of mind and language, professor at UC Berkeley from 1959 to 2019, who made significant contributions to speech act theory and the philosophy of mind, and was for several decades a prominent public participant in debates over the prospects of artificial intelligence. (Berkeley revoked his emeritus status in June 2019 following a finding that he had violated the university's sexual harassment policies.)

His early work, *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language* (Cambridge, 1969), developed J. L. Austin's Oxford lectures into a systematic taxonomy of illocutionary acts — what speakers *do* in saying things (asserting, promising, questioning, ordering) — and the conditions under which those acts succeed or fail.

In philosophy of mind he is known for the Chinese Room argument, first presented in "Minds, Brains, and Programs" (*Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 3:3, 1980). Imagine a person who knows no Chinese sitting in a room with a rulebook and slots for input and output. Symbols come in; the person looks up each one in the rulebook and produces the output the rule specifies; from outside, the room appears to converse fluently in Chinese. The person inside understands nothing. Since the person instantiates exactly the same program a computer would, and understands nothing, Searle argues that symbol manipulation by rule is not by itself sufficient for understanding — syntax does not give you semantics. The argument is directed against what Searle calls "strong AI": the claim that a suitably programmed computer thereby *has* a mind, rather than merely modeling one. The Chinese Room generated a large secondary literature; standard objections (the Systems Reply, the Robot Reply, the Brain Simulator Reply) and Searle's responses have been revisited across several decades.

His later work on intentionality (*Intentionality*, 1983), consciousness (*The Rediscovery of the Mind*, 1992), and social ontology (*The Construction of Social Reality*, 1995; *Making the Social World*, 2010) extends the same broadly biological-naturalist position: minds are real, irreducibly subjective, produced by brains, not substrate-independent computations. He coined the term "Background" for the non-representational capacities and assumptions that make intentional states possible — a notion related in spirit to what [[dreyfus-hubert|Dreyfus]] (drawing on Heidegger) had called the fore-structures of understanding, though the philosophical lineages are distinct.
