# Ferdinand Lundberg

American financial journalist and author (1902–1995). He worked through the late 1920s and 1930s as a financial reporter, including for the *New York Herald Tribune*, where he covered the 1929 stock-market crash. He left daily journalism to write books documenting the concentration of wealth and corporate power in the United States, working in the muckraking tradition of Gustavus Myers and drawing on the economic interpretation of American history associated with Charles Beard.

His first major book, *Imperial Hearst* (1936), was a critical biography of the newspaper magnate William Randolph Hearst. *America's 60 Families* (1937) argued that a small number of intermarried wealthy families functioned as a de facto American plutocracy, exercising power through corporate ownership, philanthropy, and control of the press; it became a widely read New Deal-era document. Three decades later he returned to the subject in *The Rich and the Super-Rich: A Study in the Power of Money Today* (1968), an 800-page survey of American wealth holdings, the trust-and-foundation machinery that preserves them, the tax provisions that shelter them, and the influence of money over politics and the press. The book sold widely and is the work most often cited under his name; no single later book has been generally acknowledged as its direct successor, a point reviewers of its reissues have repeated.

Lundberg also co-wrote, with the psychoanalyst Marynia F. Farnham, *Modern Woman: The Lost Sex* (1947), which argued that feminism was a neurotic reaction and that women's fulfillment lay in domestic and maternal roles. The book was later treated as a representative target of second-wave feminist criticism, notably in Betty Friedan's *The Feminine Mystique* (1963).

## Key themes

- Concentration of wealth in a small number of families and its persistence across generations through inheritance, trusts, and foundations
- The conversion of economic wealth into political influence and control of the press
- Tax law as a mechanism of wealth preservation rather than redistribution
- A documentary, list-and-ledger method: cataloguing holdings, family connections, and legal structures at length
