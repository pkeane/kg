# George M. Prince

American author and creativity consultant (George Mather Prince, 5 April 1918, New York – 9 June 2009). He grew up in Rochester, New York, and was educated at Phillips Exeter Academy and Williams College. He worked in advertising before joining Arthur D. Little, where, with William J. J. [[gordon-william-jj|Gordon]], he developed Synectics in the firm's Invention Design Group during the 1950s. The two co-founded Synectics, Inc. in Cambridge, Massachusetts, around 1960.

Prince's contribution centered on the *group process* of creative problem-solving rather than on the analogy mechanisms that Gordon emphasized. His book *The Practice of Creativity* (1970) is a manual for running creative meetings: it sets out roles (notably a "leader" who manages process rather than content, and a "client" who owns the problem), rules for deferring judgment, and techniques for keeping a speculative idea alive long enough to be developed. He argued that ordinary group behavior — premature criticism, competition for status, the reflexive killing of half-formed ideas — destroys creativity, and that productive sessions require explicitly protecting tentative contributions. He continued to lead Synectics work for decades through the successor firm Synecticsworld.

## Key ideas

- The **psychology of the creative meeting**: how groups suppress ideas through premature judgment and status competition
- **Deferred judgment** and the protection of speculative, half-formed ideas
- Process roles — a leader who manages method, a client who owns the problem — distinct from the content of the work
- Creative problem-solving as a learnable group discipline rather than individual inspiration

## Key works

- *The Practice of Creativity: A Manual for Dynamic Group Problem-Solving* (1970)
