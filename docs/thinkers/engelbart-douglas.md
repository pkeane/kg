# Douglas Engelbart

American engineer and inventor (30 January 1925, Portland, Oregon – 2 July 2013, Atherton, California). He served as a radar technician in the Second World War, where he read Vannevar Bush's 1945 essay "As We May Think," and took a doctorate in electrical engineering at the University of California, Berkeley, in 1955. At the Stanford Research Institute (SRI) he founded the Augmentation Research Center.

Engelbart's organizing aim, set out in the 1962 report "Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework," was to raise the "collective IQ" of organizations facing problems of growing complexity by pairing people with interactive computers. He treated tools, language, methods, and training as a single co-evolving system and argued for "bootstrapping" — using the tools one builds to improve the building of the next ones. His laboratory developed the oN-Line System (NLS), which implemented many of these ideas in working software.

On 9 December 1968, in San Francisco, Engelbart gave a 90-minute live demonstration of NLS later known as "The Mother of All Demos." It introduced to a general audience the computer mouse (which he had invented and patented), on-screen windows, hypertext links, version control, real-time collaborative document editing, and video conferencing. Several of his researchers later moved to Xerox PARC, carrying the work toward the graphical user interface that Apple and others subsequently commercialized. Engelbart received the Turing Award in 1997 and the National Medal of Technology in 2000. His program is a central reference point for the [[tools-for-thought]] tradition, alongside Bush, J. C. R. Licklider, and Ted [[nelson-ted|Nelson]].

## Key ideas

- **Augmenting human intellect**: computers as instruments for raising individual and collective problem-solving capacity, not merely for calculation
- **Collective IQ** and the co-evolution of tools, methods, and skills as one system
- **Bootstrapping**: improving the tools by using them to build better tools
- Working demonstrations of the **mouse, hypertext, windows, and real-time collaboration** (NLS, 1968)

## Key works

- "Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework" (SRI report, 1962)
- The oN-Line System (NLS) and the 1968 demonstration ("The Mother of All Demos")
