# Hanif Abdurraqib

American poet, essayist, and cultural critic from Columbus, Ohio. Abdurraqib writes primarily about music, performance, basketball, grief, and Black American life, moving between formal poetry, long-form essay, and reported criticism. He came up through the Columbus poetry-slam and small-press scene in the early 2010s and built his audience first through music writing — at MTV News, Pitchfork, *The New Yorker*, and elsewhere — before the books found a wider readership.

*The Crown Ain't Worth Much* (Button Poetry, 2016) was his debut collection. *They Can't Kill Us Until They Kill Us* (Two Dollar Radio, 2017), a book of essays on contemporary music and the conditions under which Black Americans listen to and make it, established the voice: first-person, associative, willing to braid a Carly Rae Jepsen show with a friend's funeral or a Future album with the aftermath of a police killing. *Go Ahead in the Rain: Notes to A Tribe Called Quest* (University of Texas Press, 2019), part of the American Music Series, is a book-length essay on the group and on hip-hop's debts to the Black music that preceded it, structured in part as letters to Phife Dawg after his death. It was longlisted for the National Book Award.

*A Little Devil in America: Notes in Praise of Black Performance* (Random House, 2021) took up minstrelsy, the cakewalk, Soul Train, Whitney Houston, magicians, and dance marathons to think about the long history of Black performance under the conditions of an American audience; it received the Andrew Carnegie Medal for Excellence in Nonfiction and the National Book Critics Circle Award for Criticism. *There's Always This Year: On Basketball and Ascension* (Random House, 2024) uses LeBron James, the Cleveland Cavaliers, and the playgrounds of Columbus to write about home, mortality, and what it costs to leave and return. He received a MacArthur Fellowship in 2021. He runs the small press 68to05 in Columbus.
