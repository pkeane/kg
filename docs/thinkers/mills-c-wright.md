# C. Wright Mills

American sociologist (Charles Wright Mills, 28 August 1916, Waco, Texas – 20 March 1962, West Nyack, New York). He took his undergraduate and master's degrees at the University of Texas at Austin and his doctorate at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, where his dissertation treated pragmatism and the sociology of knowledge. From 1946 until his death he taught at Columbia University. With Hans Gerth he translated and edited *From Max Weber: Essays in Sociology* (1946), and Weber's account of stratification by class, status, and party — together with Veblen and Marx — shaped his analysis of American power. He died of a heart attack at 45.

Mills produced a trilogy on the structure of mid-century American society: *The New Men of Power* (1948), on labor leaders; *White Collar: The American Middle Classes* (1951), on the salaried employee and the decline of the independent middle class; and *The Power Elite* (1956), his best-known work. *The Power Elite* argues that national power had concentrated in an interlocking directorate drawn from three institutional hierarchies — the large corporation, the executive branch and its agencies, and the senior military — whose members shared social origins, schooling, and clubs and circulated among the three domains. The book challenged the pluralist account of power as dispersed among competing interest groups and supplied the phrase "power elite" to general usage.

*The Sociological Imagination* (1959) sets out his methodological program: the discipline's task is to connect "personal troubles" to "public issues" by locating individual biography within history and social structure, a formulation directed polemically against both the grand abstraction of Talcott Parsons's theory and the narrowness of survey research. In his last years Mills turned to political writing — *The Causes of World War Three* (1958) and *Listen, Yankee: The Revolution in Cuba* (1960) — and his "Letter to the New Left" (1960) helped popularize the term "New Left" in the United States.

## Key ideas

- The **power elite**: an interlocking corporate, political, and military leadership, distinguished from both ruling-class theory and pluralism
- The **sociological imagination**: the linkage of personal troubles to public issues through history and structure
- The displacement of the old independent middle class by the dependent, status-anxious **white-collar** employee
- A critique of "grand theory" and "abstracted empiricism" as the twin evasions of postwar sociology
