# Emma Goldman

Anarchist, agitator, and one of the most prominent radicals in American public life between the 1890s and the First World War. Born in Kovno in the Russian Empire (now Kaunas, Lithuania), Goldman emigrated to the United States in 1885 and worked in garment factories in Rochester, New York. She was radicalized by the execution of four [[anarchism|anarchists]] after the Haymarket affair of 1886, and on moving to New York City became a lecturer and organizer, with Alexander Berkman as her lifelong comrade. In 1892 Berkman attempted to assassinate the steel executive Henry Clay Frick during the Homestead strike — an act of "propaganda of the deed" that Goldman helped plan. Berkman served fourteen years; Goldman defended the attempt for the rest of her life while moving away from individual violence as a tactic.

From 1906 to 1917 she edited the monthly *Mother Earth*. Her lecturing ranged across anarchism, free speech, atheism, the modern drama — Ibsen, Strindberg, Shaw, collected in *The Social Significance of the Modern Drama* (1914) — the critique of marriage as an economic institution, and birth control, for the distribution of contraceptive information about which she was jailed in 1916. She was skeptical that suffrage alone would emancipate women, arguing that economic dependence and what she called women's "internal tyrants" mattered more than the ballot. Her synthesis drew the anarchist communism of [[kropotkin-peter|Kropotkin]] together with the individualism she took from Nietzsche and Ibsen.

In 1917 Goldman and Berkman were convicted of conspiracy to obstruct the wartime draft and imprisoned for two years; in 1919, during the Palmer raids, they were stripped of citizenship and deported to Soviet Russia. Goldman arrived sympathetic and left within two years appalled by the Bolshevik suppression of dissent, the Cheka, and the crushing of the Kronstadt rising — an experience recorded in *My Disillusionment in Russia* (1923), one of the earliest critiques of Bolshevik authoritarianism from the left. She spent the rest of her life stateless in Europe and Canada, published the autobiography *Living My Life* (1931), and traveled to Spain in 1936 to support the anarcho-syndicalist CNT-FAI in the civil war. She died in Toronto in 1940 and was buried in Chicago, near the Haymarket martyrs.

## Key ideas

- Anarchism as the liberation of the individual from the state, capital, and the church
- Free speech as the precondition of all other freedoms
- Critique of marriage and the economic dependence of women
- Birth control and women's control of their own bodies
- Skepticism that suffrage alone would emancipate women
- Anti-militarism and opposition to conscription
- Early first-hand critique of Bolshevik authoritarianism from the left

## Key works

- *Anarchism and Other Essays* (1910)
- *The Social Significance of the Modern Drama* (1914)
- *My Disillusionment in Russia* (1923)
- *Living My Life* (1931)
