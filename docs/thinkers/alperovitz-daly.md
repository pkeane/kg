# Gar Alperovitz and Lew Daly

Gar Alperovitz (b. 1936) is an American historian and political economist, the Lionel R. Bauman Professor of Political Economy at the University of Maryland from 1999 to 2015 and co-founder of the Democracy Collaborative, known both for his early work on the decision to use the atomic bomb (*Atomic Diplomacy*, 1965) and for a later body of writing on community ownership and "the pluralist commonwealth." Lew Daly is a writer and policy analyst who has written on the intersection of religion, economics, and the welfare state.

*Unjust Deserts: How the Rich Are Taking Our Common Inheritance and Why We Should Take It Back* (The New Press, 2008) argues that the greater part of any individual's income and wealth is not earned by individual effort, talent, or risk but is a return on an inherited stock of knowledge, technology, and social organization built up over generations and held in common. Drawing on the Nobel economist [[simon-herbert|Herbert Simon]]'s estimate that the bulk of income in a wealthy society is attributable to this "social patrimony," the authors put the inherited share at up to 90 percent. The normative conclusion is that high taxation of large fortunes reclaims a common inheritance that present holders have appropriated rather than confiscating earned reward. The argument places them in a line running from [[paine-thomas|Thomas Paine]]'s *Agrarian Justice* and [[george-henry|Henry George]]'s unearned increment through Simon. See [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]].

## Key ideas

- Most income is a return on inherited social knowledge, not individual contribution
- The "common inheritance" or social patrimony as the true source of wealth
- Taxation of great wealth as reclamation rather than confiscation
