# Sandra Harding

American philosopher of science who systematized feminist standpoint theory and reframed scientific objectivity around what she called "strong objectivity." Distinguished Professor of Education and Gender Studies emerita at UCLA, she previously taught at the University of Delaware and edited *Signs* from 2000 to 2005.

*The Science Question in Feminism* (Cornell, 1986) surveys three positions feminism had taken toward science by the mid-1980s — feminist empiricism (the demand for more rigorous application of existing scientific norms), feminist standpoint theory (knowledge produced from women's social position has distinctive epistemic access), and feminist postmodernism (suspicion of all unified epistemic claims) — and assesses their strengths and limits. The book made standpoint theory available as a coherent philosophical position rather than a scattered set of arguments.

*Whose Science? Whose Knowledge? Thinking from Women's Lives* (Cornell, 1991) develops her positive contribution. The standard ideal of scientific objectivity, she argues, is in fact weak: it fails to examine the social location, interests, and assumptions of the knowing subject, treating those as either irrelevant or as sources of bias to be eliminated. A stronger objectivity would treat the social position of the knower as itself an object of systematic investigation, on the grounds that knowledge produced from marginalized standpoints is more rigorous because it is more accountable to its own conditions of production. The "view from nowhere" cannot examine itself; a self-consciously situated knowledge can. Standpoint, in Harding's account, is achieved through political and theoretical work, not given by biological or sociological location — the formulation that answers the essentialism charge most directly.

Later work extended the framework to postcolonial science studies (*Is Science Multicultural?*, 1998; *Sciences from Below*, 2008), examining how the conventional history and philosophy of science elide non-Western traditions and the role of European science in colonial projects.

Harding is one of the principal figures in the [[situated-knowledge]] conversation; her version of standpoint theory is the systematic philosophical statement, while [[haraway-donna|Haraway]]'s "Situated Knowledges" essay is closer to a manifesto.

## Key ideas

- Strong objectivity
- Standpoint as achieved through political-epistemic work, not given by social location
- The weakness of the conventional "view from nowhere"
- Three trajectories of feminist science studies (empiricism, standpoint, postmodernism)
- Postcolonial science studies
