# Maurice Merleau-Ponty

French phenomenologist whose work centered on perception, embodiment, and the body's primary role in knowing. Trained at the École Normale Supérieure alongside [[sartre-jean-paul|Sartre]] and [[de-beauvoir-simone|Beauvoir]], he taught at Lyon and the Sorbonne before being appointed to the chair of philosophy at the Collège de France in 1952, where he served until his death from a stroke at age 53.

His major work, *Phénoménologie de la perception* (1945), develops a phenomenology of the lived body (*corps propre*) as the primary site of knowing. Against empiricism — which reduces perception to the registration of sense-data by a passive mind — and against intellectualism — which treats perception as judgment performed on raw sensory input — Merleau-Ponty argues that perception is the body's skilled, pre-reflective engagement with a field of significance. The body is not an object in the world but the means by which there is a world for me. He develops the notions of the body schema (*schéma corporel*), motor intentionality, and the intentional arc connecting the body to its perceptual environment. The world is given not as a collection of discrete objects to a detached observer but as a structured field of solicitations to an embodied being.

His earlier *La Structure du comportement* (1942) had taken on Gestalt psychology and behaviorism along similar lines. His late, unfinished *Le Visible et l'invisible* (published posthumously 1964) attempts an ontology of "the flesh" (*la chair*) — a chiasmic intertwining of body and world that he proposed as an alternative to both Cartesian dualism and the Sartrean opposition of being-in-itself and being-for-itself.

Politically, until 1950 he was a close ally of Sartre on the postwar left and co-edited *Les Temps modernes*. The friendship broke over the Korean War. *Les Aventures de la dialectique* (1955) was his settling of accounts with what he saw as Sartre's uncritical fellow-traveling with Soviet communism.

His account of the body schema and motor intentionality became a central reference point for later embodied-cognition arguments, including [[dreyfus-hubert|Dreyfus]]'s critique of symbolic AI. See [[situated-knowledge]] for the broader topic.

## Key ideas

- The lived body (*corps propre*) as the site of knowing
- The body schema (*schéma corporel*) and motor intentionality
- Perception as pre-reflective engagement with a field
- The primacy of perception over reflection
- The flesh (*la chair*) as ontological category

## Key works

- *The Structure of Behavior* (*La Structure du comportement*, 1942)
- *Phenomenology of Perception* (*Phénoménologie de la perception*, 1945)
- *Humanism and Terror* (1947)
- *Sense and Non-Sense* (1948)
- *The Adventures of the Dialectic* (1955)
- *The Visible and the Invisible* (posthumous, 1964)
