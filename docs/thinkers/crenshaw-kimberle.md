# Kimberlé Crenshaw

American legal scholar, professor of law at UCLA and Columbia, and a founding figure of critical race theory. She is known for coining "intersectionality" in two essays in the late 1980s and early 1990s and for the sustained development of the concept across the following three decades.

"Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex: A Black Feminist Critique of Antidiscrimination Doctrine, Feminist Theory and Antiracist Politics" (*University of Chicago Legal Forum*, 1989) is the founding statement. Reading three federal cases in which Black women plaintiffs lost employment discrimination claims because courts treated race and sex as mutually exclusive bases of discrimination, Crenshaw argues that single-axis frameworks systematically erase those positioned at intersections. A Black woman who is discriminated against *as a Black woman* — for reasons that no Black man and no white woman would face — cannot be heard by a doctrine that requires her to choose between a race claim and a sex claim. The follow-up essay, "Mapping the Margins: Intersectionality, Identity Politics, and Violence Against Women of Color" (*Stanford Law Review*, 1991), extends the analysis to domestic violence and rape law.

Intersectionality has since traveled far beyond its original legal context, becoming a standard framework in sociology, feminist theory, public policy, and political organizing. Crenshaw and [[collins-patricia-hill|Patricia Hill Collins]] are the figures most identified with its development as social theory. Within the [[situated-knowledge]] conversation, intersectionality functions as the principal answer to the essentialism charge against standpoint theory: it specifies *which* standpoint, at *what* level of generality, against *which* intersecting structures of power.

Crenshaw co-founded and directs the African American Policy Forum (1996) and the Columbia Law School Center for Intersectionality and Social Policy Studies (2011). With Neil Gotanda, Gary Peller, and Kendall Thomas she co-edited *Critical Race Theory: The Key Writings That Formed the Movement* (New Press, 1995), the standard collection of the field's foundational essays. Her #SayHerName campaign (2014–) documents Black women and girls killed by police, addressing the gendered erasures of mainstream coverage of police violence.

## Key ideas

- Intersectionality — the irreducibility of compound social positions to single axes
- The structural erasure of Black women under single-axis antidiscrimination doctrine
- Identity politics as a positive resource when properly grounded
- Critical race theory as legal scholarship
- The gendered politics of police violence (#SayHerName)
