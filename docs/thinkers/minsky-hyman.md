# Hyman Minsky

American economist, professor for most of his career at Washington University in St. Louis, and a figure whose work was regarded by the mainstream profession as obscure and dated throughout his lifetime — and who, within a decade of his death, came to look like the clearest-eyed analyst of capitalist financial dynamics any 20th-century American economist had produced. "This is a Minsky moment" became, after 2008, the phrase reached for by journalists, central bankers, and finance writers trying to describe what had happened; the economist it named had been out of fashion for his entire career.

Minsky was the son of Menshevik émigrés to Chicago, took his BA at the University of Chicago in 1941, and after the Second World War completed his Harvard PhD in 1954 under Joseph Schumpeter and (after Schumpeter's death in 1950) Wassily Leontief. The Schumpeterian inheritance — innovation, credit, the boom-and-bust dynamics of capitalist development — is visible throughout his later work, alongside the Keynesian one; Minsky read Keynes through Schumpeter as much as through the Cambridge tradition. He held appointments at Brown, Berkeley, and Washington University before moving in 1990 to the Jerome Levy Economics Institute at Bard College, which became the institutional home of post-Keynesian and Minskyan research and remains so.

Minsky's project was to recover what he regarded as Keynes's most important insight — that capitalist economies with developed financial systems are *inherently* unstable, and that this instability is generated endogenously by the normal operation of those systems rather than imposed from outside by shocks. The argument is summarized in his *financial instability hypothesis*: in the wake of a crisis, financial relations are conservative (borrowers and lenders stick to *hedge* finance, in which expected income covers both principal and interest); as memory of the crisis fades and stable growth continues, the system migrates toward *speculative* finance (income covers interest but principal must be rolled over) and eventually *Ponzi* finance (income covers neither; the position is sustained only by capital gains or further borrowing). The transition happens not because anyone is irrational but because the rational response to stability is to take on more risk. Stability, in Minsky's formulation, is destabilizing.

*John Maynard Keynes* (1975) and *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy* (1986) are his two major books, and for most of his life they were largely unread outside a small circle of post-Keynesian economists (Joan [[robinson-joan|Robinson]]'s tradition) and the occasional practitioner. The 1975 book is his reading of Keynes against the "neoclassical synthesis" of Samuelson, Solow, and Hicks — the argument that mainstream Keynesianism had domesticated the *General Theory* by stripping out its financial-instability material, leaving an IS-LM apparatus that could not see crises coming. The 2008 financial crisis — an almost textbook case of the transition from hedge through speculative to Ponzi finance in the American housing market — brought Minsky belated recognition. *Stabilizing* was reissued by McGraw-Hill in September 2008, with a new foreword by Henry Kaufman; Paul McCulley of PIMCO had popularized "Minsky moment" in 1998 (originally describing the Russia–LTCM crisis) and the phrase entered general financial vocabulary in 2007–08. His institutional recommendations — Big Government as automatic stabilizer; Big Bank as lender of last resort; structural reforms to contain the Ponzi phase before it consumed the system — have become reference points for contemporary financial-regulation debates. L. Randall Wray, his principal student, carried the framework forward at the Levy Institute and at the University of Missouri-Kansas City, where Minsky's monetary-theoretic work became one of the foundations of Modern Monetary Theory. See [[post-2008-debates-in-economics]] for the broader context of the revival. Minsky was the Keynesian the Keynesians had forgotten.

## Key ideas

- The financial instability hypothesis
- Hedge, speculative, and Ponzi finance
- Stability is destabilizing
- Big Government and Big Bank as stabilizing institutions
- Capitalism as inherently crisis-prone
