# Ahmir "Questlove" Thompson

American drummer, bandleader, filmmaker, DJ, and writer. Co-founder with Tariq "Black Thought" Trotter of The Roots, the Philadelphia hip-hop group formed in 1987 that became one of the longest-running and most musically restless ensembles in the genre, and since 2014 the house band of *The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon*. Thompson has used the visibility of that perch to build a parallel career as a public archivist and explainer of Black American music.

His books work the same territory in prose. *Mo' Meta Blues: The World According to Questlove* (2013), written with Ben Greenman, is a memoir-cum-listening-history that became an unlikely critical success. *Soul Train: The Music, Dance, and Style of a Generation* (2013), *Creative Quest* (2018), *Music Is History* (2021), and *Hip-Hop Is History* (2024) extend the project — part oral history, part personal essay, part curatorial argument that the post-1960s Black popular music tradition is a coherent intellectual inheritance that deserves to be taught as such.

*Summer of Soul (…Or, When the Revolution Could Not Be Televised)* (2021), his directorial debut, assembled and contextualized previously unseen footage of the 1969 Harlem Cultural Festival — Nina Simone, Sly and the Family Stone, Mahalia Jackson with Mavis Staples, Stevie Wonder, the 5th Dimension, B.B. King, and others performing over six weekends in Mount Morris Park while Woodstock was happening a hundred miles north. The film won the Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature in 2022. *Sly Lives! (aka The Burden of Black Genius)* (2025) extended his work on Sly Stone. He teaches at New York University's Tisch School of the Arts and co-founded the music site Okayplayer.

## Key themes

- Black popular music as a continuous intellectual tradition
- The drummer as historian and curator
- Live performance and the bandleader's craft
- Archival recovery of erased Black cultural events
- Hip-hop's relationship to soul, funk, jazz, and gospel
