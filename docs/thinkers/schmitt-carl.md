# Carl Schmitt

German jurist and political theorist (11 July 1888, Plettenberg, Westphalia – 7 April 1985, Plettenberg). The most consequential right-wing political theorist of the twentieth century, both for his work and for his political conduct: he joined the Nazi Party on 1 May 1933, served briefly as "Crown Jurist of the Third Reich," and although he fell from Nazi favor in 1936 he never repudiated his early support. He was interned by Allied forces after the war, interrogated at Nuremberg as a possible defendant but never charged, refused to undergo denazification, was barred from teaching, and lived the next forty years in his Westphalian home town as a private scholar.

His work is read despite this history because it sets out, more clearly than any liberal or Marxist counterpart, the criticisms of parliamentary liberal democracy from the authoritarian right, and because those criticisms have been repeatedly drawn upon — by twentieth-century right-wing politics, but also, in qualified form, by Frankfurt School heirs and by parts of the post-1968 left looking for a critique of liberal proceduralism.

*Political Theology: Four Chapters on the Concept of Sovereignty* (1922) opens with the formula for which Schmitt is best known: "Sovereign is he who decides on the exception." Liberal constitutionalism, on Schmitt's reading, masks rather than resolves the question of who in fact decides when normal legal order is suspended; political authority is grounded not in rule-following but in the prior capacity to decide the exception. The book frames modern political concepts as secularized theological ones and argues that the attempt to expel decision and authority from politics in favor of impersonal rules is incoherent.

*The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* (1923) argued that liberal parliamentarism rests on a now-defunct nineteenth-century faith in rational public discussion, and that twentieth-century mass democracy — with its parties, machines, and propaganda — has fatally undermined that ideal, leaving liberal forms but emptying them of substance.

*The Concept of the Political* (1932) advanced the thesis for which Schmitt is most cited today: the political is constituted by the distinction between friend and enemy, where "enemy" is the public, collective other against whom one is prepared to fight. The political is not derived from morality, aesthetics, or economics; it is its own irreducible domain, marked by the possibility of organized killing. Liberal attempts to dissolve the political into market exchange or universal moral norms are, on this view, evasions of the basic structure of collective life.

*The Nomos of the Earth* (1950) extended the analysis to international law and the spatial order of European colonialism. *Constitutional Theory* (1928) is the major systematic statement.

Schmitt's reception is contested in a way few twentieth-century thinkers' is. His political-theology essay and friend-enemy distinction were taken up sympathetically on the post-Marxist left by Chantal Mouffe (*The Democratic Paradox*, 2000; *On the Political*, 2005), who reads them as a needed corrective to Habermasian deliberative liberalism; he was a quiet interlocutor of [[strauss-leo|Leo Strauss]] (their 1932 correspondence was published in 1995); and he has been read most cautiously by writers such as Stephen Holmes (*The Anatomy of Antiliberalism*, 1993) and Jan-Werner Müller (*A Dangerous Mind: Carl Schmitt in Post-War European Thought*, 2003) as the indispensable source for understanding the contemporary authoritarian-right critique of liberal democracy.

## Key ideas

- Sovereignty as the power to decide the exception
- The friend-enemy distinction as the constitutive criterion of the political
- Liberal parliamentarism as a depleted nineteenth-century form
- Political theology — modern political concepts as secularized theological ones
- The *Großraum* and *nomos* as categories of international order
