# David Lay Williams

American political theorist; professor of political science at DePaul University in Chicago, with a PhD in Government from the University of Texas at Austin. His field is the history of political thought, centered on [[rousseau-jj|Rousseau]] and, in his later work, on economic inequality as a problem within the Western canon. He is also affiliated with the University of Chicago's Stone Center for the study of wealth inequality and writes occasional opinion pieces for the New York Times, the Washington Post, and Time.

His first two decades of scholarship were on Rousseau. *Rousseau's Platonic Enlightenment* (2007) argues that Rousseau's thought rests on Platonic foundations — transcendent standards of justice set against the materialism of Hobbes and the *philosophes* — rather than on the purely modern premises he is often assigned. *Rousseau's 'Social Contract': An Introduction* (2014) is a commentary on the whole of that text. With James Farr he co-edited *The General Will: The Evolution of a Concept* (2015), tracing the idea from its theological origins through Rousseau to the present; with Matthew W. Maguire, *The Cambridge Companion to Rousseau's 'Social Contract'* (2024).

*The Greatest of All Plagues: How Economic Inequality Shaped Political Thought from Plato to Marx* (Princeton, 2024) — the title phrase is from Plato's *Laws* — argues that economic inequality, as distinct from poverty, has been a central preoccupation of the canon: chapters on [[plato|Plato]], Jesus and the New Testament, [[hobbes-thomas|Hobbes]], [[rousseau-jj|Rousseau]], [[smith-adam|Adam Smith]], [[mill-js|Mill]], and [[marx-karl|Marx]] reconstruct each thinker's account of what concentrated wealth does to civic bonds, political stability, and the souls of rich and poor alike. The book positions itself against the modern tendency to treat inequality as either a recent discovery or a concern only of the left; it was reviewed in the Washington Post, The Nation, and Nature, and named a book of the year by the New Statesman. It bears directly on the territory of [[inequality]] and [[what-is-fair]].

## Key themes

- Economic inequality, as distinct from poverty, as a continuous preoccupation of Western political thought from Plato to Marx
- Rousseau read as a Platonist: transcendent standards of justice against Hobbesian materialism
- The general will as a concept with a long theological and philosophical history before and after Rousseau
- Canonical thinkers' concern with what concentrated wealth does to civic bonds and political stability, not only with the condition of the poor
