# G. William Domhoff

American sociologist and psychologist (George William Domhoff, born 6 August 1936, Youngstown, Ohio). He took his doctorate in psychology at the University of Miami and spent his career at the University of California, Santa Cruz, where he became a research professor. He pursued two largely separate bodies of work: the empirical study of who holds power in the United States, and the scientific study of dreams and dream content.

His power research extends the program of C. Wright [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] into systematic class and network analysis. *Who Rules America?* (1967), revised across numerous editions into the 2020s, argues that a social upper class rooted in corporate ownership exercises disproportionate power over American government and that this can be demonstrated empirically rather than asserted. Domhoff documents the cohesion of that class through shared institutions — boarding schools, exclusive clubs, the Social Register, intermarriage — and traces its influence through a "policy-planning network" of foundations, think tanks, and policy-discussion groups (the Council on Foreign Relations, the Business Roundtable, the Brookings Institution and others) that develops and circulates policy among corporate and governmental elites. He proposes three operational "indicators of power" — who benefits, who governs, and who wins — for testing the claim empirically.

Related studies apply the method to particular institutions and questions: *The Bohemian Grove and Other Retreats* (1974) on elite social gatherings, *Fat Cats and Democrats* (1972) on wealthy backing of the Democratic Party, and, with Richard Zweigenhaft, *Diversity in the Power Elite* (1998; revised editions), which asks how far the entry of women and racial minorities into elite positions has altered the underlying structure of class power. Domhoff describes his framework as a "class-domination theory of power," distinct from both pluralist and strict Marxist accounts.

## Key ideas

- A **class-domination theory of power**: an ownership-based upper class governs through corporate and policy institutions, demonstrable by empirical test
- The **policy-planning network** of foundations, think tanks, and discussion groups as the mechanism linking corporate wealth to government policy
- Three **indicators of power** — who benefits, who governs, who wins — as operational tests
- Upper-class **social cohesion** through schools, clubs, registers, and intermarriage
