# Katrina Forrester

British political theorist and intellectual historian. She took her PhD at the University of Cambridge in 2013, held a research fellowship at St John's College, Cambridge from 2012 to 2014, and was lecturer in the history of political thought at Queen Mary University of London until moving to Harvard. She is the John L. Loeb Associate Professor of the Social Sciences in the Department of Government and on the Committee on Social Studies at Harvard.

Her *In the Shadow of Justice: Postwar Liberalism and the Remaking of Political Philosophy* (Princeton University Press, 2019) is an intellectual history of John [[rawls-john|Rawls]] and of the field that *A Theory of Justice* (1971) consolidated. Drawing on archival material going back to Rawls's drafts of the 1950s and 1960s, Forrester argues that the book did not so much invent liberal egalitarianism as crystallize a set of preoccupations already in formation within Cold War Anglo-American liberalism — concern with distribution within affluent stable democracies, with the conditions of fair cooperation among free and equal citizens, with ideal theory as a method, and with a constitutive distance from political economy, empire, race, and the international order. The book reconstructs the reception and elaboration of *A Theory of Justice* through the 1970s and 1980s — through [[nozick-robert|Robert Nozick]]'s libertarian counter, the communitarian critique, the feminist critique, the analytic Marxism of G. A. Cohen — and argues that the resulting philosophical apparatus, however internally rigorous, has been imperfectly suited to the political problems of the post–Cold War decades. The book received the Organization of American Historians' Merle Curti Award for Intellectual History, the Society for U.S. Intellectual History's Book Award, and the International Conference for the Study of Political Thought's David and Elaine Spitz Prize.

Forrester writes regular essays and reviews for the *London Review of Books*, the *New Yorker*, *Dissent*, *n+1*, *The Nation*, *Harper's*, the *Guardian*, and the *New Statesman*. Her essay subjects include work and the politics of social reproduction, contemporary feminist theory, the history of British social democracy, and the legacies of Cold War liberalism. She has edited (with Sophie Smith) *Nature, Action and the Future: Political Thought and the Environment* (Cambridge, 2018) and has written on Shulamith Firestone, on the history of housework and care, and on the contemporary politics of unionization. A second book on feminism and social reproduction has been described in interviews and lectures but is unpublished as of 2026.

## Key ideas

- *A Theory of Justice* as the consolidation, not the invention, of postwar liberal egalitarianism
- The Cold War political context that shaped the discipline of Anglo-American political philosophy
- Ideal theory as a methodological choice with substantive consequences
- The mismatch between the inherited Rawlsian apparatus and contemporary political problems (climate, empire, racial capitalism, work)
- Recovery of suppressed alternatives in mid-century political thought
