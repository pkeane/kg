# Clara E. Mattei

Italian economist working in the heterodox tradition. Mattei took her PhD at the Scuola Superiore Sant'Anna in Pisa, taught at The New School for Social Research in New York, and is now associate professor of economics at the University of Tulsa, where she directs the Center for Heterodox Economics.

Her book *The Capital Order: How Economists Invented Austerity and Paved the Way to Fascism* (University of Chicago Press, 2022) reconstructs the origins of austerity policy from archival work in Britain and Italy in the years after World War I. The argument cuts against the standard framing of austerity as a technical response to debt or inflation. Mattei treats austerity instead as a political project to discipline labor and restore capitalist class relations at a moment when those relations were under acute pressure from postwar working-class militancy, war-economy planning, and demands for industrial democracy. She decomposes austerity into three coordinated prongs — fiscal (budget cuts and regressive taxation), monetary (tight money and the return to gold), and industrial (wage suppression and the dismantling of unions) — and argues they function as a system rather than as separate policy choices.

The book's most provocative move is comparative. The Treasury View in 1920s Britain and the policies of Mussolini's finance minister Alberto De Stefani and Bank of Italy governor Bonaldo Stringher are analyzed as variants of the same program; the League of Nations and early IMF-style institutions praised Italian fiscal orthodoxy through the fascist consolidation. Liberal-democratic and fascist regimes, in this reading, converged on the same economic prescription, complicating the conventional account of fascism as an economic rupture from liberalism. The book situates Mattei in a lineage that runs through [[polanyi-karl]] on the political construction of the self-regulating market and through critical readings of [[keynes-jm]] — Keynes is read sympathetically but as part of a profession whose expert authority was used to depoliticize class conflict.

## Key ideas

- Austerity as a political project to protect the "capital order," not a technical response to debt
- The three prongs of austerity: fiscal, monetary, industrial
- 1920s Britain and fascist Italy as parallel laboratories of austerity policy
- The role of expert economic authority in depoliticizing class conflict
- Continuity rather than rupture between liberal and fascist economic policy in the interwar period
