# William J. J. Gordon

American inventor and psychologist (William John Joseph Gordon, 9 September 1919 – 30 June 2003). He held numerous United States patents across unrelated fields and worked in the Invention Design Group at Arthur D. Little, where in the 1950s he and George M. [[prince-george-m|Prince]] developed Synectics, a method of creative problem-solving. Gordon set out the approach in *Synectics: The Development of Creative Capacity* (1961), the founding statement of the method, and co-founded Synectics, Inc.

Gordon's emphasis was on the *operational mechanisms* of creativity, organized around the formula of "making the familiar strange and the strange familiar." The method works chiefly through directed use of analogy, which Gordon classified into four types: direct analogy (comparison with a parallel case, often from biology or nature), personal analogy (imaginatively identifying with the element of a problem), symbolic analogy (a compressed, often poetic image of the problem), and fantasy analogy (suspending real-world constraints to ask how one might wish the problem solved). The aim was to make the unconscious, metaphor-driven processes that Gordon believed underlie invention deliberate and repeatable. He later founded Synectics Education Systems and applied the method to teaching, including in *The Metaphorical Way of Learning and Knowing* (1971).

## Key ideas

- **Synectics**: "making the familiar strange and the strange familiar" as the engine of invention
- The four analogy mechanisms — **direct, personal, symbolic, and fantasy** analogy
- Treating metaphor and analogy as deliberate, teachable techniques rather than spontaneous gifts
- Application of the method to education and learning

## Key works

- *Synectics: The Development of Creative Capacity* (1961)
- *The Metaphorical Way of Learning and Knowing* (1971)
