# Pierre Bourdieu

French sociologist (1 August 1930, Denguin, Pyrénées-Atlantiques – 23 January 2002, Paris). Educated at the École Normale Supérieure (philosophy), he was sent to Algeria for his military service in 1958 and remained there through the war of independence, conducting the ethnographic fieldwork on Kabyle society and on uprooted Algerian peasants and workers that turned him from philosophy to sociology. He returned to teach at Lille, then at the École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales (EHESS), and from 1981 held the chair in sociology at the Collège de France until his death.

Bourdieu's project, developed across four decades and some thirty books, is the sociological analysis of how social hierarchies reproduce themselves not primarily through coercion but through the everyday operation of culture, taste, education, and the body. The conceptual vocabulary he built is now standard sociological idiom: *habitus* (the durable dispositions of perception, taste, and action acquired by growing up in a particular social position, which dispose agents to act in ways that reproduce that position without their needing consciously to choose); *field* (a structured social space — art, science, journalism, politics — with its own stakes, hierarchies, and rules of play); *cultural capital* (educational credentials, refined taste, linguistic facility, all distributed unequally and convertible into economic and social advantage); *symbolic violence* (the misrecognition by the dominated of the legitimacy of their domination); and *doxa* (the taken-for-granted assumptions of a field, invisible to its participants).

*Reproduction in Education, Society and Culture* (with Jean-Claude Passeron, 1970) argued that the apparently meritocratic French educational system in fact reproduces existing class inequality by rewarding the cultural capital already possessed by middle-class children. *Distinction: A Social Critique of the Judgement of Taste* (1979), the work for which he is most widely read in English, used a vast survey of French cultural preferences to show that aesthetic taste is structured by class position and operates as a marker of distinction and a means of exclusion. *Homo Academicus* (1984) applied the same analysis reflexively to French academia itself, *The State Nobility* (1989) to the elite *grandes écoles* and the higher civil service.

In his last years Bourdieu became a public intellectual on the European left, his interventions collected in *The Weight of the World: Social Suffering in Contemporary Society* (1993), a multi-voiced documentary ethnography of those bearing the costs of the neoliberal restructuring of the French welfare state, and *Acts of Resistance: Against the Tyranny of the Market* (1998) and *Firing Back* (2001), short polemics against what he called "the tyranny of the market" and against the unreflective neoliberalism of policy and media elites.

## Key ideas

- Habitus, field, and cultural capital
- Symbolic violence and the misrecognition of legitimate hierarchy
- Education as a mechanism of class reproduction, not class mobility
- Taste and distinction as social weapons
- Reflexive sociology: the sociologist's own field as object of study
