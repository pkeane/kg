# Niklas Luhmann

German sociologist (8 December 1927, Lüneburg – 6 November 1998, Oerlinghausen). He trained in law at Freiburg and worked for a decade in public administration in Lower Saxony before a 1960–61 year at Harvard, where he studied with Talcott [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]. From 1968 he held the first chair in sociology at the newly founded University of Bielefeld. On his appointment he registered his research project in the standard form: "Theory of society; duration: 30 years; costs: none." He regarded it as completed with *Die Gesellschaft der Gesellschaft* (1997), the year before his death.

Luhmann built a general theory of society as composed of self-reproducing systems. In his mature formulation, set out in *Soziale Systeme* (1984), social systems are systems of *communication* — not of persons or actions — that reproduce themselves from their own elements. Borrowing the concept of **autopoiesis** from the biologists Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela, he argued that modern society is **functionally differentiated** into operationally closed subsystems — politics, law, economy, science, religion, art, education, the mass media — each running on its own binary code (the political system on power / no-power, law on legal / illegal, the economy on payment / non-payment). Because each subsystem can observe the others only through its own code, no subsystem can directly steer another: the political system cannot command the economy or science, a thesis with sharp implications for any project of political planning. The framework drew on the phenomenology of meaning (Husserl) and on George Spencer-Brown's calculus of distinctions (*Laws of Form*), which Luhmann adapted into an account of **second-order observation** — the observation of how other observers observe.

His running disagreement with Jürgen [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]], published as *Theorie der Gesellschaft oder Sozialtechnologie* (1971), counterposed Luhmann's systems theory to Habermas's theory of communicative action: where Habermas sought normative grounds for consensus, Luhmann offered a deliberately non-normative, observer-relative description of how systems reduce complexity.

Luhmann attributed his productivity — roughly 70 books and 400 articles — to a paper card index, or *Zettelkasten*, of some 90,000 numbered and cross-referenced slips, which he described in a 1981 essay as a communication partner. The index has since been digitized by the Niklas Luhmann Archive in Bielefeld and is often cited as a precursor of digital linked-note systems.

## Key ideas

- **Autopoiesis**: social systems reproduce their own elements (communications) from their own elements, and are operationally closed
- **Functional differentiation**: modern society divides into subsystems each governed by a binary code, none able to steer the others
- **Communication, not action or persons**, as the basic unit of the social
- **Second-order observation**: observing how observers observe, after George Spencer-Brown's *Laws of Form*
- **Double contingency** and the reduction of complexity as the problem social order solves
