# Martin Gilens & Benjamin Page

Martin Gilens (professor of public policy and political science at UCLA, previously at Princeton) and Benjamin Page (b. 1940; Gordon Scott Fulcher Professor Emeritus of Decision Making at Northwestern) are American political scientists whose collaborative work tests whose preferences actually shape United States policy. Their 2014 article "Testing Theories of American Politics: Elites, Interest Groups, and Average Citizens" (*Perspectives on Politics*), drawing on roughly 1,800 policy questions between 1981 and 2002, found that economic elites and organized business groups have substantial independent influence on federal policy while the preferences of average citizens have little or no independent effect once elite and group preferences are accounted for. They developed the argument jointly in *Democracy in America? What Has Gone Wrong and What We Can Do About It* (2017).

Each has a substantial individual body of work. Gilens's *Affluence and Influence: Economic Inequality and Political Power in America* (2012) shows that when the preferences of the affluent diverge from those of the poor and middle class, policy outcomes track the affluent; his earlier *Why Americans Hate Welfare* (1999) examined the racialization of antipoverty politics. Page is a longtime scholar of public opinion (*The Rational Public*, 1992, with Robert Y. Shapiro) and a coauthor of *Billionaires and Stealth Politics* (2018), on how very wealthy individuals shape policy quietly, including on taxation. Their findings are a central empirical support for the claim that the wealthy exercise disproportionate influence over tax policy. See [[how-to-tax-the-wealthy]].
