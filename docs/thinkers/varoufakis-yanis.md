# Yanis Varoufakis

Greek economist and politician. Varoufakis taught economics at the University of Essex, the University of East Anglia, the University of Sydney, the University of Athens, and the University of Texas at Austin, working primarily in game theory and macroeconomics from a position he describes as heterodox and broadly Marxian-Keynesian. He served as Greece's finance minister from January to July 2015, during the climactic phase of the Eurozone crisis, and resigned the day after the July 2015 referendum in which Greek voters rejected the Troika's bailout terms but the Tsipras government accepted them regardless. His memoir of that period, *Adults in the Room* (2017), is the principal inside account of the negotiation between the Syriza government and the European Central Bank, European Commission, and IMF, and the leading first-person statement of the conflict between a democratic electoral mandate and the institutional creditors of a monetary union (see [[2008-financial-crisis-and-fallout]] and [[democratic-socialism-and-scale]]).

His economic writing centers on global imbalances and the political construction of monetary order. *The Global Minotaur* (2011) reads the post-1971 world economy as a system in which U.S. trade and budget deficits recycled global surpluses back into American financial markets, and treats the 2008 crisis as the breakdown of that mechanism. *And the Weak Suffer What They Must?* (2016) extends the analysis to the design flaws of the euro. Across this work Varoufakis is consistently critical of mainstream economics' claim to technical neutrality, a stance he shares with [[mattei-clara]], [[stiglitz-joseph]], and the Polanyian tradition. After 2015 he co-founded the pan-European movement DiEM25 (2016) and the Greek party MeRA25 (2018).

*Technofeudalism: What Killed Capitalism* (2023) argues that the platform economy has displaced markets and profit with digital fiefs and rent: "cloud capital" owned by a handful of firms (Amazon, Apple, the major platforms) extracts "cloud rent" from vassal-capitalists and users alike, a structure he calls a successor system to capitalism rather than a stage within it. The thesis overlaps with [[zuboff-shoshana]] on surveillance capitalism and [[doctorow-cory]] on platform lock-in, but the claim that this constitutes feudalism rather than a mutation of capitalism is contested; Evgeny Morozov's "Critique of Techno-Feudal Reason" (*New Left Review*, 2022) is the most developed rebuttal, arguing that rent extraction remains internal to capitalist accumulation.

## Key ideas

- The post-1971 "Global Minotaur": U.S. deficits recycling world surpluses, and 2008 as its collapse
- The euro's design as a creditor-favoring monetary order without fiscal or political union
- Democratic mandate versus the institutional creditors of a monetary union (Greece, 2015)
- Critique of mainstream economics' claim to technical, apolitical authority
- "Technofeudalism": cloud capital and cloud rent as a successor system to capitalism (contested)
