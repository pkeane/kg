Donna Haraway

1944 – ? · American
#feminism#science-studies#epistemology#philosophy-of-science#posthumanism

American historian of science and feminist theorist, professor emerita of the History of Consciousness program at UC Santa Cruz. Her doctorate (Yale, 1972) was in biology before she moved into the history and philosophy of science. Her work reshaped feminist epistemology and science and technology studies.

"A Cyborg Manifesto" (Socialist Review, 1985) proposes the cyborg — a hybrid of machine and organism, "a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction" — as a figure for a feminism freed from essentialist appeals to a natural female body or a unitary female experience. The Manifesto refuses the boundaries between human and animal, organism and machine, and physical and non-physical that structured Western humanism, and argues that feminist politics is better served by an embrace of impure, hybrid identities than by the search for a pre-technological wholeness.

"Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective" (Feminist Studies 14:3, 1988) is the founding statement of the position the title names. Haraway argues against the "god trick" of disembodied scientific objectivity — the pretense of a view from nowhere — and equally against the social-constructionist relativism she calls its "perfect mirror twin." Knowledge is always produced from somewhere — from particular bodies, instruments, histories — and partial, located perspective is the condition under which knowledge claims can be held accountable. She reframes objectivity as "embodied objectivity," answerable rather than transcendent. The essay has become a standard reference across feminist theory, science studies, philosophy of science, and qualitative social research. See Situated Knowledge and the Embodied Knower for the broader topic.

Her later work has moved further into multispecies studies, animal-human entanglement, and what she calls "making kin" with non-human others. When Species Meet (2008) develops a non-anthropocentric ethic of companion species; Staying with the Trouble (2016) proposes "Chthulucene" as a term for a present in which human and non-human futures are inseparable.

Key ideas

  • The cyborg as figure for postnatural, anti-essentialist feminism
  • Situated knowledges and the "god trick"
  • Embodied objectivity versus the view from nowhere
  • The partiality of perspective as condition of accountability
  • Companion species and multispecies kinship
