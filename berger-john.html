John Berger

1926 – 2017 · English
#art-criticism#marxism#aesthetics#visual-culture#photography#fiction

English art critic, novelist, painter, and essayist. Berger trained as a painter and began writing art criticism for the New Statesman in the early 1950s, where his Marxist commitments and his attacks on the contemporary art establishment made him a contentious figure. Over six decades he moved between criticism, fiction, poetry, screenwriting, and documentary collaboration, and from 1962 he lived mostly outside Britain, settling in the 1970s in the peasant village of Quincy in the French Alps, whose rural life supplied much of his later writing.

His 1972 BBC television series and accompanying book Ways of Seeing are the work for which he is most widely known. The novel G. won the Booker Prize the same year; in his acceptance speech Berger condemned the prize sponsor Booker McConnell for its colonial-era plantation history in the Caribbean and donated half the prize money to the British Black Panther movement, using the other half to fund his study of migrant labour. He produced several books with the photographer Jean Mohr, including A Fortunate Man (1967), a portrait of a country doctor, and A Seventh Man (1975), on migrant workers in Europe. His late fiction includes the Into Their Labours trilogy (Pig Earth, Once in Europa, Lilac and Flag), chronicling peasant life and its disappearance.

Berger's criticism draws openly on Walter Benjamin — Ways of Seeing takes its starting point from Benjamin's essay on mechanical reproduction — and on the cultural Marxism of Raymond Williams. He read images in terms of social and economic relations: what oil painting did for property, what publicity does for capitalism, how reproduction changes the meaning of a work. He is frequently grouped with Susan Sontag and Roland Barthes as a writer who brought close attention to images and photographs into general intellectual discussion.

Ways of Seeing

Ways of Seeing (1972) was made for BBC television by Berger with the producer Mike Dibb and conceived partly as a rejoinder to Kenneth Clark's earlier series Civilisation (1969), which Berger regarded as a complacent celebration of the European canon. The book version compresses the four episodes into seven "essays," three of them composed only of images.

Its central arguments:

  • Reproduction changes meaning. Following Benjamin, Berger argues that photographic reproduction strips the painting of its unique presence and the authority that came with its physical location, making the image mobile, fragmentary, and available to new uses — including uses that contradict the original.
  • Oil painting and property. The European oil-painting tradition from roughly 1500 to 1900 is read as a way of depicting and celebrating possessions; the genre renders things — land, objects, people — as ownable, and the painting itself functions as a record of what the patron owned.
  • The gaze and the depiction of women. In the section on the female nude, Berger argues that women in the tradition are painted to be looked at by an assumed male spectator-owner, summarized in the formulas "men act and women appear" and "men look at women; women watch themselves being looked at." This account anticipated and fed into later feminist work on the gaze.
  • Publicity as the successor to oil painting. The final essay argues that advertising ("publicity") inherits the visual language of oil painting but reverses its function: where oil painting displayed what the owner already had, publicity sells the spectator a glamorous image of a future self, manufacturing envy and tying it to consumption.

The series and book became a standard text in art history, media studies, and Cultural Studies, and a frequent point of departure for feminist film and art criticism.

Key themes

  • The social and economic relations encoded in images
  • Reproduction and the changed status of the artwork
  • The European nude and the spectator's gaze
  • Advertising as the heir of oil painting
  • Peasant labour, migration, and rural disappearance
  • Criticism, fiction, and photography as overlapping forms
