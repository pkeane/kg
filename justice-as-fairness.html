Justice as Fairness

17th century–present
#political-theory#liberalism#justice#philosophy#ethics

The oldest question in political philosophy is the most basic: why should I obey? If individuals generally pursue their own interests — and every serious tradition from Hobbes forward has conceded that they do — then the laws, regulations, and institutions that bind us need some account of their authority beyond the fact that they exist. The tradition gathered here gives one answer. Political order is legitimate when it is fair to those who live under it: when its basic rules could be accepted, on reflection, by the people they govern. The code we live by binds us because, and to the extent that, it is justifiable to everyone it binds.

The classical formulations worked through the idea of consent. Hobbes grounded the state in our rational self-interest in escaping the war of all against all; Locke limited it by prior natural rights; Hume rejected the contract as fiction and settled for habit and convention; Smith showed how self-interested market behavior could be harnessed inside a framework of moral norms and public law; Mill set the liberal limit with the harm principle. None of these thinkers was naive about human motivation. All of them argued that a well-designed political order was the condition under which self-interested beings could live decent lives together.

The tradition's twentieth-century reformulation is John Rawls's. A Theory of Justice (1971) asked what principles free and rational people would choose to govern their society if they did not know in advance what position they would occupy within it. The thought experiment — the "original position" behind a "veil of ignorance" — yields a conception of justice keyed not to consent but to fairness: the basic structure of society is legitimate insofar as it could be defended to every person bound by it, including those who end up at the bottom. Rawls called this "justice as fairness," and it is now the anchor for almost every serious argument in contemporary liberal political philosophy.

The heirs — Dworkin, Nussbaum, Sen, Anderson, and in a parallel German key Habermas — have extended, revised, and argued with Rawls while staying within the basic project: that a just political order is one whose rules could be justified to all the people who live under them. The critics — Nozick from the libertarian right, Sandel, MacIntyre, and Walzer from the communitarian side — have sharpened the view by exposing its blind spots. The institutional form this tradition tends to recommend is what regulated capitalism calls the welfare state: markets harnessed inside a framework of law and public provision designed to be fair to everyone, especially the least advantaged.

Annotated bibliography

Classical foundations: self-interest, consent, and the rule of law

  • Thomas Hobbes, Leviathan (1651) — the founding argument of modern political philosophy. Without a common power, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short," and the rational choice for self-interested individuals is to transfer their natural liberty to a sovereign strong enough to enforce peace. Every later social-contract argument argues on Hobbes's turf.
  • John Locke, Two Treatises of Government (1689) — against Hobbes's absolutism: the state of nature is already governed by natural rights to life, liberty, and property, and legitimate government exists only to protect them. When it fails, resistance is justified. The operating system of Anglo-American liberalism; Jefferson lifted Locke almost verbatim.
  • David Hume, A Treatise of Human Nature (1739–40) and the political essays — Hume rejected the social contract as historical fiction and grounded political authority in habit, interest, and convention. A useful corrective to the tendency to overread consent: most of the time we obey because we always have.
  • Adam Smith, The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759) and The Wealth of Nations (1776) — the two books belong together. Smith's markets work because they are embedded in moral norms and the rule of law, not because atoms pursue utility. He warned sharply against the tendency of merchants to combine against the public and against the ways the division of labor could deaden workers' minds.
  • John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859) — the harm principle: individuals should be free to act as they choose except where their actions harm others. The canonical liberal statement of liberty's limits, and the bridge from classical liberalism toward the twentieth-century welfare state.

Rawls and the fairness reformulation

  • John Rawls, A Theory of Justice (1971) — the single most important work of Anglo-American political philosophy in the twentieth century. The original position and veil of ignorance as devices of justification; the two principles (equal basic liberties, then inequalities permitted only if they benefit the least advantaged); "justice as fairness" as the name for the view. Every subsequent debate about distributive justice is downstream of this book.
  • John Rawls, Political Liberalism (1993) — the later reformulation. Given the fact of reasonable pluralism — that free citizens will disagree about ultimate questions — how can they still share a political framework? Rawls's answer is the "overlapping consensus": a political conception acceptable from multiple moral and religious starting points.
  • Samuel Freeman, Rawls (Routledge, 2007) — by the editor of Rawls's Collected Papers; the authoritative single-volume guide and the best place to go after reading Rawls himself.
  • Thomas Pogge, John Rawls: His Life and Theory of Justice (2007) — a short, direct introduction by a former student who went on to extend Rawls to global justice.

The contemporary heirs

  • Ronald Dworkin, Sovereign Virtue (2000) and Justice for Hedgehogs (2011) — the most worked-out post-Rawlsian theory of distributive justice: "equality of resources," with its hypothetical insurance markets and the distinction between option-luck and brute-luck inequalities. Law's Empire (1986) extends the fairness framework to legal reasoning: law as a principled interpretive practice expressing "equal concern and respect."
  • Martha Nussbaum, Frontiers of Justice (2006) and Creating Capabilities (2011) — the "capabilities approach," developed with Sen, extends Rawls: justice requires securing a threshold list of central human capabilities (life, bodily health, bodily integrity, affiliation, practical reason, and six others) for all members of a society. More Aristotelian than Rawls but inside the same liberal-egalitarian conversation.
  • Amartya Sen, The Idea of Justice (2009) — Sen's friendly critique: rather than specify a single ideal set of institutions (as Rawls did), compare actual arrangements and ask which are more just than others. Development as Freedom (1999) is the earlier and more accessible application of the capability approach to the real world of poverty and underdevelopment.
  • Elizabeth Anderson, Private Government (2017) and The Imperative of Integration (2010) — the American philosopher most directly continuing the Rawlsian project in the direction of regulated capitalism and workplace democracy. "Relational equality" reframes the central egalitarian concern from the distribution of stuff to the avoidance of hierarchy and domination. Private Government is short and essential: it argues that the modern American workplace is a de-facto authoritarian state that classical liberalism ought to find horrifying. Hijacked (2023) is her recent historical argument that the work ethic, originally an egalitarian Protestant critique of idle aristocrats, was captured in the nineteenth century to discipline workers.
  • Jürgen Habermas, Between Facts and Norms (1992) — the German parallel. Democratic legitimacy rests on procedures of inclusive deliberation among free and equal citizens; laws bind because they could be accepted through reasoned argument. Rawls and Habermas held a famous exchange in the 1990s and converged more than they disagreed.

The critics who sharpen the view

  • Robert Nozick, Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974) — the libertarian reply to Rawls, written by a Harvard colleague and published three years after A Theory of Justice. Any redistributive state violates Lockean rights; a "minimal state" limited to protection of persons, property, and contracts is the only state that can be justified. The indispensable opposing position; reading Rawls without Nozick is reading one side of an argument.
  • Michael Sandel, Liberalism and the Limits of Justice (1982) and The Tyranny of Merit (2020) — the communitarian critique. Rawls's original position presupposes an "unencumbered self" abstracted from the attachments and commitments that actually constitute moral identity. Tyranny of Merit extends the argument: Rawlsian meritocratic liberalism produced the hubris of the winners and the humiliation of the losers, and the populist backlash was the predictable result.
  • Alasdair MacIntyre, After Virtue (1981) — a deeper critique: modern liberal philosophy (Rawls included) has lost the tradition-embedded moral framework within which its vocabulary once made sense. We are left with moral fragments that no longer compose a coherent ethical life. Searching, demanding, and not easily answered.
  • Michael Walzer, Spheres of Justice (1983) — against Rawls's unified theory: different social goods (money, healthcare, offices, love, citizenship, political power) should be distributed by different criteria, each according to the meaning the good carries within its community. A more pluralist fairness; a reminder that what fairness requires depends on what is being distributed.

The institutional application

  • See The Welfare State Under Pressure for the political-economy literature on regulated capitalism, progressive taxation, and public provision — the institutional form this philosophical tradition tends to recommend, and the site of most of its real-world contests over the past half-century.
