What Is Capitalism and Where Does It Come From?

18th century–present
#capitalism#political-economy#economic-history#marxism#political-marxism#intellectual-history

The two questions are tied together. A definition of capitalism implies an origin story; an origin story implies a definition. The major modern accounts can be sorted by where they place the threshold — at the point where markets exist, at the point where merchant capital becomes dominant, at the point where labor itself becomes a commodity, or at the point where producers become market-dependent rather than merely market-engaged. Each threshold yields a different chronology and a different politics.

One broad division runs through the literature. On one side is what Ellen Meiksins Wood called the commercialization model: capitalism as the natural result of removing obstacles to trade, the gradual quantitative expansion of merchant activity, an outgrowth of an ancient human propensity to exchange. Adam Smith's "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange" (Wealth of Nations I.ii) is the founding statement; Hayek's account of markets as spontaneous order is its 20th-century version. On the other side are the accounts that treat capitalism as a historically specific institutional construction — late, local, and requiring force to establish. Marx's chapters on primitive accumulation in Capital vol. 1, Polanyi's "fictitious commodities," Robert Brenner's agrarian class-structure thesis, and Wood's Origin of Capitalism belong on this side. They disagree among themselves about mechanism and date — but they share the claim that capitalism is not what you get when nothing is in the way.

The most compact version of the historicist claim is that capitalism is the social order in which labor becomes a commodity. Marx makes the point at the level of mechanism: capitalism presupposes a class of people separated from the means of production and therefore compelled to sell their labor-power. Polanyi makes the point at the level of institutions: treating labor, land, and money as ordinary commodities is a 19th-century invention, not a natural state, and the resulting "self-regulating market" provoked the "double movement" of social self-defense that defined the politics of the early 20th century. Wood and Brenner make the point at the level of historical sequence: it was in the English countryside between roughly the 16th and 17th centuries that producers first became market-dependent — compelled by competition rather than coerced by extra-economic means — and from that agrarian conjuncture capitalism's distinctive logic spread.

This page is the entry to a debate, not a settled summary. The annotated bibliography below moves from the naturalist account through its main historicist challengers; for the specifically American historiography of capitalism and slavery, see The New History of Capitalism; for the racial dimension of capitalism's origins, see Racial Capitalism; for the more general intellectual frame, see Political Economy.

Annotated bibliography

The naturalist account

  • Adam Smith, An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations (1776), Book I, ch. ii — the founding statement that the division of labor arises from "a certain propensity in human nature… to truck, barter, and exchange one thing for another." Read with Book III on the rise of cities and commerce out of feudal Europe. Smith himself was more historically careful than his later reception, and The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759) shows he never thought market exchange could float free of moral norms — but the Smithian story of capitalism as the unobstructed expression of human propensity is what successors absorbed.
  • Friedrich Hayek, The Constitution of Liberty (1960) and Law, Legislation and Liberty (1973–79) — the most intellectually serious 20th-century version of the naturalist account. The market is not designed; it is "spontaneous order" (cosmos) — an evolved institution that aggregates dispersed knowledge no central planner can match. Capitalism on this account is the long product of cultural selection, not a project to be dated.
  • Ludwig von Mises, Human Action (1949) — the Austrian-school companion. Market exchange is the rational form of human cooperation under the division of labor; the alternatives reduce to chaos or coercion.

Polanyi: capitalism as institutional construction

  • Karl Polanyi, The Great Transformation: The Political and Economic Origins of Our Time (1944) — the central modern statement that the self-regulating market is not a natural condition but a 19th-century political project, imposed in Britain between roughly 1834 (new Poor Laws) and 1870 (gold standard consolidation). Labor, land, and money are "fictitious commodities" — treating them as ordinary goods exposes society to forces that will tear it apart, and provokes the "double movement" of factory acts, unions, welfare measures, protectionism, and eventually fascism. The book to read first if the question is what kind of thing capitalism is.
  • Fred Block and Margaret Somers, The Power of Market Fundamentalism: Karl Polanyi's Critique (2014) — the outstanding contemporary application of Polanyi to neoliberalism and the politics of market fundamentalism.
  • Gareth Dale, Karl Polanyi: The Limits of the Market (2010) — the best systematic interpretive study of Polanyi's thought.

Marx and primitive accumulation

  • Karl Marx, Capital, vol. 1 (1867), Part VIII ("So-Called Primitive Accumulation"), chs. 26–33 — the historical argument that capitalism presupposes a class separated from the means of production, and that this separation was produced in England by enclosure, expropriation, the destruction of customary tenure, and the legal terrorization of "free" labor through vagrancy laws and workhouse discipline. The chapter ends with the lines about the rosy dawn of capitalism being written "in letters of blood and fire." Read alongside Part I, ch. 1 on the commodity form and Part II, ch. 6 on the sale of labor-power.
  • Friedrich Engels, The Condition of the Working Class in England (1845) — the empirical companion: the human texture of early industrial Manchester observed by Engels in his early twenties.
  • Maurice Dobb, Studies in the Development of Capitalism (1946) — the first major 20th-century Marxist historical synthesis of the transition from feudalism to capitalism, and the trigger for the Sweezy-Dobb debate (1950s) on whether the transition was driven by internal class relations (Dobb) or by the expansion of long-distance trade (Sweezy). That debate set the stage for the later Brenner Debate.
  • Rodney Hilton, ed., The Transition from Feudalism to Capitalism (1976) — the volume that collected the Sweezy-Dobb debate with later contributions; the indispensable single source for the 20th-century Marxist transition literature.

Political Marxism and the Brenner Debate

  • Robert Brenner, "Agrarian Class Structure and Economic Development in Pre-Industrial Europe" (Past & Present, 1976) — the article that opened the Brenner Debate. Against Malthusian-demographic and commercialization accounts, Brenner argued that the divergent fates of England, France, and Eastern Europe in the early modern period turned on the specific class relations between lords, peasants, and the state in each region. England's distinctive outcome — peasants dispossessed, landlords leasing competitively to capitalist tenant farmers — produced agrarian capitalism. The article is collected, with replies by Postan, Le Roy Ladurie, and others, in The Brenner Debate, ed. T.H. Aston and C.H.E. Philpin (1985).
  • Ellen Meiksins Wood, The Origin of Capitalism: A Longer View (1999; revised 2002) — the clearest book-length statement of the Political Marxist position. Wood attacks the "commercialization model" — capitalism as natural extension of trade — and argues that capitalism's distinctive feature is the market dependence of producers, first achieved in the English countryside between the 16th and 17th centuries through the transformation of landlord-tenant relations. From that agrarian base, capitalism's logic of competition, productivity, and dispossession spread outward and globally. Read in tandem with Brenner's 1976 article.
  • Ellen Meiksins Wood, The Pristine Culture of Capitalism (1991) — the earlier statement, focused on how England's distinctive trajectory shaped its political culture.
  • Charles Post, The American Road to Capitalism (2011) — the major Political Marxist account of the U.S. case, arguing that the antebellum North was not yet capitalist in Wood's sense and that the Civil War cleared the way for capitalist relations across the country.

World-systems and the longue durée

  • Immanuel Wallerstein, The Modern World-System (4 vols., 1974–2011) — the major rival framework to Political Marxism. Wallerstein dates capitalism to the long 16th century, but locates it not in the English countryside but in the emergence of a Europe-centered world-economy linking core, semi-periphery, and periphery through unequal exchange. Wood and the Political Marxists rejected this account for what they saw as its commercialization premise; the disagreement is one of the major fault lines in Marxist historiography.
  • Fernand Braudel, Civilization and Capitalism, 15th–18th Century (3 vols., 1979) — the Annales school's monumental account, distinguishing material life, market exchange, and capitalism proper (the high-level, anti-competitive sphere of large merchants and financiers). Braudel's distinction between markets and capitalism — markets are old and ubiquitous, capitalism is the privileged top story built on top of them — is influential in non-Marxist economic history.
  • Giovanni Arrighi, The Long Twentieth Century (1994; revised 2010) — uses Braudel's framework to read the history of capitalism as a sequence of hegemonic financial cycles (Genoese, Dutch, British, American).

Working-class formation as the other side of the question

  • E.P. Thompson, The Making of the English Working Class (1963) — if the historicist account is right that capitalism required producing a class of wage laborers, this is the book on how that class made itself out of the materials it was given. The corresponding social history to the political-economic argument about primitive accumulation. Read also Thompson's essay "The Moral Economy of the English Crowd in the Eighteenth Century" (Past & Present, 1971), which describes the pre-capitalist normative order that market society dismantled.

Slavery, race, and the global frame

  • See The New History of Capitalism for the historiography arguing that American industrial capitalism was financed and organized through Atlantic slavery — the argument running from W.E.B. Du Bois and Eric Williams through Sven Beckert, Edward Baptist, Walter Johnson, and Caitlin Rosenthal.
  • See Racial Capitalism for the conceptual claim that capitalism was racial from its origins.
  • Eric Williams, Capitalism and Slavery (1944) — written the same year as Polanyi's Great Transformation. The Trinidadian historian's argument that British industrial capitalism was financed by the profits of Caribbean slavery — long marginalized in mainstream historiography, now widely read again.

Contemporary extensions

  • Clara E. Mattei, The Capital Order: How Economists Invented Austerity and Paved the Way to Fascism (2022) — the recent extension of the Polanyi tradition. Where Polanyi described how the self-regulating market was politically constructed in the 19th century, Mattei describes how it has been politically preserved through 20th- and 21st-century austerity policy. Austerity, on her account, is the post-WWI political program that disciplines labor and restores capitalist class relations when those relations are under pressure.
  • David Harvey, A Brief History of Neoliberalism (2005) and The New Imperialism (2003) — Harvey reads the late 20th century as the political reconstruction of capitalism after the 1970s crisis. His concept of "accumulation by dispossession" updates Marx's primitive accumulation: enclosure, privatization, and financial extraction are not just origin stories but ongoing features of capitalist accumulation.
  • Thorstein Veblen, The Theory of the Leisure Class (1899) and The Theory of Business Enterprise (1904) — the institutionalist parallel tradition: capitalism understood as a specific cultural and institutional formation, not a natural order, with industrial and pecuniary logics in tension.
  • Geoffrey Hodgson, How Economics Forgot History (2001) — a more recent institutionalist statement on why mainstream economics has lost the ability to ask the historicist question at all.

Reference works and overviews

  • Jürgen Kocka, Capitalism: A Short History (2014; English 2016) — the most useful short academic overview. German economic historian, Polanyi- and Braudel-influenced; treats capitalism as historically specific without committing to a single Marxist mechanism.
  • Larry Neal and Jeffrey G. Williamson, eds., The Cambridge History of Capitalism (2 vols., 2014) — the major mainstream economic-historical reference, useful as a counterweight to the Marxist literature; treats capitalism with a longer chronology and a less politicized vocabulary.
