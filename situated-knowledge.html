Situated Knowledge and the Embodied Knower

20th–21st century
#philosophy#epistemology#phenomenology#feminism#philosophy-of-mind#ai#existentialism

The argument that knowledge is always produced from somewhere — from a body, a position, a history — runs through several 20th-century traditions that are usually treated separately. Phenomenology develops it from the side of perception and being-in-the-world. Feminist standpoint theory develops it from the side of marginal social position and the politics of objectivity. Critiques of computational accounts of mind develop it from the side of what is missing when symbols are processed without embodiment. The common target is what Donna Haraway, in the 1988 essay that named the position, called the "god trick": the pretense of a view from nowhere that presents itself as the view from everywhere.

The deeper axis under these arguments is the contrast between essentialism and existentialism. Essentialism holds that things — including minds and persons — have a defining nature prior to and independent of their existence; existence is the instantiation of essence in matter. Existentialism inverts the order: existence comes first, and what something becomes is constituted in and inseparable from its thrown situation in a world. Sartre's slogan "existence precedes essence" (L'existentialisme est un humanisme, 1946) is one statement of the move, but the move is older and broader — Kierkegaard's existing individual against Hegelian system, Heidegger's Geworfenheit (thrownness), Merleau-Ponty's embodied perception. Once mind and knowing are conceived this way, the abstract knowing subject — Cartesian cogito, Kantian transcendental ego, the unmarked observer of classical scientific objectivity — becomes a derivative abstraction from a more primary situated existence rather than its ground.

Two hinge figures connect the philosophical commitment to the more specific arguments. Simone de Beauvoir's Le Deuxième Sexe (1949) applies Sartre's existence-precedes-essence to gender — "on ne naît pas femme: on le devient" — and supplies the template that feminist standpoint theory and Haraway's situated knowledge inherit. Hubert Dreyfus's What Computers Can't Do (1972), drawing on Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty, applies the embodiment argument to artificial intelligence: knowing requires skilled, embodied coping with a world that symbolic processing alone cannot reproduce. The two hinges connect a single philosophical commitment to two different domains — the politics of knowledge among human knowers, and the question of whether machine systems can know at all.

Annotated bibliography

Phenomenological roots

  • Edmund Husserl, Cartesian Meditations (1931) and The Crisis of European Sciences and Transcendental Phenomenology (1936) — Husserl's late work locates the genesis of scientific objectivity in the "lifeworld" (Lebenswelt), the pre-theoretical horizon of embodied experience that natural science abstracts from and then forgets it has abstracted from. The argument is not anti-scientific but anti-naïve: science cannot ground itself without acknowledging the lifeworld it presupposes.
  • Martin Heidegger, Being and Time (Sein und Zeit, 1927) — Dasein is always already thrown (Geworfenheit) into a world, a language, a history, a body, a set of practical concerns. There is no view from outside this thrownness; understanding is structured by the "fore-having," "fore-sight," and "fore-conception" the knower brings to anything they encounter. The disembodied observer is a derivative abstraction from this primary situated being-in-the-world.
  • Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Phenomenology of Perception (Phénoménologie de la perception, 1945) — a sustained working-out of the body as the primary site of knowing. Perception is not the registering of objects by a detached subject but the body's skilled engagement with a perceptual field. Merleau-Ponty's account of the "body schema" and motor intentionality becomes a central reference point for later embodied-cognition arguments, including Dreyfus's critique of AI.

The existentialist hinge: Beauvoir

  • Simone de Beauvoir, The Second Sex (Le Deuxième Sexe, 1949) — the load-bearing bridge between existentialism and feminist epistemology. Beauvoir applies the Sartrean frame to the situation of women: "one is not born, but rather becomes, a woman." Gender is not an essence given by biology but a position constituted through situation, the projects of others, and the historical accumulation of practices. The book gives feminist epistemology a directly existentialist starting point and is the genealogical ancestor of standpoint theory.
  • Jean-Paul Sartre, Existentialism Is a Humanism (1946) and Being and Nothingness (1943) — the formulation Beauvoir is working from. Sartrean existentialism is more individualistic and voluntarist than what comes after; standpoint theory's collective and political reframing of situated existence is partly a friendly revision of him.
  • Sonia Kruks, Retrieving Experience: Subjectivity and Recognition in Feminist Politics (Cornell, 2001) — the scholarly bridge. Kruks traces the lineage from Beauvoir and Merleau-Ponty through Iris Marion Young to contemporary feminist standpoint theorists, arguing that the phenomenological-existentialist inheritance is what gives feminist epistemology a non-essentialist account of social position.

Feminist standpoint theory and situated knowledge

  • Georg Lukács, History and Class Consciousness (1923) — the Marxist precursor. Lukács argues that the proletarian standpoint affords a particular epistemic access to the totality of capitalist social relations that the bourgeois standpoint, by its position, cannot achieve. The structure — that a determinate social position generates distinctive knowledge — is the template feminist standpoint theory inherits and revises.
  • Nancy Hartsock, "The Feminist Standpoint: Developing the Ground for a Specifically Feminist Historical Materialism" (in Sandra Harding and Merrill B. Hintikka, eds., Discovering Reality, 1983) — the founding statement of feminist standpoint theory. Hartsock adapts the Lukácsian frame to feminist materialism: the sexual division of labor positions women in a way that affords distinctive epistemic access to the workings of patriarchy and capitalism. Standpoint here is not given by being-a-woman; it is achieved through political and theoretical work on the experience that position makes available.
  • Dorothy Smith, The Everyday World as Problematic: A Feminist Sociology (Northeastern, 1987) — Smith develops a sociology that starts from women's situated everyday experience as the standpoint from which the "ruling relations" of bureaucratic, textual, professional power become visible. The methodological program — "institutional ethnography" — is a sociological application of standpoint thinking.
  • Sandra Harding, The Science Question in Feminism (Cornell, 1986) and Whose Science? Whose Knowledge? Thinking from Women's Lives (Cornell, 1991) — a systematic philosophical defense. Harding's "strong objectivity" argues that knowledge produced from marginalized standpoints is more rigorous, not less, because it is more accountable to its own conditions of production. The "view from nowhere" is a weaker form of objectivity because it cannot examine itself.
  • Patricia Hill Collins, Black Feminist Thought: Knowledge, Consciousness, and the Politics of Empowerment (Unwin Hyman, 1990) — Collins develops Black feminist standpoint as a distinctive epistemic tradition rooted in Black women's experience of intersecting oppressions. The book is explicit in refusing the essentialism charge: standpoint is constructed through collective intellectual work and political struggle, not deduced from social location.
  • Donna Haraway, "Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective," Feminist Studies 14:3 (Autumn 1988), 575–599 — the essay that names the position. Haraway argues on two fronts at once: against the "god trick" of disembodied scientific objectivity, and against full social-constructionist relativism, which she calls its "perfect mirror twin." Knowledge is always partial, located, embodied; "embodied objectivity" replaces transcendent objectivity. Haraway's position is more cyborg-friendly than standpoint theory's — see her earlier "A Cyborg Manifesto" (Socialist Review, 1985), which works to dissolve the human/animal/machine boundary rather than reinforce it, complicating the use of Haraway as an authority for claims that AI cannot know.

The essentialism charge from within

  • Elizabeth Spelman, Inessential Woman: Problems of Exclusion in Feminist Thought (Beacon, 1988) — an internal critique of universalizing feminism. Spelman argues that the category "woman," used as a unified ground for feminist theory, covertly smuggles in a white, middle-class, heterosexual essence and erases the women who do not fit it. The book forces standpoint theory to specify which standpoint and at what level of generality.
  • bell hooks, Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center (South End, 1984) — earlier and adjacent. hooks attacks the "common oppression" rhetoric of second-wave feminism for masking the real divisions of race and class among women, and argues for theory written from the margin rather than for it.
  • Kimberlé Crenshaw, "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex: A Black Feminist Critique of Antidiscrimination Doctrine, Feminist Theory and Antiracist Politics," University of Chicago Legal Forum (1989) — the founding statement of intersectionality. Crenshaw shows that single-axis analysis (race or sex) systematically erases the experience of those positioned at intersections, and develops a framework that becomes the standard response to the essentialism charge against standpoint theory.

Essentialist vs existentialist accounts of mind, and the AI question

  • Hilary Putnam, "The Nature of Mental States" (originally "Psychological Predicates," 1967) — early functionalism. Mental states are defined by their functional role, not by their physical substrate; in principle the same mental states can be instantiated in any system that realizes the right functional organization. This is the essentialist position about mind — there is a substrate-independent essence, the functional role, that anything can instantiate — and it underwrites the "strong AI" claim that suitably organized computation could constitute mind. Putnam himself later abandoned the position.
  • Jerry Fodor, The Language of Thought (Crowell, 1975) and The Modularity of Mind (MIT, 1983) — a thoroughgoing development of computational-representational theory of mind. Cognition is symbol manipulation over mental representations with a syntactic structure. The position is the explicit target of both Searle and Dreyfus.
  • John Searle, "Minds, Brains, and Programs," Behavioral and Brain Sciences 3:3 (1980), 417–457 — the Chinese Room argument. A person manipulating Chinese symbols by rule, with no understanding of Chinese, instantiates the same program a computer would and understands nothing; therefore symbol manipulation is not by itself sufficient for understanding. Searle's argument is about syntax versus semantics rather than about embodiment, but it belongs to the broader rejection of computational essentialism about mind.
  • Hubert Dreyfus, What Computers Can't Do: A Critique of Artificial Reason (Harper & Row, 1972), What Computers Still Can't Do (MIT, 1992), and On the Internet (Routledge, 2001) — the Heideggerian-Merleau-Pontian critique of AI. Intelligent action depends on a background of skilled, embodied coping with a world — the fore-structures Heidegger described, the body schema Merleau-Ponty described — that cannot be made fully explicit as rules and therefore cannot be replicated by symbolic computation alone. Dreyfus carries the existentialist-phenomenological argument directly onto the AI question.
  • Thomas Nagel, "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" Philosophical Review 83:4 (1974), 435–450 — a canonical statement of the irreducibility of subjective experience to third-person description. There is something it is like to be a conscious organism, and that "something" is missed by any description that does not occupy the organism's point of view. The argument frames the territory David Chalmers later names the "hard problem."
  • David Chalmers, The Conscious Mind: In Search of a Fundamental Theory (Oxford, 1996) — Chalmers distinguishes the "easy problems" of explaining cognitive functions from the "hard problem" of explaining why those functions are accompanied by subjective experience at all. The hard problem is the contemporary form of the question phenomenology raised against any purely functionalist or behaviorist account of mind.
  • Emily M. Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, Margaret Mitchell, "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?" FAccT '21 (2021); and Emily M. Bender and Alexander Koller, "Climbing towards NLU: On Meaning, Form, and Understanding in the Age of Data," ACL 2020 — the recent extension to large language models specifically. The argument is that systems trained only on textual form, without grounding in communicative intent or engagement with a world, produce coherent-looking language without the conditions that make language meaningful. The line of attack is closer to Dreyfus and Searle than to Haraway, but it belongs to the same family.

Extensions: knowing-from-somewhere in place and in consciousness

  • Wendell Berry, The Unsettling of America: Culture and Agriculture (Sierra Club, 1977) and the essays in The Art of the Commonplace (Counterpoint, 2002) — the agrarian application of situated knowing. Berry argues that good agricultural practice depends on long, particular, embodied knowledge of a specific place, and that industrial agriculture, organized around universal abstractions of yield and input, systematically destroys it. "The standard is the nearest thing to hand."
  • Vandana Shiva, Monocultures of the Mind: Perspectives on Biodiversity and Biotechnology (Zed Books, 1993) — the parallel argument from the global South. Shiva argues that industrial agriculture's universalizing science erases place-based, often women-held agricultural knowledge, and that the erasure is constitutive of the science rather than incidental to it. The argument connects situated-knowledge themes directly to development politics.
  • James C. Scott, Seeing Like a State: How Certain Schemes to Improve the Human Condition Have Failed (Yale, 1998) — the political-historical formulation. Scott contrasts mētis (local, practical, particular knowledge held by practitioners) with technē (abstract, generalizable, codifiable knowledge legible to bureaucracies and states). High-modernist schemes from Soviet collectivization to scientific forestry to ujamaa villagization failed in part because they treated mētis as ignorance to be replaced rather than as knowledge of conditions the planners could not see. Probably the readiest version of the situated-knowledge argument outside feminist epistemology.
  • Michael Pollan, The Omnivore's Dilemma (Penguin, 2006) and A World Appears: A Journey Into Consciousness (Penguin, 2026) — Pollan's method has long been first-person and immersive, in the literary-journalism tradition of John McPhee and Joan Didion; the methodological self-location is a genre convention rather than a feminist-epistemological commitment. The 2026 book moves more directly onto the territory of this topic: drawing on William James, Thomas Nagel, David Chalmers, Christof Koch, Michael Levin, and Russell Hurlburt, Pollan argues that first-person access to consciousness — including through meditation and psychedelics — yields what third-person science cannot, and is skeptical of claims that current AI systems are conscious. The argument lands on the existentialist-phenomenological side of the axis this topic describes, though Pollan does not name it that way.
  • William James, The Principles of Psychology (Henry Holt, 1890), chapter 9, "The Stream of Thought" — the classical American formulation of inner experience as continuous, selective, and embodied rather than as a sequence of discrete symbols. The chapter is a touchstone for Pollan's A World Appears and for phenomenology's treatment of inner experience more broadly.
