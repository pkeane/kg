Daniel Chandler

21st century · British
#economics#philosophy#political-theory#liberalism#distributive-justice#taxation

British economist and philosopher. He holds degrees in economics, philosophy, and history from Cambridge and the LSE; held a Henry Fellowship at Harvard, where he studied under Amartya Sen; worked as a policy adviser in the UK government; and is now based at the London School of Economics, where he is Research Director of the Programme on Cohesive Capitalism at STICERD and at the Centre for Economic Performance.

Free and Equal: A Manifesto for a Just Society (Allen Lane / Knopf, 2023, published in the UK as Free and Equal: What Would a Fair Society Look Like?) is his statement of a contemporary progressive liberalism grounded in John Rawls's A Theory of Justice (1971). The first half of the book is a guided exposition of Rawls — the original position, the veil of ignorance, the two principles of justice, the difference principle, the criticism Rawls drew from libertarian, communitarian, and Marxist directions, and the late-career idea of "property-owning democracy." The second half takes the apparatus down off the shelf and asks what its consistent application would actually require in policy terms — Chandler's contention being that Rawls's framework, taken seriously, generates a much more radical program than its conventional reception has allowed.

The proposals include: a universal basic income at roughly 60 percent of median income; a "citizen's wealth fund" — a sovereign-wealth-fund instrument financed by progressive taxation and holding equity in publicly traded companies without controlling stakes, with returns distributed to citizens; a guaranteed inheritance for all young adults at the age of majority (the proposal earlier defended by Tony Atkinson and Thomas Piketty); sectoral collective bargaining and worker codetermination on company boards on the German model; "democracy vouchers" giving each citizen an annual fixed sum to donate to a political party or candidate, so that political influence does not track wealth; and substantial expansions of public health and education provision. Chandler argues these are not a wish list but the practical entailments of treating people as "free and equal" in Rawls's sense.

The book has been read as the most accessible contemporary case for justice-as-fairness as a live political program rather than an academic abstraction. It was endorsed by Piketty as "a must-read" and reviewed in the Literary Review by Alan Ryan, whose On Politics (2012) is the loose model for this graph. The book cuts directly across the territory of How to Tax the Wealthy — its tax-and-inheritance proposals overlap with the comprehensive programs of Atkinson and Piketty, and its "democracy vouchers" address the political-voice problem documented in the work of Gilens and Page and Bartels.

Key ideas

  • Rawls's framework taken seriously generates a more radical policy program than its academic reception has allowed
  • Universal basic income and a citizen's wealth fund as instruments of a property-owning democracy
  • Guaranteed inheritance for all young adults
  • Workplace democracy: sectoral bargaining and equal worker representation on company boards
  • "Democracy vouchers" to decouple political influence from personal wealth
