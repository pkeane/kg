Post-neoliberal Political Economy

1990s–present
#political-economy#capitalism#climate#social-democracy#socialism#inequality#democracy

The defining question of contemporary political economy is what kind of system can succeed the post-1980 settlement. The climate crisis has put the question in its sharpest form — if individual consumer choice cannot bend an emissions curve set by infrastructure, zoning, freight logistics, military procurement, and industrial subsidy, then the operative variable is not personal virtue but the shape of the political-economic system itself. The same point reappears in housing, healthcare, childcare, transit, and the long stagnation of working- and middle-class incomes: outcomes that look like millions of private choices are in fact the downstream effects of decades of public-policy decisions, most of them made in the name of letting markets allocate.

The "personal carbon footprint" frame, originated as a 2004 BP marketing campaign, is the limit case of how the neoliberal era taught publics to read systemic problems as personal failings. Once that diagnosis is accepted, the constructive question follows: what kind of political economy has the capacity — institutional, financial, democratic — to direct large-scale investment toward the common good, decommodify the essentials of life, and constrain private wealth from capturing public decision-making? This topic gathers the contemporary answers.

Several traditions converge here without quite merging. Rawls's late writings rejected welfare-state capitalism on his own principles and pointed instead to property-owning democracy or liberal socialism as the only regimes compatible with justice as fairness. Thomas Piketty's Capital and Ideology extends the diagnosis into a concrete program of progressive wealth taxation, worker co-determination, and universal inheritance. The Green New Deal literature — Robert Pollin's costed plans, the political case made by Kate Aronoff and her co-authors, Naomi Klein's popular argument — translates the same impulse into climate-specific industrial policy. Mariana Mazzucato's mission economy reframes the state as a risk-taking orchestrator rather than a market-fixer (see State Capacity and Technology). Degrowth thinkers such as Jason Hickel argue that rich-world economies must be planned to contract their high-throughput sectors. Erik Olin Wright's Envisioning Real Utopias maps how these strategies — taming, dismantling, eroding capitalism — relate to one another.

What unites these figures is the conviction that the post-1980 settlement, in which markets allocate and individuals improvise, has reached the end of its capacity to deliver the public goods political life requires: shared infrastructure, ecological survival, broad-based prosperity, democratic legitimacy. What divides them is how far to push beyond capitalism, how much to retain of markets, and how the politics of transition can actually be assembled.

Annotated bibliography

Property-owning democracy and Rawlsian institutional design

  • John Rawls, Justice as Fairness: A Restatement (2001) — the clearest statement of Rawls's mature view that welfare-state capitalism does not satisfy his principles of justice; only property-owning democracy or liberal socialism do. The relevant passages are §41–42. Often surprising to readers who know only A Theory of Justice (1971), where the institutional implications were left implicit. The full case is unpacked in Justice as Fairness.
  • Martin O'Neill and Thad Williamson (eds.), Property-Owning Democracy: Rawls and Beyond (2012) — the contemporary revival. Essays clarifying what POD requires (wide ex ante dispersal of productive capital and human capital, not merely ex post transfers), why it differs from welfare-state capitalism, and what concrete policies — universal capital grants, predistribution, asset-based welfare — might realize it.
  • G. A. Cohen, Rescuing Justice and Equality (2008) — the sharpest left critique of Rawls; argues that the difference principle, applied seriously, demands more than Rawls himself acknowledged. Important for understanding why POD-versus-market-socialism debates matter inside Rawlsian liberalism, and why "philosophical liberalism" and "democratic socialism" overlap more than their adherents tend to admit.

Participatory socialism and the politics of wealth

  • Thomas Piketty, Capital and Ideology (2020) — after Capital in the Twenty-First Century (2014) had established the long-run drift of wealth concentration, this volume lays out a specific institutional program: progressive wealth and inheritance taxation, universal capital endowment for young adults (~€120,000 at 25), worker co-determination on company boards scaled up from the German Mitbestimmung model, confiscatory top tax rates, and a guaranteed floor of public spending on education. The most institutionally concrete program in the social-democratic-plus register currently in print.

The Green New Deal and eco-socialism

  • Robert Pollin, various papers from the UMass Political Economy Research Institute, including Greening the Global Economy (2015) and the climate-stabilization studies for U.S. states, the EU, and developing economies — the most rigorous costed plans for a Green New Deal: how much investment, in which sectors, with what employment effects, financed how. Less ideologically inflected than the political GND literature; more useful as a feasibility argument.
  • Kate Aronoff, Alyssa Battistoni, Daniel Aldana Cohen, and Thea Riofrancos, A Planet to Win: Why We Need a Green New Deal (2019) — the political case: public power utilities, public housing retrofit, mass transit as public works, just transition for fossil-fuel workers, internationalism over green nationalism. Closer to a manifesto than a policy paper, but unusually thoughtful about coalition and political feasibility.
  • Naomi Klein, This Changes Everything: Capitalism vs. the Climate (2014) and On Fire (2019) — the popular case that the climate crisis is a crisis of the political-economic system, not of consumer behavior. On Fire extends the argument into the Green New Deal moment.
  • Andreas Malm, Fossil Capital (2016) — the historical argument that the choice of fossil energy was political, not economic: steam beat water power because it concentrated control over labor in cities, not because it was cheaper. Establishes that the carbon economy was always a particular form of capitalist class power, which any post-fossil program has to confront.

State capacity and the mission economy

  • Mariana Mazzucato, The Entrepreneurial State (2013) and Mission Economy (2021) — reframes the state as a risk-taking innovator and mission orchestrator rather than a market-fixer. Empirically grounded in the long history of public investment in transformative technologies. Has influenced EU industrial policy and the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act. Less radical on ownership than Piketty or the eco-socialists; very specific on the institutional capacity required to deliver public goods at scale. See State Capacity and Technology for the wider literature on the state's delivery problem.

Social democracy and its limits

  • Gøsta Esping-Andersen, The Three Worlds of Welfare Capitalism (1990) — the canonical comparative analysis of welfare-state regimes (liberal, conservative-corporatist, social-democratic). Indispensable for understanding why the Nordic model works as well as it does, and why exporting it is harder than admirers assume.
  • Wolfgang Streeck, Buying Time: The Delayed Crisis of Democratic Capitalism (2014) and How Will Capitalism End? (2016) — argues that the post-war marriage of democracy and capitalism has structurally broken down: capital's exit options now overpower democratic decision-making, and the welfare state has been hollowed by debt, financialization, and offshoring. Not a positive program, but the sharpest available account of why the easy social-democratic answers no longer work. Pairs with The Welfare State Under Pressure.
  • Bhaskar Sunkara, The Socialist Manifesto: The Case for Radical Politics in an Era of Extreme Inequality (2019) — the most accessible popular statement of the contemporary U.S. democratic-socialist position. Useful as the political shorthand under which Piketty-style and Green New Deal programs travel in the Sanders / DSA register.

Degrowth and post-growth

  • Jason Hickel, Less Is More: How Degrowth Will Save the World (2020) — the most accessible case for degrowth as political economy rather than personal virtue: planned contraction of high-throughput sectors (advertising, fast fashion, SUVs, McMansions), work-time reduction, universal public services so private income matters less. Engages the "use less" intuition at the systemic level rather than the moral one.
  • Giorgos Kallis, Degrowth (2018) — the academic statement of the position, with attention to the lineage (Georgescu-Roegen, Illich, Gorz) and the philosophical critique of growthism as a political-economic ideology.

Mapping the alternatives

  • Erik Olin Wright, Envisioning Real Utopias (2010) and How to Be an Anticapitalist in the Twenty-First Century (2019) — Wright's gift was systematic mapping. The strategies he identifies — smashing, dismantling, taming, eroding, and escaping capitalism — let one see how property-owning democracy, social democracy, market socialism, eco-socialism, and degrowth relate to one another rather than as rival sects. The 2019 book is the more accessible entry point.
  • Elinor Ostrom, Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action (1990) — empirically grounded third option between pure market and pure state: polycentric governance of common-pool resources (fisheries, irrigation, forests, atmospheric commons). Important for thinking about energy and climate at sub-national and trans-national scales. Nobel in 2009 — the first economics Nobel awarded to a woman, and to a political scientist.

Diagnostic anchors

  • Mark Fisher, Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative? (2009) — the cultural diagnosis. Short, mordant, devastating on how late capitalism makes systemic alternatives literally unthinkable while loading responsibility onto individuals. The mood-music against which all the constructive programs above have to argue.
  • Naomi Klein, The Shock Doctrine: The Rise of Disaster Capitalism (2007) — the prehistory of the current moment: how neoliberal restructuring was actually imposed, country by country, over the last half-century. Necessary background for any constructive program that has to work with the institutions neoliberalism left behind.

Why these traditions and not others

Three traditions are deliberately given less weight here. Libertarian and classical-liberal responses to climate (carbon tax + dividend, Coasian bargaining, regulated markets in tradable permits) get serious attention in mainstream policy literature but rest on assumptions — that prices alone can drive transition, that property rights can be assigned to atmospheric commons, that political capture of regulators is solvable — that the figures above contest empirically. Authoritarian state-capacity arguments (the "China can build, why can't we?" school) describe real capacities but elide the question of whose interests state capacity is built to serve, which is the whole substance of the political-economic question. Anarchist and prefigurative traditions are vital correctives but, by their own commitments, do not propose the kind of macro-institutional program this topic is concerned with.

The synthesis the strongest figures point toward — Piketty's property and tax architecture, Pollin and Aronoff et al.'s climate program, Mazzucato's state-capacity theory, Rawls and O'Neill's philosophical legitimation — does not yet have a single name. Democratic socialism is the most common popular shorthand in the United States; participatory socialism (Piketty), liberal socialism (Rawls), Green New Deal, post-growth social democracy are the more precise terms in circulation. That the synthesis lacks a settled name is itself a sign of how recent the post-neoliberal moment is.
