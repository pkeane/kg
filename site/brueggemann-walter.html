Walter Brueggemann

1933 – 2025 · American
#theology#old-testament#biblical-studies#prophetic-tradition#protestant

American Old Testament scholar and theologian (Walter Albert Brueggemann, 11 March 1933, Tilden, Nebraska – 5 June 2025, Traverse City, Michigan). The son of a German Evangelical Synod minister, he was raised in small-town Missouri, took an A.B. from Elmhurst College (1955), a B.D. from Eden Theological Seminary (1958), a Th.D. from Union Theological Seminary in New York (1961, under James Muilenburg), and a Ph.D. from Saint Louis University (1974). He was professor of Old Testament at Eden Theological Seminary from 1961 to 1986 (and dean from 1968 to 1982), then William Marcellus McPheeters Professor of Old Testament at Columbia Theological Seminary in Decatur, Georgia from 1986 until his retirement. Ordained in the United Church of Christ, he wrote more than a hundred books and was, for the second half of the twentieth century and into the twenty-first, the most widely read interpreter of the Hebrew Bible in the English-speaking Protestant world.

Brueggemann's central work is The Prophetic Imagination (Fortress, 1978; revised 2001), a short book that has been continuously in print for almost five decades and is the standard text on the Hebrew prophets in mainline seminaries. The argument is that the Old Testament prophets — Amos, Hosea, Isaiah, Jeremiah — operated against what Brueggemann calls "the royal consciousness" of Solomon's monarchy: an established religious-political order organized around prosperity, satiety, and the suppression of grief, in which the LORD had been domesticated into a household god of the regime. The prophetic task, on his reading, was twofold: to criticize by making the suffering of those crushed by the royal consciousness visible and audible (the "language of grief"), and to energize by imagining a future not continuous with the present arrangements (the "language of amazement"). The book has been read, beyond its biblical-studies use, as a manual for any community trying to think beyond a system that claims to be the only possibility.

Theology of the Old Testament: Testimony, Dispute, Advocacy (Fortress, 1997) is his major systematic work, organizing the theology of the Hebrew Bible around the courtroom metaphor of testimony — Israel's "core testimony" about who YHWH is, and the "counter-testimony" within the same canon that contradicts it (the psalms of disorientation, Job, Lamentations, Qoheleth). The decision to make the Hebrew Bible's internal contradictions constitutive rather than embarrassing was characteristic of his approach.

Among his many other books, The Message of the Psalms (1984) is the standard pastoral introduction to the Psalter; An Introduction to the Old Testament: The Canon and Christian Imagination (2003) is the widely used seminary textbook; and Sabbath as Resistance: Saying No to the Culture of Now (2014) extended the prophetic critique of the royal consciousness to contemporary consumer capitalism and the unceasing productivity it demands.

Brueggemann's theology insisted on the Hebrew Bible as the dangerous, untamed source of Christian and Jewish moral imagination, against the temptation — perennial in both traditions — to reduce it to either a benign moral primer or a charter for present power.

Key ideas

  • "Royal consciousness" vs. "prophetic imagination" — the structuring opposition of the Hebrew Bible
  • The dual prophetic task: language of grief (criticism) and language of amazement (energizing)
  • Core testimony and counter-testimony as the internal structure of biblical theology
  • Sabbath as resistance to the regime of unceasing productivity
  • The Hebrew Bible as untamed source rather than domesticated charter
