James C. Scott

1936 – 2024 · American
#political-science#anthropology#anarchism#agrarian-studies#state-capacity

American political scientist and anthropologist, Sterling Professor of Political Science at Yale from 1976 until his retirement, founder of Yale's Agrarian Studies Program (1991), and one of the most-read political ethnographers of the late twentieth century. His work centered on the political life of peasants and other subordinated groups, the failures of high-modernist state planning, and the long human history of evasion of state authority.

The Moral Economy of the Peasant (Yale, 1976) draws on Burmese and Vietnamese rural society to argue that peasant rebellion is provoked not by absolute poverty but by violations of the moral norms of subsistence security that traditional patron-client relations had honored — a thesis developed in dialogue with E. P. Thompson's work on the English crowd. Weapons of the Weak (Yale, 1985), based on two years of fieldwork in a Malaysian village, documents the "everyday forms of peasant resistance" — foot-dragging, false compliance, pilfering, sabotage, gossip — that subordinated groups use in lieu of open revolt. Domination and the Arts of Resistance (Yale, 1990) extends the analysis with the distinction between the "public transcript" performed for power and the "hidden transcript" rehearsed among the subordinated.

Seeing Like a State: How Certain Schemes to Improve the Human Condition Have Failed (Yale, 1998) is his most widely read book. Scott argues that the modern state requires a legible population, landscape, and economy in order to tax, conscript, and plan, and that the project of legibility — cadastral surveys, scientific forestry, standardized weights and measures, planned cities, planned agriculture, planned villages — systematically destroys the local, practical, particular knowledge (mētis) on which the actual functioning of social systems depends. The case studies — Prussian scientific forestry, Le Corbusier's Brasília, Soviet collectivization, Tanzanian ujamaa villagization — share the structure of "authoritarian high modernism" defeated by its own legibility imperative. The book has become a standard reference well beyond political science, including in critiques of large software systems, development economics, and centralized planning generally.

The Art of Not Being Governed (Yale, 2009) extends the argument backward into the long history of upland Southeast Asia ("Zomia") as a refuge zone for peoples actively evading state incorporation. Against the Grain (Yale, 2017) reads the archaeology of the first agrarian states as a history not of progress but of capture — domestication of crops, animals, and humans alike.

Scott's distinction between mētis and technē is a serviceable version of the Situated Knowledge and the Embodied Knower argument outside feminist epistemology, and his work on agrarian life sits alongside that of Wendell Berry and Vandana Shiva. He died in July 2024.
