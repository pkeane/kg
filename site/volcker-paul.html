Paul Volcker

1927 – 2019 · American
#economics#monetary-policy#central-banking#american-thought

American economist and central banker. Chairman of the Federal Reserve from August 1979 to August 1987, appointed by Jimmy Carter and reappointed by Ronald Reagan. The disinflation engineered under his chairmanship — known as the "Volcker shock" — is the pivot most often identified in the historiography as the moment U.S. macroeconomic policy abandoned the postwar commitment to full employment and accepted high unemployment as the cost of price stability.

Born in Cape May, New Jersey, educated at Princeton, Harvard's Kennedy School, and the London School of Economics, Volcker spent his career between the Treasury Department, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, and Chase Manhattan Bank. As Treasury Undersecretary for Monetary Affairs under Nixon (1969–1974) he was a principal architect of the August 15, 1971 suspension of dollar–gold convertibility that ended the Bretton Woods fixed-exchange-rate order. He served as President of the New York Fed from 1975 until Carter elevated him to the chairmanship in August 1979, with U.S. inflation running near 12 percent.

On October 6, 1979 — the "Saturday Night Special" — the Federal Open Market Committee announced a change in operating procedure, shifting from targeting the federal funds rate to targeting nonborrowed reserves. The fed funds rate rose above 19 percent in 1981. The 1981–1982 recession that followed pushed unemployment to 10.8 percent, then the postwar high; construction firms mailed unsold two-by-fours to the Fed; farmers drove tractors around the building. Inflation fell from roughly 14 percent in 1980 to under 4 percent by 1983 and stayed there for thirty years. Critics on the left (Greider, Pollin, Stein) read the episode as the deliberate breaking of organized labor's bargaining position; sympathetic accounts (Silber) read it as a necessary and politically courageous restoration of central-bank credibility. The technical shift to monetarist operating procedures was abandoned in 1982; the political settlement it ratified — central-bank independence, inflation targeting, the subordination of full-employment policy to price stability — became the global norm.

Reagan reappointed him in 1983 but declined to reappoint him again in 1987; Alan Greenspan succeeded him. After leaving the Fed Volcker chaired Wolfensohn & Co., investigations into Holocaust-era dormant Swiss bank accounts and the UN Oil-for-Food program, and the Trustees of the International Accounting Standards Committee. In 2009–2011 he chaired Barack Obama's Economic Recovery Advisory Board. The "Volcker Rule" — Section 619 of the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act, restricting proprietary trading and certain hedge-fund and private-equity investments by commercial banks — is named for the position he argued from inside the administration, against initial resistance from Treasury Secretary Timothy Geithner and Lawrence Summers. His late memoir Keeping At It (2018) is openly worried about the institutional decay of the U.S. federal government and the erosion of what he calls "good government."

Key ideas

  • Central-bank independence and rules-bounded monetary policy
  • Disinflation through credible commitment, even at the cost of severe recession
  • Separation of commercial banking from proprietary trading (the Volcker Rule)
  • "Sound money and good government" as paired institutional commitments

Key works

  • Changing Fortunes: The World's Money and the Threat to American Leadership (1992, with Toyoo Gyohten)
  • Keeping At It: The Quest for Sound Money and Good Government (2018, with Christine Harper)

Secondary sources

  • William L. Silber, Volcker: The Triumph of Persistence (Bloomsbury, 2012) — the standard sympathetic biography, written with Volcker's cooperation.
  • William Greider, Secrets of the Temple: How the Federal Reserve Runs the Country (Simon & Schuster, 1987) — the canonical journalistic history of the Volcker Fed.
  • Judith Stein, Pivotal Decade: How the United States Traded Factories for Finance in the Seventies (Yale, 2010) — treats the Volcker appointment as the decisive moment in the Carter-era policy turn; see What Should Jimmy Carter Have Done Instead?.
  • Robert Pollin, Contours of Descent: U.S. Economic Fractures and the Landscape of Global Austerity (Verso, 2003) — post-Keynesian critique of the Volcker disinflation as the foundational event of the neoliberal U.S. macroeconomy.
  • Wolfgang Streeck, Buying Time: The Delayed Crisis of Democratic Capitalism (Verso, 2014) — places the Volcker shock as the pivot of his "three phases" of how rich democracies have postponed the conflict between democracy and capitalism.
