Tony Atkinson

1944 – 2017 · British
#economics#inequality#poverty#public-economics#taxation

British economist (Sir Anthony Barnes Atkinson, CBE, FBA; 4 September 1944, Caerleon, Wales – 1 January 2017, Oxford). The central figure of the modern economics of inequality and the teacher from whom the contemporary top-income-shares program of Thomas Piketty, Emmanuel Saez, and Gabriel Zucman descends.

Atkinson read mathematics at Cambridge before switching to economics. He was appointed professor at the University of Essex in 1971 at twenty-six, returned to Cambridge as Professor of Political Economy in 1992, and became Warden of Nuffield College, Oxford in 1994. He later held the Centennial Professorship at the London School of Economics. He was knighted in 2000 and served as president of the Royal Economic Society, the Econometric Society, the European Economic Association, and the International Economic Association, editing the Journal of Public Economics for twenty-five years.

His technical contributions opened the field. "On the Measurement of Inequality" (Journal of Economic Theory, 1970) introduced the family of inequality measures that bears his name, derived from an explicit social-welfare function with an "inequality aversion" parameter — the first such measure with a clean welfare-theoretic foundation rather than a purely statistical one. Lectures on Public Economics (1980, with Joseph Stiglitz; reissued by Princeton in 2015) became the standard graduate textbook in the field.

From the late 1990s Atkinson, with Piketty and others, reconstructed long-run series of top income shares from national tax records — first the United Kingdom (with Piketty), then France, the United States, and dozens more — work later gathered in the two-volume Top Incomes: A Global Perspective (Oxford, 2007 and 2010, ed. with Piketty) and synthesized in Atkinson, Piketty, and Saez, "Top Incomes in the Long Run of History" (Journal of Economic Literature, 2011). This is the empirical foundation on which Piketty's Capital in the Twenty-First Century (2014) and the World Inequality Database were built. Piketty has repeatedly attributed his own program to Atkinson's mentorship.

Inequality: What Can Be Done? (Harvard, 2015) is the late-career policy synthesis: fifteen specific proposals including a top income-tax rate of 65 percent, a more progressive inheritance tax, a guaranteed minimum inheritance for all adults at majority of around £5,000, a universal child benefit, a state employer of last resort, and a wealth-holding by sovereign-fund mechanism. The book is the closest analog in this graph's territory to a single comprehensive program, and it bears directly on How to Tax the Wealthy.

Atkinson also worked extensively on the measurement of poverty: the European Union's "Laeken indicators" of social inclusion, adopted in 2001, emerged from a process he chaired, and the World Bank's 2017 Atkinson Commission on Global Poverty report set the methodology the Bank still uses. He died of myeloma in Oxford on 1 January 2017.

Key ideas

  • The Atkinson index: inequality measured against an explicit social-welfare function
  • Long-run top income shares as a research program — built from tax records, across countries and a century
  • Inequality as a product of policy choice, not impersonal market forces
  • A comprehensive fifteen-point reform program: top rates, inheritance, minimum inheritance, employment, public ownership
