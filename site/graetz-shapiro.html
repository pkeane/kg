Michael Graetz & Ian Shapiro

20th–21st century · American
#taxation#political-science#law#public-policy

Michael Graetz (b. 1944; professor of law emeritus at Yale and at Columbia, and a former deputy assistant secretary of the Treasury for tax policy) and Ian Shapiro (b. 1956; Sterling Professor of Political Science at Yale, a native of South Africa) are, respectively, an American tax-law scholar and an American political theorist who have collaborated on the politics of taxation and economic security. Death by a Thousand Cuts: The Fight over Taxing Inherited Wealth (2005) is their study of the campaign that produced the 2001 repeal of the federal estate tax — a tax then paid by roughly the wealthiest two percent of estates — including its reframing as the "death tax," associated with the pollster Frank Luntz, and the decades of organized advocacy that preceded it. The Wolf at the Door: The Menace of Economic Insecurity and How to Fight It (2020) sets out their policy response.

Each works substantially apart from the collaboration. Graetz is a scholar of tax law and policy, author of The Decline (and Fall?) of the Income Tax (1997), the "Competitive Tax Plan" for a value-added tax, and The Power to Destroy: How the Antitax Movement Hijacked America (2024). Shapiro is a democratic theorist who argues that democracy's value lies chiefly in limiting domination (Politics Against Domination, 2016; The Real World of Democratic Theory, 2011). See How to Tax the Wealthy.
