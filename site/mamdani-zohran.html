Zohran Mamdani

1991 – ? · American (Ugandan-born)
#democratic-socialism#dsa#new-york#electoral-politics#american-left#muslim-american

Zohran Kwame Mamdani (b. 1991, Kampala, Uganda) is an American politician, member of the Democratic Socialists of America, and the mayor of New York City, elected in November 2025 and inaugurated January 2026. He had previously represented New York State Assembly District 36 (Astoria, Queens) from 2021 to 2025, having defeated a four-term incumbent in the 2020 Democratic primary.

He is the son of the filmmaker Mira Nair and the political scientist Mahmood Mamdani (Columbia University, scholar of African colonialism and the politics of identity). The family moved from Kampala to Cape Town to New York; Mamdani arrived in the city at age seven and became a U.S. citizen in 2018. He graduated from Bowdoin College in 2014 with a degree in Africana Studies and worked as a foreclosure-prevention counselor at the Chhaya Community Development Corporation in Queens before running for office. Earlier he had recorded hip-hop music under the name Mr. Cardamom.

In the State Assembly he was associated with a cluster of DSA-backed legislators (Julia Salazar, Jabari Brisport, Marcela Mitaynes, Phara Souffrant Forrest) who together formed the largest socialist bloc in a U.S. state legislature in decades. His legislative work concentrated on transit funding, taxi-medallion debt relief (he participated in a fifteen-day hunger strike with indebted drivers in December 2021), and the "Not on Our Dime" bill restricting New York charity funds to Israeli settlement organizations.

His 2025 mayoral campaign ran on a compressed material agenda — fare-free city buses, a rent freeze on stabilized apartments, a pilot of city-owned grocery stores in food-cost-distressed neighborhoods, and universal childcare — paired with tax proposals aimed at the city's highest earners and large corporations (proposals which require state cooperation to implement). He defeated former governor Andrew Cuomo and incumbent mayor Eric Adams in the Democratic primary under New York City's ranked-choice voting system, with a volunteer-heavy field operation organized through NYC-DSA and the Working Families Party. The campaign's media strategy relied on short-form video produced by the candidate himself, an asymmetry of native-media literacy that several analysts compared to the right's earlier dominance of the same format.

Mamdani's election is the most visible test to date of whether the post-Sanders American democratic-socialist current can hold executive power. The conditions for the win — concentrated urban electorate, mature local DSA infrastructure, fusion voting and RCV, an acute cost-of-living crisis, weakened establishment opponents — are not uniformly present at the state or national level, and the question of which elements scale and which are New York-specific is the central strategic argument on the U.S. left as of his inauguration. See Democratic Socialism and the Problem of Scale for the framework.

Key positions

  • Fare-free city buses
  • Rent freeze on stabilized apartments
  • Public option / city-owned grocery stores in underserved neighborhoods
  • Universal city-subsidized childcare
  • Taxation of the highest earners and large corporations (requires state action)
  • Restriction of charitable tax exemption for Israeli settlement organizations ("Not on Our Dime")
