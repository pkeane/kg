Hubert Dreyfus

1929 – 2017 · American
#phenomenology#philosophy-of-mind#ai#philosophy#existentialism

American philosopher who taught for forty years at UC Berkeley, known for a sustained Heideggerian and Merleau-Pontian critique of the symbolic-computational program in artificial intelligence and for an influential commentary tradition on continental phenomenology in the English-speaking world. Trained at Harvard, he was a consultant at RAND in the 1960s, where his exposure to early AI work prompted the technical report "Alchemy and Artificial Intelligence" (1965) and the book that grew from it, What Computers Can't Do (1972).

The book argues that early AI's symbolic-rule paradigm — exemplified by the work of Allen Newell and Herbert Simon, Marvin Minsky, and the General Problem Solver tradition — rests on an unexamined Cartesian-rationalist picture of cognition: that intelligence consists in the manipulation of explicit representations according to explicit rules. Drawing on Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty, Dreyfus argues that intelligent action depends on a background of skilled, embodied coping with a world — what Heidegger called the fore-structures of understanding, what Merleau-Ponty called the body schema and motor intentionality — that cannot be made fully explicit as rules and therefore cannot be replicated by symbolic computation alone. The argument was received as hostile within the AI community of the period but was substantially vindicated by the collapse of the symbolic-AI program in the 1980s and by the subsequent turn toward statistical, embodied, and situated approaches. A revised edition, What Computers Still Can't Do (MIT, 1992), assessed which of the original criticisms had held up.

His commentary work — particularly Being-in-the-World (MIT, 1991), a chapter-by-chapter reading of Division I of Being and Time — established the practical-engagement interpretation of Heidegger that became standard in the English-speaking world. With his brother Stuart Dreyfus, an operations researcher, he developed a five-stage model of skill acquisition (novice through expert) widely adopted in nursing education, military training, and professional development. On the Internet (Routledge, 2001) extended the embodiment critique to online learning and "telepresence," arguing that disembodied digital interaction loses what skilled apprenticeship in shared bodily presence makes possible.

His late book with Sean Dorrance Kelly, All Things Shining (2011), turns to Homer, Aeschylus, Dante, and Melville for resources to address what Charles Taylor and others have called the "secular age." See Situated Knowledge and the Embodied Knower for the broader topic this work belongs to.
