How to Tax the Wealthy

18th century–present
#political-economy#taxation#inequality#wealth#public-finance

In the literature gathered here the case for taxing concentrated wealth is treated as settled, not open: it rests on democratic equality, on the large role of luck and inheritance in great fortunes, and on the revenue that public goods require (see Inequality and Justice as Fairness). Thomas Piketty places rising wealth concentration among the central political problems of the era, and moral authorities have framed it in stronger terms still — Pope Francis, in Evangelii Gaudium (2013), calls inequality "the root of social ills" and says of an economy of exclusion that "such an economy kills." What remains genuinely contested, and what this page documents, is implementation: by what instrument. The disagreement among those who agree the wealthy should pay more is over the choice of tax base, the rate, the method of valuation, the treatment of unrealized gains, and the problem of collection and enforcement across borders.

The scale of the upward redistribution that motivates the literature is documented in Carter C. Price and Kathryn A. Edwards, Trends in Income From 1975 to 2018 (RAND, 2020). Measuring actual incomes against a counterfactual in which the more equal distribution of 1945–1974 had held, the authors estimate that roughly $47 trillion in income shifted from the bottom 90 percent toward the top — about $2.5 trillion in the year 2018 alone — as the top one percent's share of taxable income rose from 9 to 22 percent.

A recurring constraint organizes the mechanism literature: a tax is only as effective as its base and its administration, and the income of the very wealthy is largely unrealized, accruing as the rising market value of assets that are never sold and so never appear as taxable income. The strategy summarized as "buy, borrow, die" — hold appreciating assets, borrow against them to fund consumption, pass them to heirs at death — lets large fortunes grow across a lifetime while generating little realized income to tax. Much of the technical debate is about how to reach value the conventional income tax does not.

The proposals divide roughly into those that tax a flow (income, including the optimal-top-rate tradition and the taxation of capital gains and rents) and those that tax a stock (annual net worth, inheritance, or land value), with a third strand concerned not with the choice of tax but with measurement, valuation, and enforcement — the question of whether any of them can actually be collected. A fourth strand turns from instrument to politics, and argues that the chief obstacle is neither the choice of base nor administrative difficulty but the political power of the wealthy themselves. The annotated bibliography is organized by mechanism, closing with that political question.

Annotated bibliography

Is a great fortune earned? Wealth as inheritance, social capital, and extraction

Beneath the question of how to tax great wealth lies a prior one about its source. A line of argument holds that large fortunes are not earned in proportion to individual contribution but are mostly a return on an inherited common stock of knowledge and institutions, or are extracted from the labor and the public investment of others. On this view a high tax on great wealth reclaims a share that was never individually earned rather than confiscating just reward — which removes the strongest moral objection to taxing it heavily.

  • Herbert A. Simon, "UBI and the Flat Tax" (Boston Review, 2000). The Nobel economist argues that at least two-thirds of income in a wealthy society is attributable not to the recipient's effort or investment but to "social capital" — the accumulated knowledge, technology, and institutions, and the "happy accident" of being born into a productive society — which no living person created. He concludes that a flat income tax on the order of 70 percent, funding a universal grant, would do no more than recognize society's shared ownership of the resources that make high incomes possible.
  • Gar Alperovitz and Lew Daly, Unjust Deserts: How the Rich Are Taking Our Common Inheritance and Why We Should Take It Back (2008). The book-length development of Simon's claim. Drawing on the history of science and technology, the authors argue that the inherited stock of collective knowledge accounts for as much as 90 percent of present productivity, so that the largest part of any great fortune is a privatized return on a common inheritance, and taxing it is reclamation, not expropriation.
  • Ingrid Robeyns, Limitarianism: The Case Against Extreme Wealth (2024). The philosophical statement of the desert argument: Robeyns contends that great fortunes are produced by luck, inheritance, market structure, and the work of others far more than by individual merit, rebutting the claim that the rich deserve their wealth, and argues for an upper ceiling on permissible personal wealth enforced partly through taxation.
  • Mariana Mazzucato, The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy (2018). Reframes much elite income as value extraction — rent collected from existing value — rather than value creation, and documents how returns on publicly funded innovation (the internet, GPS, foundational pharmaceutical research) were captured privately. Recasts the question of who "earned" a fortune as a question of who created the value and who merely captured it. See also Stiglitz on rent in the section below.
  • Karl Marx, Capital, vol. 1 (1867). The foundational form of the extraction argument: surplus value as unpaid labor appropriated by the owner of capital — profit understood as value taken from the worker rather than created by the capitalist. The 19th-century ancestor of the contemporary "nobody earns a billion dollars" claim.

The classic answer: tax land and economic rent

  • Henry George, Progress and Poverty (1879). The case for a tax on the unimproved value of land — the "single tax." George's argument, built on Ricardo's theory of rent, is that location value is created by the surrounding community rather than the owner, and that because land is fixed in supply and cannot be moved or hidden, a tax on its value does not discourage production. This is the efficiency property — no deadweight loss — that has kept land taxation alive in economics long after the "single tax" movement faded.
  • Thomas Paine, Agrarian Justice (1797). An early specification of a stock tax with a defined use: a levy on inherited landed property, framed as recovering the "ground rent" owed to the commons, funding a lump-sum stakeholder grant at age 21 and a pension thereafter.
  • Joseph Stiglitz, The Price of Inequality (2012) and subsequent work. Stiglitz argues that much top-end income is economic rent — returns to monopoly, financial position, and land — rather than to productive contribution, and that taxing rents and land is therefore both more equitable and more efficient than taxing labor.

Progressive income taxation and the optimal top rate

  • James Mirrlees, "An Exploration in the Theory of Optimum Income Taxation" (Review of Economic Studies 38, 1971). The foundational formal treatment of how to set income tax rates given the tradeoff between redistribution and the disincentive to work. Mirrlees received the Nobel Prize in 1996; the framework underlies all later optimal-tax arguments.
  • Peter Diamond and Emmanuel Saez, "The Case for a Progressive Tax: From Basic Research to Policy Recommendations" (Journal of Economic Perspectives 25, 2011). Applying the Mirrlees framework to U.S. data, the authors derive an optimal top marginal tax rate of about 73 percent — a combined figure inclusive of federal, state, and local taxes and assuming deductions are eliminated — far above current rates and in the range of the 91–94 percent statutory top federal income-tax rates the United States maintained in the 1940s and 1950s.
  • Thomas Piketty, Emmanuel Saez, and Stefanie Stantcheva, "Optimal Taxation of Top Labor Incomes: A Tale of Three Elasticities" (American Economic Journal: Economic Policy 6, 2014). Decomposes the response of top earners to taxation into three channels — labor supply, tax avoidance, and compensation bargaining — and argues that where high pay reflects zero-sum bargaining rather than productivity, the revenue-maximizing top rate is considerably higher than the standard model implies. The empirical claim is that top-rate cuts since the 1980s are associated with larger top-one-percent income shares but not with faster growth.
  • Emmanuel Saez, Joel Slemrod, and Seth Giertz, "The Elasticity of Taxable Income with Respect to Marginal Tax Rates: A Critical Review" (Journal of Economic Literature 50, 2012). The survey of the single parameter on which optimal-top-rate arguments turn — how much reported income shrinks when rates rise — and a demonstration that the elasticity is not a fixed natural constant but is itself a function of the tax base and enforcement, and so can be reduced by closing avoidance channels.

Inheritance and estate taxation

  • Thomas Piketty and Emmanuel Saez, "A Theory of Optimal Inheritance Taxation" (Econometrica 81, 2013). Derives optimal bequest-tax formulas in terms of estimable "sufficient statistics" rather than assumed preferences; calibrated to French and U.S. data, the model implies a high optimal inheritance tax — on the order of a 50–60 percent rate on large bequests above a substantial exemption — for plausible weightings of the interests of those who inherit nothing.
  • Thomas Piketty, Capital and Ideology (2020). Progressive inheritance taxation as a central instrument, paired with a universal capital endowment for young adults — redistributing the stock of inherited wealth rather than only taxing income flows.
  • Lily Batchelder, "What Should Society Expect from Heirs? A Proposal for a Comprehensive Inheritance Tax" (Tax Law Review 63, 2009). Argues for taxing inheritance in the hands of the recipient, as income, rather than taxing the estate of the deceased — the design Rawls also favored, on the ground that an inheritance is an unearned windfall to the heir.
  • John Rawls, A Theory of Justice (1971) and Justice as Fairness: A Restatement (2001). Endorses inheritance and gift taxation levied on the recipient, and a proportional expenditure (consumption) tax, as instruments for limiting the intergenerational concentration of wealth and securing fair equality of opportunity. See Justice as Fairness.

The realization problem: capital gains and the step-up loophole

  • Saez and Zucman, The Triumph of Injustice (2019), and ProPublica, "The Secret IRS Files" (2021, Jesse Eisinger, Jeff Ernsthausen, and Paul Kiel). The journalism documented, from leaked returns, that the twenty-five wealthiest Americans paid a collective "true tax rate" of about 3.4 percent measured against the growth of their wealth from 2014 to 2018, because that wealth grew as unrealized gains. The reform proposals that follow target realization directly: taxing capital gains at the same rate as labor income; accrual or "mark-to-market" taxation of gains as they accumulate rather than at sale; and repeal of the step-up in basis at death, which resets an asset's cost basis to market value for heirs and so erases unrealized gains from capital-gains tax entirely. Closing step-up is the narrowest, most administrable lever aimed at "buy, borrow, die."
  • Senator Ron Wyden, Billionaires Income Tax (Senate Finance Committee draft, 2021). A worked legislative mechanism for accrual taxation: annual mark-to-market on publicly traded assets held by taxpayers with $100 million in income or $1 billion in net worth for three consecutive years, paired with a "deferral recapture" charge on illiquid assets (a stand-in interest charge applied at sale) to solve the valuation problem for non-traded holdings. Demonstrates how the realization barrier is handled in statutory detail.
  • Lily Batchelder and David Kamin, "Taxing the Rich: Issues and Options" (2019). A menu, prepared for the Aspen Economic Strategy Group, that sets the instruments side by side — higher top rates, capital-gains reform and mark-to-market, a wealth tax, stronger inheritance taxation, financial-transaction and corporate measures — with revenue estimates and an assessment of the administrative difficulty of each. The closest thing to a single comparative "how" document.

The wealth tax: design, evidence, and legal feasibility

  • Thomas Piketty, Capital in the Twenty-First Century (2014). The proposal for a progressive global tax on net wealth. Piketty treats the global scope not as utopian decoration but as the mechanism's load-bearing element: without international coordination and financial transparency, capital relocates to escape a national levy.
  • Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman, The Triumph of Injustice (2019) and "Progressive Wealth Taxation" (Brookings Papers on Economic Activity, 2019). The design case: an annual progressive tax on net worth (the basis of Elizabeth Warren's 2020 proposal — two percent above $50 million, with a billionaire surtax), supported by mark-to-market valuation, a national wealth registry, an "exit tax" on expatriating capital, and rebuilt enforcement. The Brookings paper is the technical version, estimating the U.S. base at roughly $9–13 trillion for a tax on the top 0.1 percent and arguing that earlier wealth taxes failed because of weak design — exemptions, low thresholds, poor enforcement — rather than because the instrument is unworkable.
  • OECD, The Role and Design of Net Wealth Taxes in the OECD (Tax Policy Studies No. 26, 2018). The cross-country record: of a dozen OECD countries that levied annual net-wealth taxes around 1990, most had repealed them by the 2010s, and the report attributes the failures to narrow bases, generous exemptions, valuation difficulty, and capital flight, drawing design lessons for any future levy.
  • Florian Scheuer and Joel Slemrod, "Taxing Our Wealth" (Journal of Economic Perspectives 35, 2021). A skeptical design survey. Notes that only three OECD countries still run wealth taxes and only Switzerland raises a comparable share of revenue to recent U.S. proposals, and emphasizes that the behavioral response — saving, portfolio shifts, avoidance, evasion — depends decisively on the breadth of the base and the strength of enforcement.
  • Legal feasibility in the United States. Whether a federal tax on net wealth is a "direct tax" requiring apportionment among the states under Article I has no settled answer. The Supreme Court's decision in Moore v. United States (2024) upheld the 2017 mandatory repatriation tax on a foreign corporation's undistributed earnings — narrowly, because the income had been realized by the corporation — but expressly declined to decide whether realization is constitutionally required, and four justices signaled support for such a requirement, which would also threaten federal mark-to-market proposals.

Tax administration and enforcement

  • Joel Slemrod, "Cheating Ourselves: The Economics of Tax Evasion" (Journal of Economic Perspectives 21, 2007). A statement of the "tax systems" view that the design of a tax cannot be separated from its administration: what a government can actually raise depends on the structure of reporting, withholding, and enforcement, not on statutory rates alone.
  • Henrik Kleven, Martin Knudsen, Claus Kreiner, Søren Pedersen, and Emmanuel Saez, "Unwilling or Unable to Cheat? Evidence From a Tax Audit Experiment in Denmark" (Econometrica 79, 2011). A randomized audit experiment on more than 40,000 filers showing that evasion is near zero for income subject to third-party reporting and substantial for self-reported income. The practical implication is that the feasibility of taxing any category of income or wealth turns on whether a third party (employer, bank, broker, registry) independently reports it.
  • Annette Alstadsæter, Niels Johannesen, and Gabriel Zucman, "Tax Evasion and Inequality" (American Economic Review 109, 2019). Matching leaked offshore account data (HSBC, the Panama Papers) to Scandinavian wealth records, the authors find that evasion rises steeply with wealth — the top 0.01 percent evade roughly a quarter of the taxes they owe — so that conventional measures understate both top wealth shares and the revenue at stake, and so that enforcement aimed at the top is itself a redistributive instrument. The corollary, central to the "tax gap" literature, is that better-funded and better-targeted enforcement is itself a tax instrument: it raises revenue from taxes already legally owed but uncollected, concentrated at the top.

International coordination: profit shifting and the corporate minimum

  • Gabriel Zucman, The Hidden Wealth of Nations (2015). Estimates that roughly eight percent of global household financial wealth sits in tax havens, largely unrecorded, and argues that a global financial registry and automatic cross-border information exchange are prerequisites for taxing the wealthy at all — the base must be made visible before it can be taxed.
  • Ludvig Wier and Gabriel Zucman, "Global Profit Shifting, 1975–2019" (NBER / UNU-WIDER, 2022). Documents that the share of multinational profits booked in tax havens rose from under 2 percent in the 1970s to roughly 37 percent — close to $1 trillion — by 2019, eroding the corporate tax base that funds public spending. The empirical case for coordinated minimum taxation.
  • OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework, Global Anti-Base Erosion (GloBE) Model Rules — Pillar Two (agreed 2021). The largest actually-enacted instrument in this literature: a 15 percent global minimum tax on multinationals with revenue above €750 million, agreed by some 140 jurisdictions in 2021, with the core Income Inclusion Rule taking effect in many countries from January 2024. It is aimed at corporate profit, not individuals, but it is the working precedent for the proposition that coordinated minimum taxation can overcome the capital-flight objection.
  • Gabriel Zucman, A Blueprint for a Coordinated Minimum Effective Taxation Standard for Ultra-High-Net-Worth Individuals (G20 report, 2024). Extends the Pillar Two logic from corporations to individuals: a coordinated minimum tax on the world's roughly three thousand billionaires, set at two percent of wealth, designed so that no single jurisdiction bears the flight risk alone.

Why it does not happen: unequal political voice and organized resistance

A separate body of work locates the binding constraint not in administrative feasibility but in politics: the instruments above are workable where the political will exists, and the recurring claim that taxing the wealthy is impractical is itself partly produced and financed by those it would fall upon, who hold disproportionate influence over what governments do.

A related strand asks why sustained popular pressure to tax the wealthy has been comparatively weak. Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson argue that the American right has fused an upwardly redistributive economic program with racial and cultural grievance — "plutocratic populism" — channeling economic discontent toward ethnonationalist and anti-democratic ends rather than toward taxation; Thomas Frank argues that the Democratic Party's reorientation around a credentialed professional class vacated the economic-populist ground it once held. On both accounts the absence of organized demand for taxing wealth is a political product, not evidence that the public is content with the distribution.

  • Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page, "Testing Theories of American Politics: Elites, Interest Groups, and Average Citizens" (Perspectives on Politics 12, 2014), with Gilens, Affluence and Influence (2012). Across roughly 1,800 policy questions, the authors find that economic elites and business-oriented groups have substantial independent influence on U.S. federal policy while the preferences of average citizens have little or none once elite preferences are accounted for. Gilens's book shows that where the affluent and the rest disagree, policy tracks the affluent.
  • Larry Bartels, Unequal Democracy: The Political Economy of the New Gilded Age (2008). Finds that U.S. senators' roll-call voting is far more responsive to the views of high-income constituents than to middle-income constituents, and shows no measurable response to the preferences of the bottom third of the income distribution.
  • Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, Winner-Take-All Politics (2010). Argues that the post-1970s rise in top incomes was produced by organized political action — the mobilization of business and the affluent, the decline of unions and other mass-membership organizations — rather than by impersonal market forces, with tax policy a central battleground.
  • Michael Graetz and Ian Shapiro, Death by a Thousand Cuts: The Fight over Taxing Inherited Wealth (2005). A study of the campaign behind the 2001 repeal of the estate tax — a tax then paid by roughly the wealthiest two percent — including the relabeling of it as the "death tax," a reframing associated with the pollster Frank Luntz, and the long-running organized effort that preceded the repeal. Graetz extends the account of organized anti-tax politics in The Power to Destroy: How the Antitax Movement Hijacked America (2024).
  • Isaac William Martin, Rich People's Movements: Grassroots Campaigns to Untax the One Percent (2013). A history of organized anti-tax mobilization on behalf of the wealthy since the 1913 federal income tax, identifying recurring waves that borrowed the tactics of populist, progressive, and later protest movements.
  • Jane Mayer, Dark Money: The Hidden History of the Billionaires Behind the Rise of the Radical Right (2016). Documents the network of donor-funded foundations, think tanks, academic centers, and advocacy groups built by Charles and David Koch and allied fortunes, part of whose program has been the reduction of taxes on capital and high incomes.
  • Jeffrey Winters, Oligarchy (2011). Describes an "income defense industry" of lawyers, accountants, and lobbyists retained by the wealthy to reduce their tax liabilities, and argues that American oligarchs, secure in their property, concentrate their political resources specifically on resisting taxation.
  • Brooke Harrington, Capital without Borders: Wealth Managers and the One Percent (2016). An ethnography of the wealth-management profession, based on the author's own training as a practitioner, documenting the trusts, offshore structures, and foundations through which large fortunes are placed beyond the reach of taxation.
  • Cristobal Young, The Myth of Millionaire Tax Flight: How Place Still Matters for the Rich (2017). Tests the most frequently invoked argument against higher state and national taxes on the rich — that they will simply leave — against thirteen years of U.S. tax returns, and finds millionaire migration rates lower than the general population's and very little tax-motivated relocation.
  • Anand Giridharadas, Winners Take All: The Elite Charade of Changing the World (2018). Argues that elite philanthropy and "win-win" social-impact initiatives function in part to redirect demands for structural redistribution — including progressive taxation — into voluntary action that leaves the underlying distribution of wealth and its sources untouched.
  • Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, Let Them Eat Tweets: How the Right Rules in an Age of Extreme Inequality (2020). Argues that since the early 1980s the American right has joined a plutocratic economic program — tax cuts, deregulation, the weakening of unions — to racial and cultural grievance, a combination they call "plutocratic populism" that builds mass support for upwardly redistributive policy and, to protect a minority economic agenda, turns against democratic constraints themselves.
  • Thomas Frank, Listen, Liberal: Or, What Ever Happened to the Party of the People? (2016). Argues that the Democratic Party reorganized around a credentialed professional class and largely abandoned the economic-populist agenda — including aggressive taxation of wealth — that had been its historic basis, leaving economic grievance without a major-party vehicle on the left.
