Affordable Housing and the Arts

19th–21st century
#urbanism#culture#political-economy#art#music#housing

A recurring claim in cultural and urban history holds that artistic and intellectual "scenes" — the Greenwich Village and SoHo of the 1960s–70s, the San Francisco of the same period, Weimar Berlin, postwar Paris — depend materially on cheap places to live and work. The argument is that low rent lowers the cost of failure: it lets people who are not yet (and may never be) commercially successful concentrate in one place, subsidize each other's experiments with proximity, and spend their time making things rather than making rent. On this view the cultural flowering is downstream of an economic precondition, and the precondition is usually accidental — a side effect of a city's decline, not of any policy intended to support art.

The strongest version of the claim — that cheap housing caused the 1960s flowering — is an interpretation, and it has both named defenders and named skeptics. What is not in dispute is the underlying economic fact. Rents in Lower Manhattan in the 1960s and early 1970s were low because of deindustrialization (factories and light manufacturing left the city, emptying loft buildings), white flight to the suburbs (eroding the population and tax base), and a housing glut that culminated in the 1975 fiscal crisis. A 1961 New York Times article reported SoHo-area loft rents of roughly $50–125 a month for large industrial spaces; landlords whose only alternative was vacancy accepted artist tenants, and in 1961 the Artist Tenants Association organized to legalize loft living. The cheap rent was real and structurally produced; the question is how much explanatory weight it can bear.

The debate matters beyond history because the same mechanism is now invoked in reverse: that high housing costs are hollowing out the cultural life of expensive cities, and that the "creative class" thesis turned the artist's pioneering of cheap neighborhoods into a deliberate, and self-defeating, instrument of gentrification. The pop critic Ellen Willis, writing on the counterculture, made a version of the cheap-rent argument in passing; the urbanist Jane Jacobs made it structurally; and a large subsequent literature has tested, qualified, and disputed it.

Annotated bibliography

The structural argument: cheap old space and new ideas

  • Jane Jacobs, The Death and Life of Great American Cities (1961) — the canonical statement of the mechanism. In the chapter "The need for aged buildings," Jacobs argues that "old ideas can sometimes use new buildings. New ideas must use old buildings." Her point is economic: high-overhead new construction can only be afforded by established, high-yield enterprises (chain stores, banks, well-subsidized institutions), while low-overhead old buildings are where marginal, experimental, and not-yet-profitable activities — including most cultural production — can survive. A district of uniformly new buildings, on this argument, structurally excludes the new.
  • Jane Jacobs, The Economy of Cities (1969) — extends the argument: economic and cultural novelty comes from the "import-replacement" work that happens in dense, mixed, low-cost urban environments, not from planned development.

The SoHo case study

  • Sharon Zukin, Loft Living: Culture and Capital in Urban Change (1982) — the foundational academic study. Zukin traces how artists' colonization of cheap deindustrialized loft space in SoHo created an "artistic mode of production" that was then captured by capital: the artists made the neighborhood desirable, real-estate interests and the city codified loft living, and the rising values that followed pushed the artists out. The book established the now-standard account of the artist-led gentrification cycle.
  • Aaron Shkuda, The Lofts of SoHo: Gentrification, Art, and Industry in New York, 1950–1980 (2016) — a detailed history of how SoHo went from manufacturing district to artist neighborhood to luxury zone, with attention to the specific policy decisions (zoning variances, the Artists in Residence program) that made artist occupancy legal and then made it profitable.
  • Robert Caro, The Power Broker: Robert Moses and the Fall of New York (1974) — context for why so much of the city was being cleared, depopulated, and devalued in the postwar decades; the supply of cheap space was partly a byproduct of urban-renewal destruction.

The fiscal and economic backdrop

  • Kim Phillips-Fein, Fear City: New York's Fiscal Crisis and the Rise of Austerity Politics (2017) — the history of how New York reached near-bankruptcy in 1975. Useful for understanding that the cheap-rent era was the same era as municipal collapse: the conditions that made the city affordable for artists were the conditions of its breakdown.
  • David Harvey, Rebel Cities (2012) — the Marxist-geography frame: cheap rent and the later "reinvestment" that displaces it are two moments of the same circuit of capital. Gentrification is not a betrayal of the artistic scene but its predictable sequel.

The countercultural memoir and criticism

  • Ellen Willis — the pop-music and political critic argued, in her writing on the 1960s counterculture, that the period's cultural ferment depended in part on the cheapness of urban life, which let young people cluster in cities and pursue non-remunerative work. Her essays are collected in Beginning to See the Light (1981), No More Nice Girls (1992), and The Essential Ellen Willis (2014, ed. Nona Willis Aronowitz).   • Patti Smith, Just Kids (2010) — memoir of her life with Robert Mapplethorpe in late-1960s and early-1970s New York; a first-person record of how cheap rent (and the Chelsea Hotel) made a life in art survivable for people with almost no income. Anecdotal evidence for the structural claim.

The contemporary policy debate and the skeptics

  • Richard Florida, The Rise of the Creative Class (2002) — turned the observation that artists cluster in affordable, tolerant, amenity-rich places into a municipal development strategy: attract the "creative class" and growth follows. Widely criticized for providing intellectual cover for the gentrification that displaces the very artists it celebrates; Florida himself later acknowledged some of this in The New Urban Crisis (2017).
  • David Ley, "Artists, Aestheticisation and the Field of Gentrification" (Urban Studies, 2003) — argues empirically that artists function as the "shock troops" of gentrification: they pioneer devalued neighborhoods, raise their cultural cachet, and are then priced out as the middle class follows. Complicates the romantic version by showing that the cheap-rent scene contains the seed of its own destruction.
  • Edward Glaeser, Triumph of the City (2011) — the dissenting economist's view: cities thrive on density and human capital, and the right response to high housing costs is to build more housing, not to romanticize the cheap-rent past. Skeptical that artistic scenes require subsidy; thin on the distributional question of who the new density is for.
  • Matthew Desmond, Evicted: Poverty and Profit in the American City (2016) — not about artists, but the closest contemporary parallel to Jacobs's street-level observation: a study of what happens to ordinary people when affordable urban housing disappears. The grim counterpart to the cheap-rent renaissance.
