Post-neoliberal Political Economy

1990s–present
#political-economy#capitalism#climate#social-democracy#socialism#inequality#democracy

Post-neoliberal political economy refers to a body of contemporary scholarship and policy proposals concerned with alternatives to the political-economic settlement that took shape in developed economies after roughly 1980. The literature treats inequality, stagnant median incomes, climate change, declining welfare-state capacity, and the limits of market allocation as connected problems that call for institutional rather than primarily individual responses.

A common starting point in this literature is the argument that systemic outcomes are often framed as the aggregate of individual consumer choices, and that this framing can obscure the role of political-economic structure. The "personal carbon footprint" concept, popularized by a 2004 advertising campaign for BP, is a frequently cited example. Carbon emissions, housing affordability, and access to public goods are described as products of infrastructure, regulation, zoning, taxation, and capital allocation, not of individual virtue.

The proposals discussed under this heading vary in their distance from market capitalism, in the role they assign to the state, and in the political coalitions they presuppose. They include property-owning democracy in the Rawlsian tradition; the participatory socialism of Thomas Piketty; Green New Deal industrial policy; the mission-oriented state-investment framework of Mariana Mazzucato; Nordic-style social democracy and analyses of its erosion; degrowth; and the comparative frameworks of Erik Olin Wright and Elinor Ostrom. Several of the figures listed below are also discussed in the related topics Justice as Fairness, The Welfare State Under Pressure, and State Capacity and Technology.

Annotated bibliography

Property-owning democracy

  • John Rawls, Justice as Fairness: A Restatement (2001). In §41–42, Rawls states that welfare-state capitalism does not satisfy his two principles of justice, and that only property-owning democracy or liberal socialism do. Property-owning democracy is defined as a regime in which productive capital and human capital are widely distributed ex ante, rather than redistributed ex post through transfers. The argument is implicit in A Theory of Justice (1971) and made explicit in the Restatement. See Justice as Fairness for fuller treatment.
  • Martin O'Neill and Thad Williamson (eds.), Property-Owning Democracy: Rawls and Beyond (2012). A collection of essays on the institutional content of property-owning democracy and how it differs from welfare-state capitalism. Topics include universal capital grants, predistribution, asset-based welfare, and education and inheritance policy.
  • G. A. Cohen, Rescuing Justice and Equality (2008). A critique of Rawls from the egalitarian left. Cohen argues that the difference principle, applied consistently, requires a more egalitarian distribution than Rawls's institutional framework allows.

Participatory socialism

  • Thomas Piketty, Capital and Ideology (2020). Following Capital in the Twenty-First Century (2014), which documented long-run trends in wealth concentration, this volume proposes an institutional program: progressive wealth and inheritance taxation; a universal capital endowment for young adults (Piketty proposes approximately €120,000 at age 25); worker co-determination on company boards modeled on the German Mitbestimmung system; high marginal income tax rates; and a guaranteed floor of public spending on education.

Green New Deal and eco-socialism

  • Robert Pollin, papers and books from the UMass Political Economy Research Institute, including Greening the Global Economy (2015) and a series of climate-stabilization studies for the United States, the European Union, and several developing economies. Pollin estimates the level of investment, sectoral allocation, employment effects, and financing required to reach specified emissions targets.
  • Kate Aronoff, Alyssa Battistoni, Daniel Aldana Cohen, and Thea Riofrancos, A Planet to Win: Why We Need a Green New Deal (2019). The authors argue for public power utilities, public housing retrofit, mass transit as public works, and a just transition for workers displaced from fossil-fuel industries.
  • Naomi Klein, This Changes Everything: Capitalism vs. the Climate (2014) and On Fire (2019). Klein argues that climate change should be understood as a problem of political economy rather than of consumer behavior. On Fire extends the argument into the period of Green New Deal organizing.
  • Andreas Malm, Fossil Capital (2016). A historical study of the transition from water power to steam power in early industrial Britain. Malm argues that the choice of coal was driven by the control it gave employers over the location and discipline of labor, rather than by lower energy cost.

State capacity and the mission economy

  • Mariana Mazzucato, The Entrepreneurial State (2013) and Mission Economy (2021). Mazzucato documents the role of public investment in transformative technologies, including components of the iPhone, mRNA vaccine platforms, GPS, and the internet, and proposes a framework in which the state sets and coordinates major missions rather than only correcting market failures. Elements of the framework have been adopted in European Union industrial policy and in the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act. See State Capacity and Technology for related literature.

Social democracy and its limits

  • Gøsta Esping-Andersen, The Three Worlds of Welfare Capitalism (1990). A comparative analysis of welfare-state regimes that distinguishes liberal, conservative-corporatist, and social-democratic types and identifies the institutional features (universalism, decommodification, labor-market policy) that differentiate them.
  • Wolfgang Streeck, Buying Time: The Delayed Crisis of Democratic Capitalism (2014) and How Will Capitalism End? (2016). Streeck argues that the post-war combination of democracy and capitalism has been undermined by capital mobility, financialization, and accumulated public debt, with the result that the welfare state can no longer be financed at its historical scale. See The Welfare State Under Pressure.
  • Bhaskar Sunkara, The Socialist Manifesto: The Case for Radical Politics in an Era of Extreme Inequality (2019). A statement of the contemporary U.S. democratic-socialist position written for a general audience.

Degrowth

  • Jason Hickel, Less Is More: How Degrowth Will Save the World (2020). Hickel argues that high-income economies should plan a contraction of high-throughput sectors — including advertising, fast fashion, private vehicles, and oversized housing — combined with reduced working hours and an expansion of universal public services.
  • Giorgos Kallis, Degrowth (2018). An academic statement of the position with attention to its intellectual antecedents, including Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen, Ivan Illich, and André Gorz.

Comparative and institutional frameworks

  • Erik Olin Wright, Envisioning Real Utopias (2010) and How to Be an Anticapitalist in the Twenty-First Century (2019). Wright classifies strategies for moving beyond capitalism under five headings — smashing, dismantling, taming, eroding, and escaping — and uses the framework to compare property-owning democracy, social democracy, market socialism, eco-socialism, and degrowth. The 2019 book is the shorter of the two.
  • Elinor Ostrom, Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action (1990). Ostrom documents cases in which communities have managed common-pool resources — fisheries, irrigation systems, forests, grazing lands — through institutions that are neither pure markets nor centralized state agencies, and identifies design principles common to successful cases. Ostrom received the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences in 2009.

Diagnostic background

  • Mark Fisher, Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative? (2009). Fisher argues that late capitalism has narrowed the range of imagined political alternatives and shifted moral responsibility for systemic outcomes onto individuals.
  • Naomi Klein, The Shock Doctrine: The Rise of Disaster Capitalism (2007). A history of late-twentieth-century neoliberal restructuring, focused on the role of crises — military coups, natural disasters, and financial collapses — in enabling rapid policy change.
