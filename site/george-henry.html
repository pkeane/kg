Henry George

1839 – 1897 · American
#economics#political-economy#land#inequality#taxation

American political economist and journalist whose Progress and Poverty (1879) was among the best-selling books of its century and the founding document of the movement later called Georgism. George set out to explain a paradox he had observed in California and the industrializing East: why material progress — rising productivity, railroads, growing cities — was accompanied by deepening poverty rather than its relief. His answer located the cause in land. As population and economic activity grow, the value of well-located land rises, and that increase accrues to landowners as unearned rent — a return not to any labor or capital of theirs but to the presence and effort of the surrounding community.

The argument extends David Ricardo's theory of rent into a program. George proposed to tax away the rental value of land — the "unimproved" or location value, as distinct from buildings and improvements — and, because he believed this single levy would raise enough to fund government, to abolish other taxes on labor, production, and exchange. Hence the name "single tax." The mechanism's appeal, restated by later economists, is that land cannot be hidden, moved offshore, or reduced in supply in response to the tax, so a land-value tax does not discourage production the way taxes on income or sales do. This is the feature Joseph Stiglitz and others have cited in arguing that taxing economic rents and land is more efficient than taxing labor or capital.

George built a mass political following. He ran for mayor of New York City in 1886 on a labor ticket, finishing second — ahead of the Republican candidate, Theodore Roosevelt — and died during a second mayoral campaign in 1897. His ideas shaped Progressive-era reform, influenced figures from Tolstoy to John Dewey, and survive in contemporary debates over land-value taxation and the taxation of unearned wealth alongside Piketty's capital tax and Paine's earlier claim, in Agrarian Justice, that ground rent is owed back to the commons.

Key ideas

  • Economic rent on land as unearned, community-created value
  • The land-value (or "single") tax: taxing location value, not improvements
  • Land taxation as efficient because land's supply is fixed and immobile
  • Material progress and poverty as linked rather than opposed

Key works

  • Progress and Poverty (1879)
  • Social Problems (1883)
  • Protection or Free Trade (1886)
  • The Science of Political Economy (1898, posthumous)

Secondary sources
