Contingency and Randomness in History

classical–present
#history#historiography#philosophy-of-history#contingency#randomness

How much of historical outcome is driven by deep structural forces, and how much by contingent events — individual decisions, accidents, intersections of independent causal chains — that could easily have gone another way? The deterministic camp has had the most systematic theorists: Hegel, Marx, Fernand Braudel and the longue durée, Jared Diamond's geographic determinism. The contingency camp has had fewer systematic theorists but a long tradition of skeptical critique, running from Pascal's "Cleopatra's nose" observation to Isaiah Berlin's 1954 attack on historical inevitability to Nassim Taleb's contemporary work on rare events.

Most working historians operate in between. Structural forces constrain the range of possible outcomes; particular decisions, personalities, and accidents determine which outcome within that range actually occurs. The interesting scholarship is about how to weigh the two at different scales — and about the systematic tendency of historians in hindsight to understate how open the historical moment actually was, because once an outcome has happened it begins to look like the only outcome that could have happened.

The stakes are not only methodological. If history is deeply determined, then policy choices matter less than they appear to; if history is deeply contingent, forecasts are unreliable and moral responsibility for specific decisions is sharpened. The argument connects to questions about the reliability of expert prediction, the role of leadership, and whether the social sciences can ever produce laws of the kind the natural sciences do — a question Popper answered with a flat no in The Poverty of Historicism.

Annotated bibliography

The classical reference point

  • Blaise Pascal, Pensées (published posthumously 1670) — the origin of the canonical line: "Cleopatra's nose: had it been shorter, the whole face of the world would have been changed." The image became the shorthand for everything the deterministic tradition would later deny.
  • J.B. Bury, "Cleopatra's Nose" (1916, in Selected Essays) — the Cambridge historian's short essay arguing that chance, understood as the intersection of independent causal chains, is ineliminable from historical explanation. The starting point for most twentieth-century discussion of contingency.

Philosophical foundations

  • Isaiah Berlin, Historical Inevitability (1954) — the canonical mid-century attack on determinism in its Hegelian, Marxist, and positivist forms. Berlin argued that ascribing inevitability to historical outcomes is not just bad social science but a moral evasion — it dissolves the judgments of praise and blame on which ordinary historical and political discourse depends.
  • Karl Popper, The Poverty of Historicism (1957) — the parallel case from philosophy of science. Popper argued that the search for "laws of historical development" is incoherent on its own terms: human knowledge cannot predict its own future growth, so no predictive science of history is possible. Published alongside The Open Society and Its Enemies as part of his anti-totalitarian project.
  • R.G. Collingwood, The Idea of History (1946, posthumous) — a philosopher-historian's argument that history is the reenactment of past thought, which makes it irreducible to structural generalization. Collingwood is not a contingency theorist per se but supplies the methodological foundation for treating individual decisions as the proper object of historical explanation.
  • E.H. Carr, What Is History? (1961) — the classic counterweight. Carr's lectures defended a roughly Marxist view that the historian's job is to identify significant causes and that "chance" is mostly a label for causes we haven't yet traced. Essential reading precisely because it argues well against the contingency position. The Berlin–Carr exchange, conducted at distance, sets the terms of the debate.

Counterfactual history

  • Niall Ferguson, ed., Virtual History: Alternatives and Counterfactuals (1997) — the explicit manifesto. Essays on what might have happened if Charles I had avoided civil war, if the Kaiser's 1914 plans had worked, if the Cold War had gone nuclear. Ferguson's long introduction defends counterfactual reasoning as a methodologically legitimate — indeed necessary — historical tool, on the grounds that any causal claim implicitly depends on a counterfactual.
  • Robert Fogel, Railroads and American Economic Growth (1964) — the econometric version. Fogel argued, controversially, that American economic growth in the nineteenth century would have been only modestly slower without railroads, because canal and road alternatives would have substituted. The book launched cliometrics and forced economic historians to take counterfactuals seriously.
  • Richard Evans, Altered Pasts: Counterfactuals in History (2014) — the skeptic's reply. Evans argues that most counterfactual history is either trivial or speculative to the point of uselessness, and that the genre often serves ideological purposes (Ferguson, he argues, uses it to rehabilitate the British Empire). Useful corrective.

Complexity, rare events, evolutionary contingency

  • Nassim Nicholas Taleb, Fooled by Randomness (2001) and The Black Swan (2007) — not history in the scholarly sense, but a sustained argument that human affairs are dominated by high-impact, low-probability events that are unpredictable before they happen and rationalized as inevitable afterward. Taleb's critique of narrative in retrospect ("the narrative fallacy") has been widely absorbed into contemporary thinking about history.
  • Stephen Jay Gould, Wonderful Life: The Burgess Shale and the Nature of History (1989) — the paleontologist's thought experiment: rewind the tape of life on Earth and play it again, and the same outcomes would not recur. An argument about evolutionary history that extends naturally to human history — the deepest case made in the twentieth century for treating contingency as metaphysically central rather than as a residual category.

Working historians foregrounding contingency

  • Barbara Tuchman, The Guns of August (1962) and The March of Folly (1984) — narrative history built around the thesis that rulers and generals routinely make catastrophic decisions against available evidence, that wars begin through miscalculation, and that the shape of twentieth-century history is the compounding of such decisions. Tuchman is often dismissed as popular rather than academic, but the argument — that contingency acts through individual failures of judgment at decision points — is substantive.
  • A.J.P. Taylor, The Origins of the Second World War (1961) — the celebrated and controversial claim that Hitler had no master plan, that the war emerged from a sequence of diplomatic miscalculations and opportunistic responses, and that structural theses about Nazism's war aims are overdetermined. Whether or not one accepts Taylor's specific argument, the book is the clearest case in twentieth-century historiography of a first-rank historian attacking a structural consensus with a contingentist reading.
  • Margaret MacMillan, The War That Ended Peace (2013) — a contemporary demonstration. MacMillan's account of 1914 argues that WWI was not inevitable given European structures, that a handful of decisions by a handful of men in July 1914 actually made the difference, and that the counterfactual in which the war is avoided is historically serious rather than fanciful.

Contingency within structure: the contemporary synthesis

  • John Lewis Gaddis, The Landscape of History: How Historians Map the Past (2002) — the Cold War historian's reflection on method. Gaddis argues that historians, like cartographers, work at multiple scales and that the tension between structural and contingent explanation is best handled by letting the question dictate the scale rather than by choosing sides. The most lucid short statement of the middle position.
  • Daron Acemoglu and James A. Robinson, Why Nations Fail (2012) — the "critical junctures" framework. Institutions exert long structural influence on political and economic outcomes, but institutions themselves are established at moments of contingency — wars, revolutions, conquests — where the outcome could have gone several ways. Small differences at a few historical moments compound over centuries into large national divergences. A disciplined way to hold contingency and structure together.
  • Eric Beinhocker, The Origin of Wealth (2006) — applies complexity-science tools (evolutionary dynamics, path dependence, sensitivity to initial conditions) to economic history. Contingency reframed as what complex adaptive systems do.

The structural alternatives (for context)

  • Fernand Braudel, The Mediterranean and the Mediterranean World in the Age of Philip II (1949) — the foundational statement of the longue durée. History unfolds at three levels: the nearly motionless time of geography, the medium time of social and economic structures, and the short time of events ("mere foam on the waves of history"). Contingency, in Braudel's scheme, operates only at the most superficial level.
  • Karl Marx, The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte (1852) — the most quoted single formulation of the structural/contingent tension: "Men make their own history, but they do not make it as they please." Marx's actual historical writing is more attentive to contingency than the caricature allows, but his system as received insists on structural inevitability.
  • Jared Diamond, Guns, Germs, and Steel (1997) — geographic and ecological determinism: the long arc of world history follows from the continental distribution of domesticable plants and animals and from the orientation of continental axes. Contested in its specifics, influential as a structural alternative to contingency-heavy world-historical narrative.
  • Eric Hobsbawm's four-volume "long nineteenth century / short twentieth century" series sits in this camp too — not deterministic in Braudel's strong sense, but skeptical of readings in which individual decisions do much work against the grain of economic and social forces.

The theological reading

  • Reinhold Niebuhr, The Irony of American History (1952) — adjacent to but distinct from the contingency literature. Niebuhr's history is not random; it is ironic. Human actions routinely produce consequences opposite to what their authors intended, virtues become vices at scale, and the plans of the powerful are undone by forces they could not see. The theological vocabulary differs from Taleb's but the phenomenon identified is kin: history refuses the intentions of its makers.
