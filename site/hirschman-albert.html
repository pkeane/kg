Albert O. Hirschman

1915 – 2012 · German-American
#economics#political-economy#development#political-theory#intellectual-history

German-born American economist and social theorist (Otto Albert Hirschmann, 7 April 1915, Berlin – 10 December 2012, Ewing, New Jersey). He left Germany in 1933 for Paris, fought in the Spanish Civil War, helped Varian Fry's emergency rescue committee smuggle refugees and intellectuals out of Vichy France, then served in the U.S. Army during the Second World War. After the war he worked at the Federal Reserve on European reconstruction, then spent several years as an economic adviser in Colombia, and held professorships at Yale, Columbia, and Harvard before joining the Institute for Advanced Study at Princeton in 1974, where he remained for the rest of his life.

Hirschman's work resists assimilation to a single discipline or school: it sits in the seam between development economics, political theory, and intellectual history, written in a voice that prefers small puzzles, modest claims, and patient case-by-case reasoning to grand theory. The Strategy of Economic Development (1958), drawing on his Colombia years, argued against the then-fashionable "balanced growth" prescription in favor of unbalanced sequences in which one bottleneck creates the incentive to solve the next. Journeys Toward Progress (1963) extended the method to specific Latin American reforms.

His best-known framework appears in Exit, Voice, and Loyalty: Responses to Decline in Firms, Organizations, and States (1970), which identifies three available responses to deterioration in any institution: leaving (exit), complaining (voice), or attachment that suspends the choice (loyalty). The framework has been applied across economics, political science, organizational theory, and consumer behavior. The Passions and the Interests: Political Arguments for Capitalism Before Its Triumph (1977) is a slim intellectual history showing that the seventeenth- and eighteenth-century case for commerce was originally a moral case — commercial self-interest was advanced as a discipline that would tame the destructive passions of glory, faction, and revenge — a genealogy that complicates the standard story of capitalism's self-understanding.

Shifting Involvements: Private Interest and Public Action (1982) proposed that public life moves in long cycles between private absorption and public engagement, each phase generating the disappointments that motivate the next. The Rhetoric of Reaction: Perversity, Futility, Jeopardy (1991) catalogued the three master tropes by which conservative argument resists progressive reform — the reform will produce the opposite of what it intends (perversity), it will produce nothing (futility), or it will jeopardize earlier achievements (jeopardy). The book has been repeatedly applied to subsequent debates over the welfare state, climate policy, and most recently the wealth-tax literature.

Key ideas

  • Exit, voice, and loyalty as the three responses to institutional decline
  • The seventeenth-century moral case for commerce as a tamer of the passions
  • Reactionary rhetoric reduced to three master tropes
  • Unbalanced growth and the politics of bottleneck-by-bottleneck reform
  • Cycles between private absorption and public engagement
