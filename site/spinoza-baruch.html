Baruch Spinoza

1632 – 1677 · Dutch
#philosophy#rationalism#metaphysics#ethics#political-theory#biblical-criticism#jewish-thought

Dutch philosopher of Portuguese-Jewish descent, one of the principal rationalist metaphysicians of the seventeenth century alongside Descartes and Leibniz. Born in Amsterdam into a Sephardic merchant family, Spinoza was expelled from the city's Jewish community by a writ of cherem (excommunication) in 1656, at the age of twenty-three, for opinions later developed in his philosophy. He never took a university post or married, supported himself in part by grinding optical lenses, and died at forty-four in The Hague of a lung illness likely aggravated by glass dust. He published little under his own name in his lifetime; his major work, the Ethics, appeared only after his death and was promptly banned.

Spinoza's metaphysics is a substance monism. There is, he argues, only one infinite substance, which can be called either God or Nature — Deus sive Natura — of which thought and extension (mind and matter) are two attributes, and of which all particular things are modes. There is no transcendent creator standing outside the world and no genuinely free will; everything follows from the necessity of the divine nature as conclusions follow from premises. This identification of God with nature led contemporaries to condemn him as an atheist, though his later readers have variously described the position as pantheism or panentheism. Each finite thing strives to persist in its own being — Spinoza calls this striving conatus — and human freedom, for Spinoza, consists not in escaping necessity but in understanding it: the passage from passive subjection to the emotions toward an adequate, rational grasp of one's place in nature, culminating in what he calls the intellectual love of God (amor Dei intellectualis).

The Ethics (published 1677) is written more geometrico, in the deductive form of Euclid — definitions, axioms, propositions, and demonstrations — and moves from God and substance through the mind, the emotions, human bondage, and finally human freedom. The earlier Theological-Political Treatise (1670, published anonymously) applies historical and critical method to the Hebrew Bible, treating scripture as a human document to be read in its context rather than as literal revelation, and argues that the state should guarantee freedom of thought and expression. It is a founding text of both modern biblical criticism and the liberal defense of toleration.

Condemned for roughly a century, Spinoza was rehabilitated by the German Romantics and Idealists: the Pantheismusstreit of the 1780s (Lessing, Jacobi, Mendelssohn) made him central, and Johann Wolfgang von Goethe, G. W. F. Hegel, and later Friedrich Nietzsche all drew on him. In the twentieth century a distinct Spinozist current ran through Gilles Deleuze (Expressionism in Philosophy: Spinoza, 1968) and through the Marxism of Althusser, Antonio Negri (The Savage Anomaly, 1981), and Étienne Balibar (Spinoza and Politics), who read him as a thinker of immanence, of the affects, and of democratic "constituent power" from below.

Spinoza and Berger

John Berger's late book Bento's Sketchbook (2011) takes Spinoza as its companion. "Bento" was Spinoza's given name in his Portuguese-Jewish community (Bento de Espinosa; Latin Benedictus). Berger had long been drawn to a legend that Spinoza, the lens-grinder, also drew, and that a sketchbook of his once existed; no such book survives, so Berger kept his own — interleaving his drawings and observations with passages from the Ethics and the Treatise on the Emendation of the Intellect. The pairing is not incidental to Berger's lifelong concern with looking. Spinoza's insistence on the unity of mind and nature, on the body as a means of knowledge, and on understanding as a form of love offered Berger a philosophical frame for drawing as an act of attention — a way of grasping the order of what is seen rather than merely recording its surface.

Key themes

  • Substance monism: one infinite substance, Deus sive Natura
  • Thought and extension as attributes; particular things as modes
  • Necessity and the denial of free will
  • Conatus — the striving of each thing to persevere in its being
  • Bondage and freedom of the emotions; the intellectual love of God
  • Historical-critical reading of scripture and the defense of toleration

Key works

  • Principles of Cartesian Philosophy (1663) — the only work published under his name in his lifetime
  • Theological-Political Treatise (1670)
  • Ethics, Demonstrated in Geometrical Order (posthumous, 1677)
  • Treatise on the Emendation of the Intellect (posthumous)
  • Political Treatise (unfinished, posthumous)
