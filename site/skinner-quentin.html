Quentin Skinner

1940 – ? · British
#intellectual-history#political-theory#philosophy#methodology

British intellectual historian (Quentin Robert Duthie Skinner, b. 26 November 1940, Oldham, England). He read history at Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge, and remained at Cambridge for most of his career, holding the Regius Professorship of Modern History from 1996 to 2008 before moving to Queen Mary, University of London as Barber Beaumont Professor of the Humanities. With J. G. A. Pocock and John Dunn he is identified as a founder of the "Cambridge School" of intellectual history.

The Cambridge School position was set out in Skinner's "Meaning and Understanding in the History of Ideas" (History and Theory, 1969), a methodological manifesto against both the textualist habit of reading canonical political works as timeless statements of timeless arguments and the lazier history that asked only for "anticipations" of contemporary positions. The alternative, drawn partly from J. L. Austin and the philosophy of language, was that a political text must be read as an act in its historical context — what its author was doing in writing it, against which contemporary positions, in which available vocabulary. To understand a text is to recover its illocutionary force, which requires recovering the conventions of argument the author was working within and against.

The Foundations of Modern Political Thought (two volumes, 1978) put the method to work, narrating the emergence of the modern concept of the state from late-medieval republican rhetoric through the Reformation. Liberty Before Liberalism (1998) recovered a "neo-Roman" or republican conception of liberty, distinct both from Isaiah Berlin's "negative" and "positive" categories, in which to be free is to be free from domination — from arbitrary power, even when that power is not actively interfering — a conception Skinner argued ran through Machiavelli, the English commonwealthmen, and Harrington before being eclipsed by Hobbes's redefinition of liberty as the absence of physical impediment. Reason and Rhetoric in the Philosophy of Hobbes (1996) and Hobbes and Republican Liberty (2008) developed the case at length. Visions of Politics (three volumes, 2002) collected the essays.

Skinner's recovered republican conception of liberty has been taken up by the political philosopher Philip Pettit (Republicanism, 1997) as the basis for a contemporary political theory of non-domination, and is part of the standard apparatus of contemporary debates about democracy and the rule of law.

Key ideas

  • Political texts as historically-situated acts; recovering authorial intention through context
  • The Cambridge School method in the history of political thought
  • Republican / neo-Roman liberty as freedom from domination, distinct from Berlin's negative-positive dichotomy
  • The emergence of "the state" as the modern abstract object of political theory
