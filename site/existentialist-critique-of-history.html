The Existentialist and Absurdist Critique of History

19th–20th century
#philosophy#existentialism#absurdism#philosophy-of-history#theology#historicism

Existentialism inherits the historicist premise — that nothing is timeless, that existence is always situated in a particular historical moment — and refuses its consolations. The consolations in question are the ones the 19th-century philosophy of history had built around the premise: that history is going somewhere, that it has a shape, that suffering inside the process is redeemed by the outcome the process is moving toward, that one can and should align one's action with "the direction of history." Hegel, Marx, Comte, and the progressivist tradition generally supplied versions of this consolation. The existentialist-absurdist tradition rejects them all.

The rejection takes several forms. Kierkegaard's: the single individual is irreducible to any system of world-historical development; existence is prior to system. Nietzsche's: too much historical consciousness paralyzes life and makes action impossible. Camus's: the willingness to defer moral judgment to history's future verdict is what licensed Soviet terror, and the refusal of that deferral is the rebel's defining act. Benjamin's: the progressive narrative is the story told by the victors, and the task of the historian is to interrupt it rather than extend it. The arguments are different; the common core is a refusal to let the collective historical narrative swallow the single life or dissolve its moral judgments.

A subsidiary but important thread diagnoses the modern philosophy of history as secularized theology. Karl Löwith's argument in Meaning in History (1949) is the sharpest statement: the structure of providential narrative — creation, fall, redemption, consummation — survived in Hegel and Marx after the God who gave it any warrant had been dismissed. Once this is seen, the existentialist refusal looks less eccentric: it is the refusal to keep believing the theology after the theology has been officially abandoned.

Annotated bibliography

The 19th-century ancestors

  • Søren Kierkegaard, Concluding Unscientific Postscript (1846) and Either/Or (1843) — the template for everything that follows. Kierkegaard's polemic against Hegelian system-building insists that existence is prior to and ineliminable by any philosophical narrative. The single individual's decision — in fear and trembling, without guarantee — is what Hegelian world-history erases and Christian existence preserves. The anti-historicist move is theological in Kierkegaard; it will become atheist in Nietzsche, Camus, and Benjamin, but the structure is the same.
  • Fyodor Dostoevsky, Notes from Underground (1864) — the underground man's tirade against the progressive-rationalist schema ("the Crystal Palace") is the first major literary statement of the existentialist refusal. "Twice two is four is no longer life, gentlemen, but the beginning of death." The book is directed at Chernyshevsky's utilitarian-progressive socialism, but the target is broader: any account of history in which the individual's unruly freedom is to be rationalized away by future arrangements.
  • Friedrich Nietzsche, On the Uses and Disadvantages of History for Life (1874, second Untimely Meditation) — the foundational text. Nietzsche argues that the 19th-century German cult of historical consciousness is a sickness: a person saturated in history cannot act, cannot create, cannot live. History serves life or it destroys life; the modern overvaluation of history has made action impossible and produced a culture of belated scholars rather than creators. The case against historicism as an existential problem, not just a methodological one.

The mid-century articulation

  • Albert Camus, The Rebel (1951) — the most direct absurdist attack on historicism. Camus argues that the move from legitimate rebellion to legitimated terror runs through the acceptance of "historical necessity" — the idea that present cruelty is redeemed by future justice. Soviet communism is the case in point; Jacobin terror is the earlier case. The rebel refuses the trade: moral judgment is to be made now, in one's own name, not deferred to the verdict of a history that always turns out to vindicate whoever writes it. The book broke Camus's friendship with Sartre, whose willingness to credit Marxist historicism ran the opposite direction.
  • Jean-Paul Sartre, Critique of Dialectical Reason (1960) and, earlier, Nausea (1938) — the long struggle in the other direction. Sartre spent decades trying to reconcile existentialist freedom with historical materialism, producing an enormous book that tried to ground Marxism in a phenomenology of groups-in-formation. The attempt is respected more than it is followed. But Nausea's treatment of existence itself as de trop — superfluous, unjustified, brute — is the existentialist thesis at its purest, and the tension between that and Sartre's later Marxism is the drama of his intellectual life.
  • Walter Benjamin, "Theses on the Philosophy of History" (1940) — written in the last months of his life as he fled the Nazis, before his suicide at the French-Spanish border. Short, densely imagistic, and among the most cited texts of 20th-century philosophy. Benjamin attacks historicism as "empty, homogeneous time" — the flat conveyor belt of progress, the story told by the victors — and opposes it with Jetztzeit, "now-time," the moment of messianic interruption in which the past is redeemed not by its continuation but by being seized and recognized. The ninth thesis — the Angel of History blown backward into the future, staring at the piled wreckage — is one of the great images of the century. A Marxist rejecting the progressive narrative; the theological vocabulary is doing work the Marxist vocabulary could not.

The secularized-eschatology diagnosis

  • Karl Löwith, Meaning in History: The Theological Implications of the Philosophy of History (1949) — the diagnostic book. Löwith, a German-Jewish philosopher who fled Germany and wrote in Japanese and American exile, argues that the modern philosophy of history from Voltaire through Hegel and Marx is a secularized Christian eschatology: the structure of providential narrative survives after the God who authored it has been dismissed. The implication is devastating: the "laws of history" tradition is theology in disguise, and the serious choice is between honest theology (which assumes its own warrant) and honest refusal (which does not pretend to know where history is going).
  • Hans Blumenberg, The Legitimacy of the Modern Age (1966) — the great counter-argument. Blumenberg spent a thousand pages trying to defend modernity against Löwith's charge — arguing that modern progress is not a secularized eschatology but a genuine historical achievement, an answer to questions medieval Christianity raised but could not resolve. The Löwith-Blumenberg exchange is one of the great 20th-century debates on the philosophy of history. Even readers who finish it persuaded by Blumenberg concede that Löwith's diagnostic sharpened what modernity had to answer for.
  • Leszek Kołakowski, Main Currents of Marxism (three volumes, 1976) — the great ex-Marxist's account of Marxism as, among other things, a religion: its eschatology, its theodicy, its drama of fall and redemption. Not strictly existentialist, but the closest thing to an empirical vindication of Löwith's claim — a history of the Marxist tradition written by someone who lived inside it and now sees the theological structure clearly. His earlier essay "The Priest and the Jester" is a shorter and more readable statement of the same insight.

The pessimist coda

  • E.M. Cioran, A Short History of Decay (1949) and History and Utopia (1960) — the Romanian aphorist in Parisian exile, probably the purest absurdist voice on history as such. For Cioran, history is not a movement toward anything; it is the accumulation of folly, and every utopian attempt to accelerate or redeem it has merely added to the wreckage. The prose is dense and ironic, and the refusal is more thoroughgoing than Camus's — there is no rebellion that can meaningfully oppose history, only lucidity about its character. Less a philosophy than a temperament, but the temperament has been influential.
  • Theodor Adorno, Minima Moralia (1951) and Negative Dialectics (1966) — too rigorous to be an absurdist, but the diagnosis is kin. For Adorno, the historical process after Auschwitz no longer redeems anything; "the whole is the false"; any dialectic that promises reconciliation is complicit in the suffering it claims to be heading toward. The famous line about "no poetry after Auschwitz" is a statement about the collapse of historical meaning under the weight of 20th-century catastrophe, not a literal aesthetic prohibition.

Literary embodiments

  • Franz Kafka, The Trial (1925, posthumous) and The Castle (1926, posthumous) — the bureaucratic-historicist machinery grinding on toward no comprehensible end. Kafka predates most of the figures in this bibliography, and he is not reducible to existentialism, but he supplies the controlling image the tradition keeps returning to: systems that claim rational direction, experienced from the inside as absurd.
  • Milan Kundera, The Book of Laughter and Forgetting (1979) and The Unbearable Lightness of Being (1984) — the most direct literary-absurdist treatment of historicism, drawing on the specific experience of Czechoslovakia after 1968. Kundera's concept of kitsch — the aesthetic-political denial of everything that does not fit the preferred narrative, "the absolute denial of shit" — is among the sharpest modern formulations of the anti-historicist position. History as forced forgetting, as repetition that empties meaning rather than producing it, as the betrayal of the individual to a narrative that claims to know what things mean.
