Post-neoliberal Politics in America

2008–present
#political-economy#democracy#authoritarianism#american-politics#progressive-strategy#antitrust#social-democracy#trump-era

Post-neoliberal politics in America refers to the body of contemporary writing concerned with how a more progressive political-economic settlement might be advanced through electoral coalitions in the United States. The substance of the proposals — higher taxation of capital, wealth, and inheritances; restored antitrust enforcement; public industrial policy; expanded universal social provision; sectoral wage standards — would not be classified as radical against the postwar Western European baseline. In American political discourse, however, the same proposals are routinely coded as "anti-capitalist" or "socialist." This topic groups writers concerned with the political-strategic question that gap raises, rather than with the policy content itself. The policy substance is treated in Post-neoliberal Political Economy.

Two pressures organize the recent literature. The first is the post-2016 perceived threat to liberal-democratic institutions associated with the two Trump presidencies, including the January 6, 2021 attack on the Capitol, the ongoing reshaping of the federal civil service, and the erosion of conventional limits on executive power. The second is the continuing pressure of post-1980 wealth concentration, documented across the work of Thomas Piketty, Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman, and Joseph Stiglitz. Writers in this literature differ on the relation between the two pressures: whether economic transformation is a precondition for democratic stabilization (the broadly social-democratic position), whether democratic stabilization is a precondition for any economic transformation (the broadly liberal-defensive position), or whether the two tracks must be pursued simultaneously by overlapping coalitions.

A second division concerns tempo. One position, associated with the historian Gary Gerstle, reads the post-2008, post-2016, post-2020 sequence as a "hinge moment" at which the post-1980 neoliberal order is breaking and a successor settlement is being negotiated; on this reading, programmatic ambition is warranted. A second position emphasizes the structural constraints on transformative federal politics in the United States — narrow electoral majorities, the malapportioned Senate, the conservative Supreme Court majority, the lock-in effects of financialization, the discipline of mobile capital — and argues for incremental, coalition-preserving moves. The two positions cohabit within the broad center-left and are not fully sorted out by the 2024 election results or the early returns from the second Trump term.

The 2024 mayoral primary victory of Zohran Mamdani in New York City, running explicitly as a Democratic Socialist, is one data point sometimes cited as evidence that the language of socialism has become electorally usable within American urban Democratic coalitions; how far that finding generalizes to federal or sunbelt-state politics remains contested.

Annotated bibliography

The policy substance that reads as radical in America

  • Mariana Mazzucato, The Entrepreneurial State (2013) and Mission Economy (2021). Documents the historical role of public investment in transformative technologies and proposes a framework in which the state sets and coordinates major missions rather than only correcting market failures. Elements of the framework appear in the Inflation Reduction Act and the CHIPS and Science Act.
  • Ha-Joon Chang, 23 Things They Don't Tell You About Capitalism (2010) and Bad Samaritans (2007). Argues from economic history that today's developed economies did not in fact develop under the free-trade, small-state policies subsequently exported through the WTO and IMF.
  • Branko Milanović, Capitalism, Alone (2019). Argues that there is at present no systemic alternative to capitalism on offer; the choice is between liberal-meritocratic capitalism (the Western model) and political capitalism (the Chinese model). Useful precisely because it refuses the post-capitalism framing.
  • Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman, The Triumph of Injustice: How the Rich Dodge Taxes and How to Make Them Pay (2019). The technical case for higher capital, wealth, and inheritance taxation; the policy substrate of the Warren and Sanders wealth-tax proposals.
  • Isabella Weber, How China Escaped Shock Therapy (2021) and her 2022 working-paper argument on "sellers' inflation" — that the post-pandemic price surge was substantially driven by firm pricing power in concentrated sectors rather than by excess demand. The argument shifted parts of the macroeconomic conversation; it was attacked at the time by mainstream commentators and partly absorbed since.
  • Daniela Gabor, "The Wall Street Consensus" (2021, Development and Change). Argues that the dominant model of climate finance and development finance subordinates public investment to the requirements of private asset managers, and proposes an alternative public-investment-led framework.

Selling progressive economics to the American electorate

  • Anand Giridharadas, The Persuaders: At the Front Lines of the Fight for Hearts, Minds, and Democracy (2022). Reports on organizers, candidates, and movement strategists who treat persuasion of unconverted voters — rather than internal-coalition purification — as the central task. Argues against what he describes as a left tendency toward purity-based purging.
  • Michael Sandel, The Tyranny of Merit: What's Become of the Common Good? (2020). Argues that the meritocratic framing of economic outcomes — the language in which winners deserve their position and losers theirs — is itself part of what drove non-college-educated voters away from the center-left across the developed world after 1990.
  • Heather McGhee, The Sum of Us: What Racism Costs Everyone and How We Can Prosper Together (2021). The "drained pool" argument, named for the post-civil-rights closure of municipal swimming pools rather than integration: anti-Black racism is the mechanism by which white Americans were repeatedly sold out of universal public goods. The book is closer than any other in the current literature to a manual for the multiracial economic coalition the post-neoliberal program presupposes.
  • Naomi Klein, Doppelganger: A Trip into the Mirror World (2023). Argues that the contemporary American right has absorbed the affect of anti-establishment dissent — vaccine skepticism, anti-corporate populism, anti-war language — that the left once owned, and that the left has not yet adapted.
  • Astra Taylor and Leah Hunt-Hendrix, Solidarity: The Past, Present, and Future of a World-Changing Idea (2024). A history of the concept and an argument for solidaristic politics as the form best suited to bridging the economic-democratic divide on which the post-neoliberal coalition depends.
  • Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, Let Them Eat Tweets: How the Right Rules in an Age of Extreme Inequality (2020). Argues that plutocratic parties — parties whose economic program benefits a small minority — require sustained cultural-grievance mobilization to win competitive elections. Implication: the right's authoritarian turn and its economic program are functionally connected, not separable problems.

Incrementalism, clean break, and the hinge-moment thesis

  • Gary Gerstle, The Rise and Fall of the Neoliberal Order: America and the World in the Free Market Era (2022). Argues that political orders in the United States last roughly forty years and that the New Deal order (c. 1932–1980) and the neoliberal order (c. 1980–2020) are now succeeded by a contested interregnum, in which both Trumpist economic nationalism and Biden-era industrial policy are read as evidence that the post-1980 settlement has broken.
  • Robert Kuttner, Going Big: FDR's Legacy, Biden's New Deal, and the Struggle to Save Democracy (2022). The most direct attempt to argue that the Biden administration's first two years constituted an FDR-scale break with neoliberalism rather than incremental adjustment; written before the 2024 election and now read partly as a record of what was attempted under unified Democratic control.
  • Adam Tooze, Crashed (2018), Shutdown (2021), and the ongoing Chartbook newsletter. Tooze treats the policy fights of the post-2008 period as historical political economy in real time. Useful as a corrective to both clean-break triumphalism and continuity-thesis dismissiveness.
  • Ganesh Sitaraman, The Great Democracy: How to Fix Our Politics, Unrig the Economy, and Unite America (2019). Sitaraman, a Vanderbilt law professor and former Warren staffer, argues that the constitutional structure presupposes a broadly equal middle class and that restoring such a class is therefore a constitutional rather than only a redistributive question.
  • Lina Khan, "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox" (2017, Yale Law Journal). The student note that became the founding document of the "new Brandeisian" antitrust revival. Khan subsequently chaired the Federal Trade Commission under Biden (2021–2025) and is an example of theory-to-policy translation that operates under existing statutory authority rather than requiring new legislation.
  • Zephyr Teachout, Break 'Em Up: Recovering Our Freedom from Big Ag, Big Tech, and Big Money (2020); Matt Stoller, Goliath: The 100-Year War Between Monopoly Power and Democracy (2019). The popular and historical wings of the new Brandeisian argument. The shared claim is that the concentrated economic power of the post-Reagan period is itself a democratic problem and can be addressed through antitrust without requiring the political coalition needed for transformative redistribution.
  • Tim Wu, The Curse of Bigness: Antitrust in the New Gilded Age (2018). The shortest and most quotable of the new-Brandeisian books; Wu served on the Biden National Economic Council.
  • Wendy Brown, Undoing the Demos: Neoliberalism's Stealth Revolution (2015). The bleaker reading: neoliberalism is not only an economic doctrine but a mode of subject-formation that hollows out the democratic citizen capable of choosing an alternative; the political subject required by post-neoliberal politics may have already been disassembled.
  • Wolfgang Streeck, How Will Capitalism End? Essays on a Failing System (2016) and Buying Time: The Delayed Crisis of Democratic Capitalism (2014). Frankfurt-school-inflected pessimism on whether democratic capitalism can be stabilized at all in conditions of capital mobility and financialization.
  • Mark Fisher, Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative? (2009). The diagnostic background for everything in this section: a description of the ideological condition — capitalism as the only thinkable horizon — that the writers above are, in different registers, trying to break.

The authoritarian half of the double task

  • Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt, How Democracies Die (2018) and Tyranny of the Minority: Why American Democracy Reached the Breaking Point (2023). Comparative-politics framework applied to the United States; the 2023 book argues that the counter-majoritarian features of the American constitutional order have become acutely destabilizing once one party has stopped accepting electoral defeat.
  • Anne Applebaum, Twilight of Democracy: The Seductive Lure of Authoritarianism (2020) and Autocracy, Inc.: The Dictators Who Want to Run the World (2024). The international dimension of contemporary authoritarianism; treats the American case as embedded in a transnational network of authoritarian regimes and their financial-information infrastructure.
  • Timothy Snyder, On Tyranny: Twenty Lessons from the Twentieth Century (2017) and On Freedom (2024). Snyder, a historian of twentieth-century Central Europe, argues for an active conception of freedom — freedom-to rather than freedom-from — as the basis on which democratic politics can be reconstructed.
  • John Ganz, When the Clock Broke: Con Men, Conspiracists, and How America Cracked Up in the Early 1990s (2024). A pre-history of Trumpism through Pat Buchanan, David Duke, Ross Perot, and the militia movement of the early 1990s; argues that the elements were assembled long before they cohered.
  • Robert O. Paxton, The Anatomy of Fascism (2004) and Paxton's January 2021 essay "I've Hesitated to Call Donald Trump a Fascist. Until Now." (Newsweek). Paxton is the leading American historian of European fascism; the 2021 essay is significant because Paxton had previously resisted the fascism designation for Trump and revised his position after January 6.
