I Ching (Book of Changes)

Zhou dynasty–present
#chinese-thought#divination#cosmology#classics#taoism#confucianism

The I Ching (Yijing, "Classic of Changes") is one of the oldest Chinese texts and a foundational work of the Chinese tradition. It began as a manual of divination and was later overlaid with a body of commentary that turned it into a cosmological and philosophical classic, counted among the Five Classics of the Confucian canon.

Its core is a system of sixty-four hexagrams (gua), each composed of six stacked lines (yao) that are either broken (yin) or unbroken (yang). The sixty-four arise as the combinations of the eight trigrams (bagua), themselves the eight arrangements of three such lines. Each hexagram carries a name, a "judgment," and statements attached to its individual lines. In divination a questioner generates a hexagram by a randomizing procedure — traditionally the manipulation of yarrow stalks, later the tossing of three coins — and reads the corresponding text; "changing" lines transform one hexagram into a second, so that a reading describes a situation and its tendency to alter. The text's organizing premise is that reality is constituted by ceaseless change (yi) arising from the interplay of complementary polarities, and that the patterns of the hexagrams correspond to the patterns of natural and human affairs.

Tradition ascribes the trigrams to the legendary Fu Xi and the judgments to King Wen and the Duke of Zhou (early Zhou dynasty, late second millennium BCE); the philosophical commentaries known as the "Ten Wings," once attributed to Confucius, are now dated to the Warring States and Han periods. Western engagement has been substantial: Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, corresponding with the Jesuit Joachim Bouvet, read the broken and unbroken lines as an expression of binary arithmetic (1703). Richard Wilhelm's German translation (1924), rendered into English by Cary F. Baynes (1950) with a foreword by Carl Jung, became the standard Western edition and the vehicle for Jung's notion of "synchronicity." The composer John Cage used the text to make compositional choices by chance from Music of Changes (1951) onward — the use that places it in the Tools for Thought tradition of external randomizing procedures — and it was widely adopted in the postwar counterculture.

Key ideas

  • Change (yi) as the fundamental character of reality, generated by the interplay of yin and yang
  • Sixty-four hexagrams built from eight trigrams, each a six-line binary figure with attached texts
  • Divination by chance procedure (yarrow stalks or coins), with "changing lines" yielding a second hexagram
  • Correspondence between cosmic patterns and human affairs; the "Ten Wings" commentaries as the philosophical layer
  • Western reception: Leibniz's binary reading, Wilhelm–Baynes translation, Jung's synchronicity, Cage's chance operations
