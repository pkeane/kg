Selected Recent Reading

21st century
#bibliography#political-economy#inequality#monopoly#democracy

A short, deliberately narrow shelf of books cited elsewhere in this graph. The criteria are: written in (or translated into) English, published from roughly 2015 onward, intended for a general reader rather than a specialist audience, and cited in one or more entries here. The list is selective, not representative; it tilts toward inequality, taxation, monopoly power, and the institutional conditions of democracy. For fuller bibliographies see the individual topic pages cross-referenced below.

Annotated bibliography

Inequality, taxation, and political economy

  • Matthew Desmond, Poverty, by America (2023) — Argues that American poverty is sustained by the active choices of the non-poor: the tax expenditures that subsidize the affluent, the labor-market arrangements that hold wages down, the zoning that segregates opportunity. The companion argument to his earlier Evicted (2016). See Poverty in America.
  • Mariana Mazzucato, The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy (2018) — A history of how economic thought has lost the distinction between value creation and value extraction, and an argument that the rents captured by finance, pharma, and platform monopolies have been intellectually disguised as "value." See How to Tax the Wealthy.
  • Clara Mattei, The Capital Order: How Economists Invented Austerity and Paved the Way to Fascism (University of Chicago Press, 2022) — Reconstructs the post–World War I origins of austerity doctrine in Britain and Italy as a deliberate political response to working-class power. The interwar British Treasury and the early Mussolini governments emerge as parallel cases.
  • Daniel Chandler, Free and Equal: A Manifesto for a Just Society (Allen Lane, 2023) — Translates Rawls's Theory of Justice into a concrete contemporary program: workplace democracy, capital grants at adulthood, public broadcasting, campaign-finance reform, predistribution. The most accessible recent statement of a Rawlsian politics. See Justice as Fairness.
  • Ha-Joon Chang, 23 Things They Don't Tell You About Capitalism (2010; still in print) — A brisk catalogue of mainstream economic claims that the empirical record does not support, each treated in five or six pages. Twenty-three short chapters.
  • Ha-Joon Chang, Edible Economics: A Hungry Economist Explains the World (2022) — Seventeen short essays, each anchored to a food, that re-state the same heterodox-developmentalist argument as the earlier book in a more recent and personal register.
  • Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman, The Triumph of Injustice: How the Rich Dodge Taxes and How to Make Them Pay (W. W. Norton, 2019) — The book-length argument for restoring progressive taxation in the United States, including the proposed wealth tax that became the Warren and Sanders campaign plans in 2019–2020. See Gabriel Zucman and How to Tax the Wealthy.
  • Ingrid Robeyns, Limitarianism: The Case Against Extreme Wealth (Astra House, 2024) — Argues for an upper limit on permissible private wealth, on democratic, ecological, and moral grounds. The most fully developed recent statement of the position.
  • Thomas Piketty, A Brief History of Equality (Harvard University Press, 2022) — Piketty's short and unusually optimistic synthesis: a long-run history of the reduction of inequality from the late eighteenth century to the present, with the argument that the trend, though uneven, is real.
  • Tony Atkinson, Inequality: What Can Be Done? (Harvard University Press, 2015) — Fifteen concrete policy proposals — from a higher top rate of income tax to a guaranteed minimum inheritance — assembled by the dean of twentieth-century inequality research.

Monopoly, platforms, and concentration

  • Tim Wu, The Curse of Bigness: Antitrust in the New Gilded Age (Columbia Global Reports, 2018) — A short polemic recovering the Brandeisian tradition in American antitrust and arguing that the "consumer welfare" standard introduced by Robert Bork has been a disaster for democratic politics. The foundational text of the neo-Brandeisian revival; see Lina Khan and Monopoly and Democracy.
  • Matt Stoller, Goliath: The 100-Year War Between Monopoly Power and Democracy (Simon & Schuster, 2019) — A narrative history of American anti-monopoly politics from the Progressive Era through the Chicago School to the present, written from inside the neo-Brandeisian movement.
  • Cory Doctorow and Rebecca Giblin, Chokepoint Capitalism: How Big Tech and Big Content Captured Creative Labor Markets and How We'll Win Them Back (Beacon Press, 2022) — Documents how a handful of intermediaries (Spotify, Amazon, Live Nation, the major publishers) extract rents from the creative workers whose labor they distribute, and proposes specific structural remedies.
  • Shoshana Zuboff, The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (PublicAffairs, 2019) — The long book on the political economy of behavioral-data extraction. Argues that the digital advertising business model — Google, Facebook, and their imitators — constitutes a new economic logic with implications for democratic self-government.

Democracy in crisis

  • Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt, Tyranny of the Minority: Why American Democracy Reached the Breaking Point (Crown, 2023) — The sequel to How Democracies Die (2018). Argues that the central problem of American democracy is the structural overrepresentation of an electoral minority, sustained by the Senate, the Electoral College, the filibuster, and the federal judiciary.
  • Anne Applebaum, Autocracy, Inc.: The Dictators Who Want to Run the World (Doubleday, 2024) — Documents the operational networks linking the contemporary authoritarian regimes — Russia, China, Iran, Venezuela, Belarus, Zimbabwe — through finance, propaganda, and surveillance technology.
  • Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson, Let Them Eat Tweets: How the Right Rules in an Age of Extreme Inequality (Liveright, 2020) — Analyzes what the authors call "plutocratic populism": the alliance between an economic agenda unpopular on its merits and an ethno-nationalist political mobilization that distracts from it.

History and race

  • Eric Foner, The Second Founding: How the Civil War and Reconstruction Remade the Constitution (W. W. Norton, 2019) — A short legal-political history of the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments and the long subsequent process by which they were narrowed by the Supreme Court.
  • Isabel Wilkerson, Caste: The Origins of Our Discontents (Random House, 2020) — Reads American racial hierarchy through the comparative lens of caste, drawing on the Indian Dalit tradition and on Nazi-era jurists who studied American Jim Crow law.

Economics for general readers

  • Daron Acemoglu and Simon Johnson, Power and Progress: Our Thousand-Year Struggle Over Technology and Prosperity (PublicAffairs, 2023) — A long-run history of technological change arguing that the broad sharing of productivity gains is a political achievement, not a natural feature of technical progress.
  • Adam Tooze, Shutdown: How Covid Shook the World's Economy (Viking, 2021) — A first-draft history of the global economic response to the pandemic of 2020–21: the Federal Reserve's interventions, the fiscal scale of the rescue, the divergence of recoveries across the rich and the developing world.
