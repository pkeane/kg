Gabriel Zucman

1986 – ? · French
#economics#political-economy#inequality#taxation#tax-havens

French economist (b. 30 October 1986, Paris). He took his doctorate at the Paris School of Economics in 2013 under Thomas Piketty, taught at the London School of Economics, and joined the University of California, Berkeley faculty in 2015; since 2023 he has held a chaired professorship at the Paris School of Economics. He is the founding director of the EU Tax Observatory, based in Paris, and director of the James M. and Cathleen D. Stone Center on Wealth and Income Inequality at Berkeley. He received the John Bates Clark Medal in 2023.

Zucman's central contribution has been to make hidden wealth measurable. La richesse cachée des nations (2013), translated as The Hidden Wealth of Nations: The Scourge of Tax Havens (University of Chicago Press, 2015), used asymmetries in international portfolio-investment statistics — assets recorded as liabilities in one country but not as assets in any other — to estimate that roughly eight percent of global household financial wealth is held in tax havens, much of it unrecorded. The book argues that a global financial registry and automatic cross-border information exchange are prerequisites for taxing the wealthy at all, since the base must be made visible before it can be reached. With Ludvig Wier ("Global Profit Shifting, 1975–2019," NBER / UNU-WIDER, 2022) he extended the method to corporate profits, finding that the share of multinational profits booked in tax havens rose from under two percent in the 1970s to roughly 37 percent — close to one trillion dollars — by 2019.

With Emmanuel Saez he co-authored The Triumph of Injustice (2019) and the design of the wealth-tax proposal Elizabeth Warren ran on in 2020; with Piketty and Saez he developed "distributional national accounts," which allocate the whole of national income across the population so the distribution adds up to macroeconomic totals (Piketty, Saez, and Zucman, "Distributional National Accounts," Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2018). With Annette Alstadsæter and Niels Johannesen, "Tax Evasion and Inequality" (American Economic Review, 2019) matched leaked offshore data to Scandinavian wealth records and found that tax evasion rises steeply with wealth — the top 0.01 percent evade roughly a quarter of the taxes they owe.

His 2024 Blueprint for a Coordinated Minimum Effective Taxation Standard for Ultra-High-Net-Worth Individuals, prepared for the Brazilian presidency of the G20, proposes a coordinated minimum tax on the world's roughly three thousand billionaires, set at two percent of wealth, designed so that no single jurisdiction bears the flight risk alone — extending to individuals the logic of the OECD/G20 Pillar Two corporate minimum tax. The proposal drew unusually personal opposition from Bernard Arnault, owner of LVMH and at intervals the world's wealthiest individual, who attacked Zucman by name in the French press.

The joint work with Saez and its empirical claims — most prominently the finding that the four hundred richest Americans paid a lower total tax rate than the bottom half of households in 2018 — have been contested by other economists on methodology; that dispute is documented in the joint entry Emmanuel Saez & Gabriel Zucman. The disputes concern magnitude and feasibility rather than the basic direction of the trend.

Key ideas

  • Offshore wealth and tax havens as measurable, large shares of global wealth and corporate profit
  • A global financial registry and automatic information exchange as prerequisites for taxation
  • A coordinated international minimum tax on billionaires modeled on Pillar Two
  • Tax evasion as steeply rising with wealth, so that enforcement is itself a redistributive instrument
