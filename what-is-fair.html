What Is "Fair"?

20th century–present
#justice#ethics#philosophy#political-theory#psychology#inequality

This page concerns the relation between fairness of procedures and fairness of outcomes. Modern institutions are typically certified as fair in advance: a tax code, a college admissions policy, a sentencing guideline. When the outcomes those procedures produce diverge sharply by group — when the same populations persistently end up on top or at the bottom — the divergence is commonly explained after the fact by individual merit or individual failing. The literature gathered here examines that explanatory move from four directions: the philosophy of desert, the critique of meritocracy, the moral-luck and determinism arguments against desert itself, and the social psychology and empirical data on how the move works and what it conceals.

The design tradition — how to specify fair procedures in the first place — is covered at Justice as Fairness. The two pages divide at the same point the literature does. Rawls distinguished pure procedural justice, where fairness attaches to the procedure and any outcome it produces counts as just, from cases where outcomes can be assessed independently (A Theory of Justice, §14). Nozick and Hayek hold versions of the procedural view: if the steps were just, the result is just (Nozick), or market outcomes are not the kind of thing that can be just or unjust at all (Hayek). The positions collected below press on the other side: whether anyone can be said to deserve their starting position, their talents, or even their capacity for effort — and if not, what is left of the vocabulary of merit and individual responsibility that does the legitimating work when outcomes are challenged.

A third strand runs underneath: determinism. If behavior is the product of causes the agent did not choose — genes, development, circumstance — then desert-based defenses of unequal outcomes and desert-based justifications of punishment face the same objection. That argument appears in analytic philosophy (Galen Strawson), in neuroscience (Sapolsky), and in the criminal-justice literature linked at Mass Incarceration and The Prison-Industrial Complex.

Annotated bibliography

Procedure and outcome

  • John Rawls, A Theory of Justice (1971), §§14, 17, 48 — the source texts. §14 distinguishes pure, perfect, and imperfect procedural justice. §17 makes the moral-arbitrariness argument: the distribution of natural talents is "arbitrary from a moral point of view," and the willingness to make an effort "depends in large part upon fortunate family and social circumstances for which [a person] can claim no credit." §48 separates moral desert from legitimate expectations: just institutions generate entitlements, but the institutions themselves are not designed to reward antecedent moral desert.
  • Robert Nozick, Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974) — the entitlement theory: "Whatever arises from a just situation by just steps is itself just." Justice is historical, not patterned; if acquisitions and transfers were legitimate, no further question about the fairness of the resulting distribution arises. The position the rest of this page argues against, stated at full strength.
  • F.A. Hayek, Law, Legislation and Liberty, vol. 2: The Mirage of Social Justice (1976) — Hayek argues that "social justice" is a category error: market outcomes are not intended by anyone, and only intended actions can be just or unjust. Fairness applies to the rules of the game, never to its results.
  • Ronald Dworkin, Sovereign Virtue (2000) — the distinction between option luck (outcomes of gambles deliberately taken) and brute luck (outcomes nobody chose), and an attempt to build a theory of distribution that holds people responsible for the former while compensating the latter. The founding text of what came to be called luck egalitarianism.
  • Elizabeth Anderson, "What Is the Point of Equality?" (Ethics, 1999) — the internal critique of luck egalitarianism: sorting citizens by how responsible they are for their own misfortune is itself disrespectful, and the point of equality is the abolition of hierarchy, not the policing of desert. Her Hijacked (2023), discussed at Justice as Fairness, traces how the work ethic was converted from an egalitarian critique into a discipline applied to the poor.

Merit

  • Michael Young, The Rise of the Meritocracy (1958) — the book that coined the word, as satire: a dystopia set in 2034 in which IQ-plus-effort selection produces a self-regarding elite and a demoralized underclass stripped of the old excuse of bad luck. Young objected in "Down with meritocracy" (The Guardian, June 2001) that the term he invented as a warning was being used as praise.
  • Michael Sandel, The Tyranny of Merit (2020) — meritocratic sorting teaches winners that their success is their own doing and losers that their failure is too; Sandel calls the result "meritocratic hubris" and connects the "rhetoric of rising" in both parties to the populist backlash. Argues credentialism functions as a socially acceptable prejudice.
  • Daniel Markovits, The Meritocracy Trap (2019) — a Yale Law School account of meritocracy as the mechanism by which privilege is now transmitted: elite education absorbs enormous parental investment, the resulting credentials capture elite jobs, and the cycle reproduces a hereditary class while presenting itself as open competition.
  • Adrian Wooldridge, The Aristocracy of Talent (2021) — the defense: meritocratic selection, whatever its flaws, displaced systems of inherited and purchased position and remains better than the alternatives. The opposing position within this section.

Moral luck and determinism

  • Bernard Williams, "Moral Luck" (1976; collected in Moral Luck, 1981) — coined the term. Morality presents itself as immune to luck — what you deserve depends only on what you control — yet judgments of agents in fact depend pervasively on how things turn out.
  • Thomas Nagel, "Moral Luck" (1976; collected in Mortal Questions, 1979) — the companion piece from the same symposium. Nagel sorts luck into four kinds — constitutive (what you are like), circumstantial (the situations you face), antecedent (the causes that formed you), and resultant (how your acts turn out) — and argues that as each is subtracted, the area of genuine agency "shrinks to an extensionless point."
  • Galen Strawson, "The Impossibility of Moral Responsibility" (Philosophical Studies, 1994) — the Basic Argument: to be truly responsible for your actions you would have to be responsible for the character that produced them, which would require earlier self-creation, and so on in a regress. Ultimate moral responsibility is impossible whether or not determinism is true.
  • Robert Sapolsky, Determined: A Science of Life Without Free Will (2023) — the neuroscientist's case that behavior is the product of causes running from seconds before an act back through development, genes, and culture, with no remainder for free will; on this view both meritocratic reward and retributive punishment are unjustified. Behave (2017) lays out the underlying biology.
  • Daniel Dennett and Gregg Caruso, Just Deserts: Debating Free Will (2021) — a debate volume pairing the compatibilist defense of responsibility practices (Dennett: desert-talk survives determinism because it regulates behavior) with free-will skepticism (Caruso). The live philosophical dispute in compact form.

The psychology of deservingness

  • Melvin Lerner, The Belief in a Just World: A Fundamental Delusion (1980) — the just-world hypothesis. Lerner's experiments found that observers, unable to compensate a suffering victim, instead derogate her — preserving the belief that people get what they deserve by adjusting their view of the person rather than of the world.
  • Lee Ross, "The Intuitive Psychologist and His Shortcomings" (Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, 1977) — names the fundamental attribution error: the systematic tendency to explain others' outcomes by disposition and character while underweighting situation and circumstance.
  • John Jost, A Theory of System Justification (2020) — two decades of research on the motivation to defend and rationalize existing social arrangements, found also among those the arrangements disadvantage.

What the data show

  • Raj Chetty, Nathaniel Hendren, Patrick Kline, and Emmanuel Saez, "Where Is the Land of Opportunity? The Geography of Intergenerational Mobility in the United States" (Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2014) — the Opportunity Insights baseline: a child's chance of rising from the bottom income quintile to the top varies severalfold by commuting zone. Where a child grows up is a strong predictor of adult income.
  • Raj Chetty et al., "The Fading American Dream: Trends in Absolute Income Mobility Since 1940" (Science, 2017) — the fraction of children earning more than their parents fell from roughly 90 percent for the 1940 birth cohort to roughly 50 percent for the 1980s cohorts.
  • Raj Chetty, Nathaniel Hendren, Maggie Jones, and Sonya Porter, "Race and Economic Opportunity in the United States" (Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2020) — Black-white income gaps persist even conditional on parental income, driven largely by outcomes for men; Black boys raised in top-quintile households are about as likely to fall to the bottom quintile as to remain at the top.
  • Marianne Bertrand and Sendhil Mullainathan, "Are Emily and Greg More Employable than Lakisha and Jamal?" (American Economic Review, 2004) — the résumé audit study: identical résumés sent with white-sounding names received about 50 percent more callbacks than those with Black-sounding names. A direct measurement of unfairness inside a nominally merit-based procedure.
  • U.S. Sentencing Commission, Demographic Differences in Sentencing (2017) — federal sentencing data for 2012–2016: Black male offenders received sentences on average 19.1 percent longer than similarly situated white male offenders, controlling for offense and criminal history. See Mass Incarceration.
  • Sendhil Mullainathan and Eldar Shafir, Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much (2013) — experimental evidence that poverty itself consumes cognitive bandwidth, degrading the decision-making that "individual failings" explanations treat as an independent cause. See Poverty in America.
  • Robert H. Frank, Success and Luck: Good Fortune and the Myth of Meritocracy (2016) — an economist's account of how small chance differences compound in winner-take-all markets, and survey evidence that successful people systematically underestimate luck's contribution to their own outcomes.

Responsibility and punishment

  • Barbara Fried, "Beyond Blame" (Boston Review, 2013) — a short statement of the position in this page's title note: blame-centered moral and political philosophy persists in the face of overwhelming evidence about the situational and causal determinants of behavior.
  • Erin Kelly, The Limits of Blame: Rethinking Punishment and Responsibility (2018) — argues criminal justice should be detached from judgments of moral desert; the criminal law can condemn acts and protect the public without claiming to measure the blameworthiness of persons.
  • Gregg Caruso, Rejecting Retributivism: Free Will, Punishment, and Criminal Justice (2021) — the free-will-skeptical case against desert-based punishment, with a public-health-quarantine model as the alternative: incapacitation where necessary, without the claim that offenders deserve to suffer. See The Prison-Industrial Complex.
